Stiffness and Compliance Matrices

Structural Mechanics - Force-Based Approaches and Virtual Work Methods

1. Introduction to Stiffness and Compliance Matrices

Within the context of force-based approaches, the PCVW (Principle of Complementary Virtual Work) and Menabrea's theorem can be used for evaluating:

  1. Unknown reaction forces (statically indeterminate systems)
  2. Displacements in given points for given set of loads

Consider now this second case. The PCVW for the DSV beam was found to be:

\[\int_{\ell} \left( \delta T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + \delta M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} + \delta T_x \frac{T_x}{GA_x^*} + \delta T_y \frac{T_y}{GA_y^*} + \delta M_z \frac{M_z}{GJ} \right) dz = \delta F_i \hat{u}_i + \delta M_j \hat{\theta}_j + \delta \bar{F}_r u_r + \delta \bar{M}_s \theta_s\]

Note: The components due to \(T_x\), \(T_y\), \(M_z\) have not been discussed yet, and are reported here for generality purposes.

Simple Example: Evaluating Rotation

To understand how to evaluate displacement/rotations, consider this simple example:

Which is the rotation \(\theta\)?

The internal bending moment is:

\[M_x = -Fz\]

The real generalized deformation is then:

\[\frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} = -\frac{Fz}{EJ_{xx}}\]

2. The Dummy System Approach

Consider now a properly selected "dummy" system for evaluating a suitable set of equilibrated variation of forces.

The dummy system is chosen with regard to the displacement component to be evaluated, in such a manner that the unknown, in this case \(\theta\), appears in the external work.

For the dummy system: \(\delta M_x = -1\)

Application of PCVW

The application of the PCVW is:

\[\int_0^{\ell} \frac{Fz}{EJ_{xx}} dz = \theta\]
Result: \[\boxed{\theta = \frac{F\ell^2}{2EJ_{xx}}}\]

External Virtual Work

In this example no displacements were imposed, so:

\[\delta W_e^* = \left| \delta F_i \hat{u}_i + \delta M_j \hat{\theta}_j \right| + \delta \bar{F}_r u_r + \delta \bar{M}_s \theta_s\]

The dummy system was taken by considering a variation of the applied moment \(\delta \hat{M} = 1\), while \(\delta \hat{F} = 0\).

So:

\[\delta W_e^* = \delta \hat{M} \theta = \theta\]

The quantity \(\delta M_x\) in the expression of \(\delta W_i^*\) is the equilibrated variation of bending moment due to the application of \(\delta \hat{M}\).

3. Stiffness Matrix for a Cantilever Beam

Consider a cantilever beam and denote with \(F_i\) and \(U_i\) the generalized forces and displacements, respectively.

The convention for \(F_i\) and \(U_i\) is completely arbitrary.

Compliance and Stiffness Relations

Obtaining the compliance and the stiffness matrices means obtaining a relation in the form:

\[\underline{U} = \underline{C} \, \underline{F} \quad \text{(Compliance)}\] \[\underline{F} = \underline{K} \, \underline{U} \quad \text{(Stiffness)} \quad \text{with} \quad \underline{K} = \underline{C}^{-1}\]

Remarks

  1. Linearity and Superposition: The procedure relies upon the linearity assumption of the problem. It follows that the principle of superposition holds. (The displacement \(U_i\) due to the application of \(F_A\) and \(F_B\) is \(U_i = U_A + U_B\) where \(U_A\) is the displacement due to \(F_A\) and \(U_B\) is the displacement due to \(F_B\))
  2. Approach Types: The compliance/stiffness matrix will be derived in the context of a force-based approach. The result will be the compliance matrix while the stiffness matrix will be derived indirectly, after inverting \(\underline{C}\).
    • Force-based approaches → \(\underline{C}\) (\(\underline{K} = \underline{C}^{-1}\))
    • Displacement-based approaches → \(\underline{K}\) (\(\underline{C} = \underline{K}^{-1}\))

4. Evaluation of Compliance Coefficients

The compliance matrix relates displacements to forces:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} U_1 \\ U_2 \\ U_3 \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} C_{11} & C_{12} & C_{13} \\ & C_{22} & C_{23} \\ & & C_{33} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} F_1 \\ F_2 \\ F_3 \end{Bmatrix}\]

The generic term \(C_{ij}\) denotes the i-th displacement due to the j-th unitary force.

① Coefficient \(C_{11}\) (Axial)

Real system: \(T_z = F_1\)

Dummy system: \(\delta T_z = 1\)

\[\int_0^{\ell} \frac{\delta T_z \cdot T_z}{EA} = 1 \cdot U_1\] \[F_1 \frac{\ell}{EA} = U_1\]
\[\boxed{C_{11} = \frac{\ell}{EA}}\]
② Coefficient \(C_{12}\)

Real system: \(M_x = 0\), \(T_z = 0\)

Dummy system: \(\delta M_x = 0\), \(\delta T_z = 1\)

\[\boxed{C_{12} = 0}\]
③ Coefficient \(C_{13}\)

Real system: \(M_x = -F_3\), \(T_z = 0\)

Dummy system: \(\delta M_x = 0\), \(\delta T_z = -1\)

\[\boxed{C_{13} = 0}\]
④ Coefficient \(C_{22}\) (Bending - Transverse Displacement)

Real system: \(M_x = -F_2 z\)

Dummy system: \(\delta M_x = -z\)

\[U_2 = \int_0^{\ell} \frac{F_2 z^2}{EJ} dz = \frac{F_2 \ell^3}{3EJ}\]
\[\boxed{C_{22} = \frac{\ell^3}{3EJ}}\]
⑤ Coefficient \(C_{23}\) (Coupled Bending)

Real system: \(M_x = -F_3\)

Dummy system: \(\delta M_x = -z\)

\[U_2 = \int_0^{\ell} \frac{F_3 z}{EJ} dz = \frac{F_3 \ell^2}{2EJ}\]
\[\boxed{C_{23} = \frac{\ell^2}{2EJ}}\]
⑥ Coefficient \(C_{33}\) (Rotational)

Real system: \(M_x = -F_3\)

Dummy system: \(\delta M_x = -1\)

\[U_3 = \int_0^{\ell} \frac{F_3}{EJ} dz = \frac{F_3 \ell}{EJ}\]
\[\boxed{C_{33} = \frac{\ell}{EJ}}\]

Complete Compliance Matrix

The compliance matrix is then obtained as:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} U_1 \\ U_2 \\ U_3 \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\ell}{EA} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{\ell^3}{3EJ} & \frac{\ell^2}{2EJ} \\ 0 & \frac{\ell^2}{2EJ} & \frac{\ell}{EJ} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} F_1 \\ F_2 \\ F_3 \end{Bmatrix} \quad \text{(Compliance)}\]

Stiffness Matrix

The stiffness matrix is available after inverting \(\underline{C}\):

\[\begin{Bmatrix} F_1 \\ F_2 \\ F_3 \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & \frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{4EJ}{\ell} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} U_1 \\ U_2 \\ U_3 \end{Bmatrix} \quad \text{(Stiffness)}\]

5. Extension to a Generic Beam System

The same approach can be adopted when dealing with a more complex system of beams.

The derivation of the compliance matrix represents a sort of condensation of the structure's response to a few points of interest.

After defining the forces and displacement of interest, a relation can be found in the form \(\underline{U} = \underline{C} \, \underline{F}\)

where the generic contribution \(C_{ij}\) represents the i-th displacement due to a unitary force j.

\(C_{ij}\) is obtained by considering a real system where the force \(F_j\) is applied, and a dummy system with unitary force \(F_i\)

Summary:

  • \(C_{ij}\) is obtained with the i-th dummy system
  • and the j-th real system

6. Free-Free Beam

The most general case is given by a beam which is free at the two ends.

  • From this case any set of boundary conditions can be derived as a special case.
  • The resulting stiffness matrix will be useful for the computer implementation of a simple code for analyzing relatively complex systems of beams.

The goal is to derive a relation in the form:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \underline{F}_A \\ \underline{F}_B \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \underline{K}_{AA} & \underline{K}_{AB} \\ \underline{K}_{BA} & \underline{K}_{BB} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \underline{U}_A \\ \underline{U}_B \end{Bmatrix}\]

where:

  • \(\underline{F}_A = \{F_1 \quad F_2 \quad F_3\}^T\) and \(\underline{U}_A = \{U_1 \quad U_2 \quad U_3\}^T\)
  • \(\underline{F}_B = \{F_4 \quad F_5 \quad F_6\}^T\) and \(\underline{U}_B = \{U_4 \quad U_5 \quad U_6\}^T\)

Submatrix Evaluation Strategy

① \(\underline{K}_{BB}\) is available from the previous results of the cantilever beam.

② \(\underline{K}_{AA}\) can be obtained with a similar procedure (by repeating all the steps)

Smarter Approach for \(\underline{K}_{AA}\)

A smarter approach is to consider that:

  1. The direct terms \(C_{\alpha\alpha}\) do not change (the displacement \(U_1\) due to \(F_1\) is equal to the displacement \(U_4\) due to \(F_4\), and similarly for the other terms)
  2. The mixed contributions \(C_{\alpha\beta}\) may change sign. (\(C_{23}\) and \(C_{32}\), as \(C_{13} = C_{23} = 0\))

At End A:

\(F_3\) determines a displacement \(U_2\) in the downward direction

\(F_2\) determines a rotation in the clockwise direction (negative)

At End B:

\(F_6\) determines a displacement \(U_5\) in the upward direction

\(F_5\) determines a rotation in the counterclockwise direction (positive)

Result: \(C_{23}\) and \(C_{32}\) change sign!

Thus:

\[\underline{C}_{AA} = \begin{bmatrix} C_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & C_{22} & -C_{23} \\ 0 & -C_{23} & C_{33} \end{bmatrix}\]

and so:

\[\underline{K}_{AA} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & \frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{4EJ}{\ell} \end{bmatrix}\]

③ Evaluation of \(\underline{K}_{AB}\)

Even in this case, it is possible to obtain \(\underline{K}_{AB}\) term by term. A smarter strategy is to proceed as follows.

From equilibrium:

\[F_1 = -F_4\] \[F_2 = -F_5\] \[F_3 = -F_6 - F_5 \ell\]

or \(\underline{F}_A = \underline{T} \, \underline{F}_B\) with:

\[\underline{T} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -\ell & -1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Recall now that:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \underline{F}_A \\ \underline{F}_B \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \underline{K}_{AA} & \underline{K}_{AB} \\ \underline{K}_{AB}^T & \underline{K}_{BB} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \underline{U}_A \\ \underline{U}_B \end{Bmatrix}\]

Assume the set \(\underline{U}_A = \underline{0}\), then:

\[\underline{F}_A = \underline{K}_{AB} \, \underline{U}_B\] \[\underline{F}_B = \underline{K}_{BB} \, \underline{U}_B\]

but \(\underline{F}_A = \underline{T} \, \underline{F}_B\), so:

\[\boxed{\underline{K}_{AB} = \underline{T} \, \underline{K}_{BB}}\]

It is then obtained that:

\[\underline{K}_{AB} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & -\frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{2EJ}{\ell} \end{bmatrix}\]

With a similar approach it is found \(\underline{K}_{BA} = \underline{K}_{AB}^T\)

Complete Stiffness Matrix

Summarizing, the stiffness matrix reads:

\[\underline{K} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{EA}{\ell} & & & -\frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & \frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & & -\frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{4EJ}{\ell} & & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{2EJ}{\ell} \\ -\frac{EA}{\ell} & & & \frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & -\frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & & \frac{12EJ}{\ell} & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{2EJ}{\ell} & & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{4EJ}{\ell} \end{bmatrix}\]

7. Axial and Bending Stiffness

Evaluate now the role played by the axial and the bending stiffness and the corresponding load paths.

Consider the cantilever beam here below:

The stiffness matrix was found to be:

\[\underline{K}_{BB} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{EA}{\ell} & & \\ & \frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} \\ & -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} & \frac{4EJ}{\ell} \end{bmatrix}\]

Nondimensional Analysis

For comparing the role played by the various contributions it is useful to highlight some nondimensional parameters:

  1. \(EA\) is, dimensionally, a force. So:
    • Forces can be normalized with respect to \(EA\)
    • Moments can be normalized with respect to \(EA \cdot \ell\)
  2. The displacements can be normalized with respect to \(\ell\)

Normalized quantities:

\[\bar{F} = \frac{F}{EA} \qquad \bar{M} = \frac{M}{EA\ell} \qquad \bar{u} = \frac{u}{\ell}\]

Derivation of Nondimensional Relations

Starting from the dimensional relations:

\[F_4 = \frac{EA}{\ell} U_4 \quad \rightarrow \quad \text{Multiply by } \frac{1}{EA}\] \[F_5 = \frac{12EJ}{\ell^3} U_5 - \frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} U_6 \quad \rightarrow \quad \text{Multiply by } \frac{1}{EA}\] \[F_6 = -\frac{6EJ}{\ell^2} U_5 + \frac{4EJ}{\ell} U_6 \quad \rightarrow \quad \text{Multiply by } \frac{1}{EA\ell}\]

After normalization:

\[\frac{F_4}{EA} = \frac{U_4}{\ell}\] \[\frac{F_5}{EA} = \frac{12EJ}{EA\ell^2} \frac{U_5}{\ell} - \frac{6EJ}{EA\ell^2} U_6\] \[\frac{F_6}{EA\ell} = -\frac{6EJ}{EA\ell^2} \frac{U_5}{\ell} + \frac{4EJ}{EA\ell^2}\]

Defining now the slenderness ratio:

\[\boxed{\lambda^2 = \frac{EA\ell^2}{EJ}}\]

it is possible to write the relation between nondimensional forces and nondimensional displacements:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \bar{F}_4 \\ \bar{F}_5 \\ \bar{F}_6 \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & & \\ & \frac{12}{\lambda^2} & -\frac{6}{\lambda^2} \\ & -\frac{6}{\lambda^2} & \frac{4}{\lambda^2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \bar{U}_4 \\ \bar{U}_5 \\ \bar{U}_6 \end{Bmatrix}\]

Remarks on Slenderness

  • When \(\lambda^2\) is high, the bending-related contributions tend to zero
  • \(\lambda^2\) is high when \(EA\ell^2 >> EJ\). This condition is verified whenever:
    1. \(EA\) is high
    2. \(\ell\) is high (for fixed sectional properties \(EA\) and \(EJ\), the value of \(\lambda\) increases as \(\ell\) is increased)

Conclusion: The major contributions to the load carrying capability are due to the axial behavior whenever \(\lambda^2\) is high.

This is true for beams characterized by high values of axial stiffness \(EA\) (high has to be intended with respect to \(EJ\)) and/or slender beams with high \(\ell\).

8. Structural Efficiency and Load Paths

Axial vs. Bending Efficiency

  • A structure which is designed for sustaining the loads via axial behaviour is an extremely efficient structure.
  • On the contrary, the efficiency reduces when the loads are carried via bending response.
  • Systems of trusses are highly efficient structures and for this reason they are widely employed in the space field.
  • Even the early aeronautical structures were based on the axial load carrying capabilities of the wings.

This kind of configuration (truss) was changed for aerodynamic reasons. The structure is efficient!

Ideal vs. Real Structural Behavior

If the real structure, after idealizing all the junctions as hinges, is statically determined (thus no kinematisms are possible) then the behaviour will resemble the ideal one, and the loads will be carried via axial behaviour.

In other words, if a purely axial load path is possible, then the structure will use it for sustaining loads. (It is stiffer with respect to bending).

Clearly, if the axial path is not possible due to onset of a mechanism, the response requires the contribution of bending.

Key Insight: If a purely axial load path is NOT possible due to the formation of a mechanism, the structure must rely on bending to carry the loads.

9. Contribution of Shear to the Internal Complementary Virtual Work

It was mentioned that in many cases (more specifically, for slender beams) the energy contribution associated with the shear can be neglected.

To justify this assumption consider the example here below:

Bending Contribution

\[\delta W^*_{i, bending} = \int_0^{\ell} \frac{Fz^2}{EJ} dz = \frac{F\ell^3}{3EJ}\]

Shear Contribution

\[\delta W^*_{i, shear} = \int_0^{\ell} \frac{\delta T \cdot T}{GA^*} dz = \frac{F\ell}{GA^*}\]

Ratio Analysis

The ratio between the two contributions is then:

\[\frac{\delta W^*_{i, bend}}{\delta W^*_{i, shear}} = \frac{F\ell^3}{3EJ} \cdot \frac{GA^*}{F\ell} = \frac{GA^* \ell^2}{3EJ} \propto \frac{A\ell^2}{J}\]

(\(A^*\) is of the order of \(A\); \(G\) is of the order of \(E\))

Result: \[\boxed{\frac{\delta W^*_{i, bend}}{\delta W^*_{i, shear}} \propto \lambda^2}\]

with \(\lambda\) = slenderness ratio

For slender beams the strain energy due to bending is much higher in comparison to the shear contribution.

Thin-Walled Beams

For thin-walled beams this will NOT be, in general, true.

As a matter of fact:

\[\frac{GA^*\ell^2}{3EJ} \propto \frac{A\ell^2}{J} \quad \text{iff} \quad A^* \sim A\]

This is the case of a rectangular beam, where:

\[A^*/A = 5/6 = \chi \quad \text{(shear factor)}\]

For thin walled beams \(\chi << 1\), then, in many cases, the shear deformability will NOT be negligible.
