Tensor Algebra

Preliminaries, Notation, and Fundamental Operations

Preliminaries

Vector Notation

A generic vector \(\underline{V}\) can be expressed as:

\[\underline{V} = V_1 \underline{e}_1 + V_2 \underline{e}_2 + V_3 \underline{e}_3\]

where \(\underline{e}_1\), \(\underline{e}_2\) and \(\underline{e}_3\) are the unit vectors of a Cartesian coordinate system, and \(V_1\), \(V_2\) and \(V_3\) are the components of \(\underline{V}\) along the directions 1, 2 and 3.

The following notation can be adopted:

\[\underline{V} = V_i \underline{e}_i \qquad i = 1, 2, 3\]

where the summation is implied with respect to the repeated indexes (in this case just \(i\)). In other words:

Einstein's Convention
\[\underline{V} = \sum_{i=1}^{3} V_i \underline{e}_i = V_i \underline{e}_i\]

Second-Order Tensor Notation

Similarly, a second-order tensor can be represented as:

\[\underline{\underline{A}} = A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j\]

where summation is now implied with respect to \(i\) and \(j\), or:

\[\underline{\underline{A}} = \sum_{i=1}^{3} \sum_{j=1}^{3} A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j = A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j\]

Tensor Components and Reference Systems

The term \(A_{ij}\) identifies the components of the tensor \(\underline{\underline{A}}\) for a given set of unit vectors \(\underline{e}_i\).

The tensor itself does not depend upon the choice of the reference system, but its components \(A_{ij}\) depend on the reference system.

\[\underline{\underline{A}} = A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j = \hat{A}_{ij} \hat{\underline{e}}_i \otimes \hat{\underline{e}}_j \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \hat{A}_{ij} \neq A_{ij}\]

where \(\underline{e}_i\) and \(\hat{\underline{e}}_i\) are different sets of unit vectors.

Array Representation

Often it is useful to collect the components of vectors and second-order tensors into one- and two-dimensional arrays:

\[\underline{V} = V_i \underline{e}_i \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{bmatrix} V_1 \\ V_2 \\ V_3 \end{bmatrix}\]
\[\underline{\underline{A}} = A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j \quad \Rightarrow \quad \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & A_{13} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} & A_{23} \\ A_{31} & A_{32} & A_{33} \end{bmatrix}\]

This is an intuitive way for "storing" the components of a tensor, making it possible to perform tensor operations by means of the techniques of matrix algebra.

Important: However it is worth highlighting that tensors are not arrays!

Scalar Product

Scalar Product Between Two Vectors

The scalar product between two vectors \(\underline{a}\) and \(\underline{b}\) is:

\[c = \underline{a} \cdot \underline{b}\]
\[= (a_1 \underline{e}_1 + a_2 \underline{e}_2 + a_3 \underline{e}_3) \cdot (b_1 \underline{e}_1 + b_2 \underline{e}_2 + b_3 \underline{e}_3)\]
\[= a_1 b_1 \underline{e}_1 \cdot \underline{e}_1 + a_1 b_2 \cancel{\underline{e}_1 \cdot \underline{e}_2} + a_1 b_3 \cancel{\underline{e}_1 \cdot \underline{e}_3} + a_2 b_1 \cancel{\underline{e}_2 \cdot \underline{e}_1} + a_2 b_2 \underline{e}_2 \cdot \underline{e}_2 + a_2 b_3 \cancel{\underline{e}_2 \cdot \underline{e}_3} + a_3 b_1 \cancel{\underline{e}_3 \cdot \underline{e}_1} + a_3 b_2 \cancel{\underline{e}_3 \cdot \underline{e}_2} + a_3 b_3 \underline{e}_3 \cdot \underline{e}_3\]
\[= a_1 b_1 \underline{e}_1 \cdot \underline{e}_1 + a_2 b_2 \underline{e}_2 \cdot \underline{e}_2 + a_3 b_3 \underline{e}_3 \cdot \underline{e}_3\]
\[= a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3\]

Note: Crossed-out terms indicate orthogonality of unit vectors (\(\underline{e}_i \cdot \underline{e}_j = 0\) when \(i \neq j\)).

Compact Notation

Using a compact notation:

\[c = \underline{a} \cdot \underline{b}\]
\[= a_i \underline{e}_i \cdot b_j \underline{e}_j\]
\[= a_i b_j \underline{e}_i \cdot \underline{e}_j\]
\[= a_i b_j \delta_{ij}\]
\[= a_i b_i = a_j b_j\]
Note: The indexes used for \(\underline{a}\) and \(\underline{b}\) must be different(!) to avoid any misunderstanding on the repeated indexes.
Kronecker Delta
\[\delta_{ij} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \\ 1 & \text{if } i = j \end{cases}\]

Scalar Product Between a Tensor and a Vector

Consider now the scalar product between a second order tensor and a vector:

\[\underline{\underline{A}} \cdot \underline{b} = \underline{\underline{A}} \underline{b} \qquad \text{(the dot can be explicitly reported or not)}\]
\[= A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j \cdot b_k \underline{e}_k\]
\[= A_{ij} b_k \underline{e}_i \otimes \underbrace{\underline{e}_j \cdot \underline{e}_k}_{\delta_{jk}}\]
\[= A_{ij} b_k \underline{e}_i \delta_{jk}\]
\[= A_{ij} b_k \delta_{jk} \underline{e}_i\]
\[= A_{ij} b_j \underline{e}_i = A_{ik} b_k \underline{e}_i\]

The result is then a vector \(\underline{c}\), whose component \(c_i\) is equal to \(A_{ik} b_k\).

Expanding the previous expression:

\[\underline{\underline{A}} \cdot \underline{b} = A_{ik} b_k \underline{e}_i\]
\[= A_{1k} b_k \underline{e}_1 + A_{2k} b_k \underline{e}_2 + A_{3k} b_k \underline{e}_3\]
\[= (A_{11} b_1 + A_{12} b_2 + A_{13} b_3) \underline{e}_1 + (A_{21} b_1 + A_{22} b_2 + A_{23} b_3) \underline{e}_2 + (A_{31} b_1 + A_{32} b_2 + A_{33} b_3) \underline{e}_3\]

Double Scalar Product (Contraction)

Introduction

When dealing with second- (or higher-) order tensors it is possible to introduce a "double" scalar product, which is commonly denoted with a double dot.

Scalar Product

\[\underline{a} \cdot \underline{b} = c\]

A scalar quantity is obtained from two vectors.

Contraction

\[\underline{\underline{A}} : \underline{\underline{B}} = c\]

A scalar quantity is obtained from two second-order tensors.

\[\underline{\underline{A}} : \underline{\underline{B}} = A_{ij} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j : B_{rs} \underline{e}_r \otimes \underline{e}_s\]
\[= A_{ij} B_{rs} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j : \underline{e}_r \otimes \underline{e}_s\]

It is important to establish a convention to define how the double scalar product is taken (this choice is not unique, and different conventions are found in the literature).

Convention for Double Dot Product

The convention adopted here considers the scalar products according to the graphical representation above, meaning that:

\[= A_{ij} B_{rs} (\underline{e}_i \cdot \underline{e}_r)(\underline{e}_j \cdot \underline{e}_s)\]
\[= A_{ij} B_{rs} \delta_{ir} \delta_{js}\]
\[= A_{ij} B_{ij} = A_{rs} B_{rs}\]

Expanded Form

The expanded form is then:

\[\underline{\underline{A}} : \underline{\underline{B}} = A_{ij} B_{ij}\]
\[= A_{1j} B_{1j} + A_{2j} B_{2j} + A_{3j} B_{3j}\]
\[= A_{11} B_{11} + A_{12} B_{12} + A_{13} B_{13} + A_{21} B_{21} + A_{22} B_{22} + A_{23} B_{23} + A_{31} B_{31} + A_{32} B_{32} + A_{33} B_{33}\]
\[\underline{\underline{A}} : \underline{\underline{B}} = \sum_{i=1}^{3} \sum_{j=1}^{3} A_{ij} B_{ij}\]

Application: Strain Energy Density

Strain Energy Density Formula

One example of application of the contraction operation is the evaluation of the strain energy density:

\[u = \frac{1}{2} \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\]

where \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}}\) and \(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\) are proper measures of stresses and strains (which are second order tensors).

Symmetric Tensors

One can note that if \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}}\) and \(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\) are symmetric then the contraction reads:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \sigma_{11} \varepsilon_{11} + \sigma_{12} \varepsilon_{12} + \sigma_{13} \varepsilon_{13} + \sigma_{12} \varepsilon_{12} + \sigma_{22} \varepsilon_{22} + \sigma_{23} \varepsilon_{23} + \sigma_{13} \varepsilon_{13} + \sigma_{23} \varepsilon_{23} + \sigma_{33} \varepsilon_{33}\]
\[= \sigma_{11} \varepsilon_{11} + \sigma_{22} \varepsilon_{22} + \sigma_{33} \varepsilon_{33} + \sigma_{12} (2\varepsilon_{12}) + \sigma_{13} (2\varepsilon_{13}) + \sigma_{23} (2\varepsilon_{23})\]

For this reason, stress and strain components can be collected into one-dimensional arrays as:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \sim \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} \\ \sigma_{22} \\ \sigma_{33} \\ \sigma_{23} \\ \sigma_{13} \\ \sigma_{12} \end{bmatrix}\]
\[\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \sim \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{11} \\ \varepsilon_{22} \\ \varepsilon_{33} \\ 2\varepsilon_{23} \\ 2\varepsilon_{13} \\ 2\varepsilon_{12} \end{bmatrix}\]

Note: The factor of 2 appears on the shear strain components in the array representation.

Voigt Notation

And the operation of contraction is then represented as:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \{ \varepsilon_{11} \quad \varepsilon_{22} \quad \varepsilon_{33} \quad 2\varepsilon_{23} \quad 2\varepsilon_{13} \quad 2\varepsilon_{12} \} \begin{Bmatrix} \sigma_{11} \\ \sigma_{22} \\ \sigma_{33} \\ \sigma_{23} \\ \sigma_{13} \\ \sigma_{12} \end{Bmatrix}\]
\[= \underline{\varepsilon}^T \underline{\sigma} = \underline{\sigma}^T \underline{\varepsilon}\]

This notation, generally denoted as Voigt-notation, is based on the representation of second-order tensor components by means of one-dimensional arrays. While it is useful for performing several algebraic manipulations, it is important to bear in mind that \(\underline{\sigma}\) and \(\underline{\varepsilon}\) are not vectors.

Important: For instance, the rotation of \(\underline{\sigma}\) and \(\underline{\varepsilon}\) follows the rules of the rotation of a second-order tensor, not those of a vector!

Gradient and Divergence

Definitions

The gradient and divergence operators are defined as:

Gradient
\[\nabla(\cdot) = \text{grad } (\cdot) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} (\cdot) \underline{e}_i\]
Divergence
\[\nabla \cdot (\cdot) = \text{div } (\cdot) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} (\cdot) \cdot \underline{e}_i\]

Gradient of a Vector

According to the above definitions, the gradient of a vector \(\underline{a}\) is:

\[\text{grad } \underline{a} = \frac{\partial \underline{a}}{\partial x_i} \underline{e}_i\]
\[= \frac{\partial (a_j \underline{e}_j)}{\partial x_i} \underline{e}_i\]
\[= \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x_i} \underline{e}_j \otimes \underline{e}_i\]
\[= a_{j,i} \underline{e}_j \otimes \underline{e}_i = a_{i,j} \underline{e}_i \otimes \underline{e}_j\]
The gradient of a vector produces a second-order tensor.

Note: Comma notation is used for partial derivatives: \(a_{j,i} = \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x_i}\)

Divergence of a Vector

Similarly, the divergence is:

\[\text{div } \underline{a} = \frac{\partial \underline{a}}{\partial x_i} \cdot \underline{e}_i\]
\[= \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x_i} \underline{e}_j \cdot \underline{e}_i\]
\[= \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x_i} \delta_{ij} = a_{i,i}\]
The divergence of a vector produces a scalar.

Divergence of a Second-Order Tensor

The divergence of a second-order tensor is:

\[\text{div } \underline{\underline{A}} = \frac{\partial A_{rs}}{\partial x_i} \underline{e}_r \otimes \underbrace{\underline{e}_s \cdot \underline{e}_i}_{\delta_{si}}\]
\[= A_{rs,i} \delta_{si} \underline{e}_r\]
\[= A_{ri,i} \underline{e}_r = A_{ir,r} \underline{e}_i\]
The divergence of a second-order tensor produces a vector.

Note: Comma notation: \(A_{rs,i} = \frac{\partial A_{rs}}{\partial x_i}\). The final result shows index contraction on the derivative index.
