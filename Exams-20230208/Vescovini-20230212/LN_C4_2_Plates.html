Part I: Mindlin Plate Theory

First-Order Shear Deformation Theory

1. Introduction and Plate Idealization

A plate idealization can be applied to those 3D solids characterized by one of the three dimensions which is much smaller in comparison to the other two. The smallest dimension is commonly denoted as thickness.

It is worth noting, since the beginning, that the Mindlin plate model (as well as any other plate model) represents a simplified description of a structure that in reality is, in any case, inherently 3D.

Definition: A plate identifies a structure characterized by a flat middle surface, whereas shells are those structures characterized by a curved middle surface.

Coordinate System

Consider now a relatively thin panel, where \(t \ll a\) (say in the range \(a/t > 20\)), characterized by thickness \(t\), and planar dimensions \(a\) and \(b\).

The midsurface is taken as the surface passing through the middle of the thickness (although it is not strictly necessary; any through-the-thickness position could be considered).

A Cartesian coordinate system is taken over the midsurface with \(xy\) defining the plane of the midsurface and \(z\) the thickness-wise direction.

Kinematic Formulation Approach

The plate model is developed in the context of a kinematic formulation, in analogy to the approach adopted in the case of beams. This means that the underlying idea consists in describing the displacement field of the 3D structure (recall, the plate is characterized by \(t \ll a\) but is, in any case, 3D) starting from the knowledge of generalized displacement quantities referred to the reference surface of the plate.

2. Kinematic Model

The Mindlin plate model, which is the 2D counterpart of the Timoshenko beam model, relies upon the assumption that sections initially normal to the reference surface can rotate during the deformation process, but are constrained to remain straight.

Kinematic Assumptions:

  1. The membrane part of the displacement is characterized by an \(x\)-wise displacement equal for all the points belonging to the cross section
  2. The bending part is given by a rotation \(\phi_x\), due to a rotation of the section

The same kinematic description is adopted with regard to the \(zy\) plane.

Kinematic Field Formulation

The kinematic model is formulated as:

\[u(x,y,z) = U_o(x,y) + z\phi_x(x,y)\] \[v(x,y,z) = V_o(x,y) + z\phi_y(x,y)\] \[w(x,y,z) = W_o(x,y)\]

where \(U_o\), \(V_o\), \(W_o\), \(\phi_x\), \(\phi_y\) are the generalized displacement components of the Mindlin plate model.

The kinematic description should be interpreted as a "rule" for describing the 3D displacement field of the plate (\(u\), \(v\) and \(w\) are functions of \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\)), starting from the generalized components which are referred to the plate reference surface (\(U_o\), \(V_o\), \(W_o\), \(\phi_x\) and \(\phi_y\) depend on \(x\) and \(y\) only).

Relation to Rotations

The generalized displacement components \(\phi_x\) and \(\phi_y\) are related to the rotations around the \(x\) and \(y\) axes (taken positive according to the right hand rule) by:

\[\phi_x = \theta_y\] \[\phi_y = -\theta_x\]

where \(\theta_x\), \(\theta_y\) are rotations around the \(x\) and \(y\) axes

Applicability of the Straight Section Assumption

Note that the assumption of sections remaining straight is, indeed, an assumption. No guarantees exist that the thickness-wise behaviour will be characterized by such behaviour. In many cases of practical interest this is a reasonable approximation leading to sufficiently accurate results.

Attention should be paid when the assumption is applied to plates characterized by:

  1. Abrupt variations of mechanical properties along the thickness direction (e.g., sandwich panels)
  2. Relatively thick panels (\(a/t \leq 20\)) especially when the transverse shear stiffnesses are relatively weak (e.g., composite panels)

Indicial Notation

Given the amount of not null contributions and the number of contributions characterizing the expression of the virtual work, it is convenient to adopt a compact notation. According to the indicial notation, the kinematic field can be expressed as:

\[U_\alpha(X_\alpha, X_3) = U_{o_\alpha}(X_\alpha) + X_3 \phi_\alpha(X_\alpha) \quad \alpha = 1, 2\] \[W(X_\alpha, X_3) = W_o(X_\alpha) \quad X_3 \equiv z\]

3. Generalized Deformations

By substituting the kinematic field into the expression of the infinitesimal displacement strain tensor:

\[\varepsilon_{xx} = U_{,x} = U_{o,x} + z\phi_{x,x}\] \[\varepsilon_{yy} = V_{,y} = V_{o,y} + z\phi_{y,y}\] \[\gamma_{xy} = V_{,x} + U_{,y} = V_{o,x} + U_{o,y} + z(\phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x})\] \[\gamma_{xz} = W_{,x} + U_{,z} = W_{o,x} + \phi_x\] \[\gamma_{yz} = W_{,y} + V_{,z} = W_{o,y} + \phi_y\] \[\varepsilon_{zz} = W_{,z} = 0\]

By separating the dependence on \((x,y)\) from the dependence on \(z\):

\[\begin{pmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} \\ \varepsilon_{yy} \\ \gamma_{xy} \\ \gamma_{xz} \\ \gamma_{yz} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} U_{o,x} \\ V_{o,y} \\ V_{o,x} + U_{o,y} \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + z \begin{pmatrix} \phi_{x,x} \\ \phi_{y,y} \\ \phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x} \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ W_{o,x} + \phi_x \\ W_{o,y} + \phi_y \end{pmatrix}\] \[= \underline{\hat{\varepsilon}} + z\underline{K} + \underline{t}\]

Where:

  • \(\underline{\hat{\varepsilon}}\): generalized membrane deformations
  • \(\underline{K}\): generalized bending deformations (curvatures)
  • \(\underline{t}\): generalized transverse shear deformations

Deformations in Indicial Notation

\[\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta} = \hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} + X_3 K_{\alpha\beta}\] \[\gamma_{\alpha 3} = W_{,\alpha} + \phi_\alpha\]

Where the membrane deformations \(\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta}\) and the curvatures \(K_{\alpha\beta}\) are:

\[\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} = \frac{1}{2}\left(U_{o_{\alpha,\beta}} + U_{o_{\beta,\alpha}}\right)\] \[K_{\alpha\beta} = \phi_{\alpha,\beta}\]

4. Generalized Stresses

The generalized stresses are obtained by application of the Principle of Virtual Work (PVW):

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta\underline{\varepsilon} : \underline{\sigma} \, dV = \int_V \left( \delta\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta} \, \sigma_{\alpha\beta} + \delta\gamma_{\alpha 3} \, \sigma_{\alpha 3} \right) dV\]

Substituting the expressions of \(\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\) and \(\gamma_{\alpha 3}\) as obtained from the kinematic model:

\[= \int_V \left[ \left( \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} + z \, \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \right) \sigma_{\alpha\beta} + \delta\gamma_{\alpha 3} \, \sigma_{\alpha 3} \right] dV\] \[= \int_A \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} \int_t \sigma_{\alpha\beta} \, dx_3 \, dA + \int_A \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \int_t z \, \sigma_{\alpha\beta} \, dx_3 \, dA + \int_A \delta\gamma_{\alpha 3} \int_t \sigma_{\alpha 3} \, dx_3 \, dA\]

Definition of Generalized Stress Resultants

Membrane forces per unit length:

\[N_{\alpha\beta} = \int_t \sigma_{\alpha\beta} \, dx_3\]

Moments per unit length:

\[M_{\alpha\beta} = \int_t z \, \sigma_{\alpha\beta} \, dx_3\]

Transverse shear forces per unit length:

\[Q_\alpha = \int_t \sigma_{\alpha 3} \, dx_3\]

Internal Virtual Work

\[\delta W_i = \int_A \left( \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} \, N_{\alpha\beta} + \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} + \delta\gamma_{\alpha 3} \, Q_\alpha \right) dA\] \[= \int_A \left( \delta\underline{\hat{\varepsilon}} : \underline{N} + \delta\underline{K} : \underline{M} + \delta\underline{\gamma} \cdot \underline{Q} \right) dA\]

This expression illustrates the work conjugacy between:

  • Stress fluxes \(\underline{N}\) and membrane deformations \(\underline{\hat{\varepsilon}}\)
  • Moment fluxes \(\underline{M}\) and curvatures \(\underline{K}\)
  • Transverse shear fluxes \(\underline{Q}\) and transverse shear deformations \(\underline{\gamma}\)

Note: The moments are not defined according to the right-hand rule. For example, \(M_{xx}\) is not the moment around the \(x\)-axis. Rather, the moments are defined as:

\[M_{\alpha\beta} = \int_t \sigma_{\alpha\beta} \, X_3 \, dt\]

So \(M_{xx}\) is the moment due to the \(\sigma_{xx}\) components. Also, \(M_{xy}\) is the torsional moment due to the thickness-wise distribution of in-plane shear stresses \(\sigma_{xy}\).

5. Generalized External Forces

Similarly to the case of the Timoshenko beam model, the Mindlin plate allows for the description of transverse shear deformations. The external loads consistent with this kinematic model are then in the form of:

  • Distributed axial forces \(\hat{n}_x\), \(\hat{n}_y\)
  • Transverse shear load \(\hat{n}_z = p\)
  • Distributed couples \(\hat{m}_x\) and \(\hat{m}_y\) (thanks to the ability of the model to account for shear deformability)

External Virtual Work:

\[\delta W_e = \int_A \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{n}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, p + \delta\phi_\alpha \, \hat{m}_\alpha \right) dA \quad \alpha = 1, 2\]

Where the generalized external forces are defined as:

\[\hat{n}_\alpha = \int_t F_\alpha \, dx_3\] \[p = \int_t F_3 \, dx_3\] \[\hat{m}_\alpha = \int_t F_\alpha \, X_3 \, dx_3\]

and \(\underline{F}\) is the volume force vector.

6. Equilibrium Conditions

The equilibrium conditions are derived by applying the PVW. The plate is characterized by the boundary \(\Gamma\) which is partially subjected to imposed displacements (\(\Gamma_U\)) and to applied generalized forces (\(\Gamma_F\)):

\[\Gamma = \Gamma_F \cup \Gamma_U \quad \text{and} \quad \Gamma_F \cap \Gamma_U = \emptyset\]

PVW Statement

\[\int_A \left( \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} \, N_{\alpha\beta} + \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} + \delta\gamma_{\alpha 3} \, Q_\alpha \right) dA =\] \[= \int_A \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{n}_\alpha + \delta\phi_\alpha \, \hat{m}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, p \right) dA + \int_{\Gamma_F} \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{N}_\alpha + \delta\phi_\alpha \, \hat{M}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, \hat{Q} \right) d\Gamma_F\]

Derivation of Equilibrium Equations

The equilibrium equations are obtained by expressing the virtual variations in terms of displacement components and integrating by parts. After collecting virtual variations and applying the PVW condition \(\delta W_i = \delta W_e\):

Equilibrium Equations in \(\Omega\)
\[N_{\alpha\beta,\beta} + \hat{n}_\alpha = 0\]

(membrane equilibrium, 2 eqs)

\[M_{\alpha\beta,\beta} - Q_\alpha + \hat{m}_\alpha = 0\]

(moment equilibrium, 2 eqs)

\[Q_{\alpha,\alpha} + p = 0\]

(shear equilibrium, 1 eq)

Boundary Conditions

Natural (in \(\Gamma_F\)):

\[N_{\alpha\beta} \, n_\beta = \hat{N}_\alpha\] \[M_{\alpha\beta} \, n_\beta = \hat{M}_\alpha\] \[Q_\alpha \, n_\alpha = \hat{Q}\]

Essential (in \(\Gamma_U\)):

\[\delta U_\alpha = 0, \quad \delta\phi_\alpha = 0, \quad \delta W_o = 0\]

Key Points:

  • This is the strong-form formulation of the problem for the Mindlin plate model
  • The set of equations and boundary conditions is obtained automatically by substituting the kinematic assumptions into the PVW
  • The problem is well-posed and a solution can be obtained
  • The set of equations is given by 5 PDEs in the 5 unknowns \(U_{o_\alpha}\), \(W_o\), \(\phi_\alpha\)

Alternative Derivation via Differential Element

The equilibrium equations could also have been obtained by imposing equilibrium on an infinitesimal plate element.

For example, equilibrium along \(x\):

\[(N_{xx} + dN_{xx} - N_{xx}) \, dy + (N_{xy} + dN_{xy} - N_{xy}) \, dx + \hat{n}_x \, dx \, dy = 0\]

Dividing by \(dx \, dy\):

\[N_{xx,x} + N_{xy,y} + \hat{n}_x = 0\]

Similarly, equilibrium along \(y\):

\[N_{yy,y} + N_{xy,x} + \hat{n}_y = 0\]

The advantage of the PVW approach is that it provides an automatic answer, furnishing both the equilibrium conditions and the boundary conditions consistent with the kinematic model assumed.

7. Rectangular Plates

The set of governing equations previously derived can be specialized to the case of rectangular plates, which represent one of the most common cases within the typical applications of plate structures.

The normal vectors to the 4 edges are:

  • Top edge: \(\underline{n} = \underline{e}_x\)
  • Bottom edge: \(\underline{n} = -\underline{e}_x\)
  • Right edge: \(\underline{n} = -\underline{e}_y\)
  • Left edge: \(\underline{n} = \underline{e}_y\)

Boundary Conditions in Expanded Form

Membrane part:

\[N_{xx} n_x + N_{xy} n_y = \hat{N}_x \quad \text{or} \quad \delta U_x = 0\] \[N_{xy} n_x + N_{yy} n_y = \hat{N}_y \quad \text{or} \quad \delta U_y = 0\]

Bending and transverse shear part:

\[M_{xx} n_x + M_{xy} n_y = \hat{M}_x \quad \text{or} \quad \delta\phi_x = 0\] \[M_{xy} n_x + M_{yy} n_y = \hat{M}_y \quad \text{or} \quad \delta\phi_y = 0\] \[Q_x n_x + Q_y n_y = \hat{Q} \quad \text{or} \quad \delta W_o = 0\]

Common Boundary Condition Types

The boundary conditions express the equilibrium between the internal (generalized) forces and the applied loads. In any portion of the boundary, either a natural condition or an essential condition has to be specified.

The common sets of boundary conditions (restricting focus to the bending part) are:

Type Description Conditions
Free edge No kinematic condition specified 3 natural conditions
Simply-supported Transverse displacement and rotation around normal prevented; rotation around tangent free 2 essential + 1 natural
Clamped All displacement components prevented 3 essential conditions

8. Plate Constitutive Law

In order to express the equations in terms of the generalized displacement components, it is necessary to introduce the plate constitutive law. This is the relation establishing a link between the generalized stresses and the generalized deformations of the kinematic model.

Homogeneous Isotropic Material

Consider the case of homogeneous isotropic material. Under the assumption of state of plane stress (viz. \(\sigma_{zz} = 0\)):

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} \\ \sigma_{yy} \\ \sigma_{xy} \end{Bmatrix} = \frac{E}{1-\nu^2} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \nu & 0 \\ \nu & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1-\nu}{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} \\ \varepsilon_{yy} \\ \gamma_{xy} \end{Bmatrix}\]

The transverse shear stress components are available from the 3D constitutive law:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \sigma_{yz} \\ \sigma_{xz} \end{Bmatrix} = \chi \begin{bmatrix} G & 0 \\ 0 & G \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \gamma_{yz} \\ \gamma_{xz} \end{Bmatrix}\]

where \(\chi\) is the shear factor, taken equal to \(\chi = 5/6\), which is introduced to reduce the intrinsic excess of stiffness of the model (exactly as done for the Timoshenko beam).

Membrane Forces

Recalling the expression of the generalized stresses and performing the integration:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} N_{xx} \\ N_{yy} \\ N_{xy} \end{Bmatrix} = \frac{Et}{1-\nu^2} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \nu & 0 \\ \nu & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1-\nu}{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \hat{\varepsilon}_{xx} \\ \hat{\varepsilon}_{yy} \\ 2\hat{\varepsilon}_{xy} \end{Bmatrix}\]

Bending Moments

Defining the flexural stiffness:

\[D = \frac{Et^3}{12(1-\nu^2)}\]
\[\begin{Bmatrix} M_{xx} \\ M_{yy} \\ M_{xy} \end{Bmatrix} = D \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \nu & 0 \\ \nu & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1-\nu}{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \phi_{x,x} \\ \phi_{y,y} \\ \phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x} \end{Bmatrix}\]

Transverse Shear Forces

\[\begin{Bmatrix} Q_y \\ Q_x \end{Bmatrix} = \chi \begin{bmatrix} Gt & 0 \\ 0 & Gt \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \phi_y + W_{o,y} \\ \phi_x + W_{o,x} \end{Bmatrix}\]

Summary: Constitutive Relations

General Form

\[\begin{pmatrix} N_{xx} \\ N_{yy} \\ N_{xy} \\ M_{xx} \\ M_{yy} \\ M_{xy} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ A_{12} & A_{22} & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & A_{66} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & D_{11} & D_{12} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & D_{12} & D_{22} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & D_{66} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} U_{o,x} \\ V_{o,y} \\ U_{o,y} + V_{o,x} \\ \phi_{x,x} \\ \phi_{y,y} \\ \phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x} \end{Bmatrix}\] \[\begin{Bmatrix} Q_y \\ Q_x \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A_{44}^* & 0 \\ 0 & A_{55}^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \phi_y + W_{o,y} \\ \phi_x + W_{o,x} \end{Bmatrix}\]

(The notation \(A_{ii}^*\) reminds that a shear factor has been introduced)

The relations reported above hold for an isotropic plate. However, the expression of the constitutive law has the same pattern also for orthotropic plates.

The case of generically layered composite plates can be considered by adopting a similar approach. In this case the only difference would be that, in general, the matrices expressing the constitutive law would be fully populated.

9. Equilibrium Equations in Terms of Displacements

Having introduced the constitutive relation of the plate, it is possible to express the equilibrium equations in terms of displacement components.

Extended Form

Equation (1) - Membrane equilibrium in x:

\[\left[ A_{11} U_{o,x} + A_{12} V_{o,y} \right]_{,x} + \left[ A_{66} (U_{o,y} + V_{o,x}) \right]_{,y} + \hat{n}_x = 0\]

Equation (2) - Membrane equilibrium in y:

\[\left[ A_{12} U_{o,x} + A_{22} V_{o,y} \right]_{,y} + \left[ A_{66} (U_{o,y} + V_{o,x}) \right]_{,x} + \hat{n}_y = 0\]

Equation (3) - Moment equilibrium about y:

\[\left[ D_{11} \phi_{x,x} + D_{12} \phi_{y,y} \right]_{,x} + \left[ D_{66} (\phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x}) \right]_{,y} - A_{55}^* (\phi_x + W_{o,x}) + \hat{m}_x = 0\]

Equation (4) - Moment equilibrium about x:

\[\left[ D_{12} \phi_{x,x} + D_{22} \phi_{y,y} \right]_{,y} + \left[ D_{66} (\phi_{x,y} + \phi_{y,x}) \right]_{,x} - A_{44}^* (\phi_y + W_{o,y}) + \hat{m}_y = 0\]

Equation (5) - Transverse equilibrium:

\[\left[ A_{55}^* (\phi_x + W_{o,x}) \right]_{,x} + \left[ A_{44}^* (\phi_y + W_{o,y}) \right]_{,y} + p = 0\]

Part II: Kirchhoff Plate Theory

Classical Plate Theory (Thin Plate Theory)

10. Introduction to Kirchhoff Theory

The plate model due to Kirchhoff can be seen as the 2D counterpart of the Euler-Bernoulli model. Indeed, the Kirchhoff plate model is based upon a kinematic field which leads to null transverse shear strains.

This means that Kirchhoff model is a suitable plate model for those cases where the energy contribution due to the transverse shear can be reasonably neglected.

Applicability: This is the case of thin isotropic plates and thin composite plates. The range of applicability for isotropic plates is generally wider in comparison to the case of composite materials.

Indeed, the transverse shearing stiffness of typical aerospace composite materials (\(G_{23}\) and \(G_{13}\) shear moduli) is much lower in comparison to the isotropic ones, and the role played by shear deformability is then higher for some values of relative thickness \(a/t\).

Guideline:

  • A geometrical ratio \(a/t = 100\) is certainly a situation that can be analyzed referring to the Kirchhoff model, independently on the material type
  • When the plate gets thicker, say \(a/t = 50\), it could be worth checking the appropriateness of the Kirchhoff model, also in relation to the material

11. Kinematic Model

Consider a plate of dimensions \(a\), \(b\), thickness \(t\) and a Cartesian reference system where \(X_3 \equiv z\) is the thickness-wise coordinate, while the reference surface is identified by \(x_1 - x_2\).

Kirchhoff Assumption: Sections normal to the plate midsurface remain normal after the deformation process.

This means that, in addition to the assumption introduced by Mindlin of section remaining straight, it is now assumed that they remain normal.

Displacement Field

According to the above mentioned assumption, the displacement field is expressed as:

\[U_\alpha (X_\alpha, X_3) = U_{o_\alpha} - X_3 W_{o,\alpha} \quad \alpha = 1, 2\] \[W_o (X_\alpha, X_3) = W_o\]

Where \(U_{o_\alpha}\), \(W_{o,\alpha}\) and \(W_o\) are the generalized displacement components of the Kirchhoff plate model.

Key observation: In the context of the Kirchhoff model, the rotation coincides with the first derivatives of the out-of-plane displacement field.

Strains

\[\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta} = \hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} + X_3 K_{\alpha\beta} \quad \alpha, \beta = 1, 2\] \[\gamma_{\alpha 3} = 0\]

Where the membrane strains and curvatures are:

\[\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} = \frac{1}{2} \left( U_{o_{\alpha,\beta}} + U_{o_{\beta,\alpha}} \right)\] \[K_{\alpha\beta} = -W_{o,\alpha\beta}\]

Differences with Mindlin Model

  1. Curvatures \(K_{\alpha\beta}\) are associated with the second derivative of the out-of-plane displacement (on the contrary, the membrane deformations are still equal)
  2. Transverse shear deformations \(\gamma_{\alpha 3}\) are null. The Kirchhoff plate model is thus uncapable of accounting for transverse shear deformability

In other words, the Kirchhoff model relies upon the assumption that the strain energy contribution due to the transverse shear deformability is negligible with respect to the bending one. This is in analogy with the assumption adopted in the case of slender beams.

Note on \(\varepsilon_{zz}\): In analogy with Mindlin, the normal deformation \(\varepsilon_{33} = \varepsilon_{zz}\) is equal to zero. This represents a static inconsistency of the model, as the result \(\varepsilon_{zz} = 0\) is in contrast with the assumption of plane stress condition. However, the effect of the inconsistency is energetically not relevant as the internal work \(\sigma_{zz} \varepsilon_{zz}\) is null.

12. Generalized Stresses and Internal Work

The expression of the internal work is formally equal to the one obtained for the Mindlin model. Indeed, the expression of \(\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\) is equal and the difference is nested in the expression of the curvatures \(K_{\alpha\beta}\).

Given the fact that \(\gamma_{\alpha 3} = 0\), the internal work due to the transverse shear is null.

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta\underline{\varepsilon} : \underline{\sigma} \, dV = \int_A \left( \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} \, N_{\alpha\beta} + \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} \right) dA\]

The definition of \(N_{\alpha\beta}\) and \(M_{\alpha\beta}\) (forces and moments per unit length) is identical to the ones reported in the context of the Mindlin model.

Generalized External Forces

\[\delta W_e = \int_A \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{n}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, p \right) dA\]

(With respect to the Mindlin model, the distributed moments \(\hat{m}_\alpha\) are not considered due to the inability of the model to account for transverse shear deformation effects.)

13. Equilibrium Conditions

The equilibrium conditions — viz. the strong form formulation of the problem — are derived by applying the PVW.

PVW Statement

\[\int_A \left( \delta\hat{\varepsilon}_{\alpha\beta} \, N_{\alpha\beta} + \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} \right) dA =\] \[= \int_A \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{n}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, p \right) dA + \int_{\Gamma_F} \left( \delta U_{o_\alpha} \, \hat{N}_\alpha - \delta W_{o,\alpha} \, \hat{M}_\alpha + \delta W_o \, \hat{Q} \right) d\Gamma_F\]

Note that the contribution \(-\delta W_{o,\alpha} \, \hat{M}_\alpha\) is characterized by a negative sign as the rotation is the opposite of the first derivative of \(W_o\).

Bending Part Analysis

The membrane part of the PVW is identical to the Mindlin case. The analysis is then restricted to the bending part:

\[\delta W_i = \int_A \delta K_{\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} \, dA = -\int_A \delta W_{o,\alpha\beta} \, M_{\alpha\beta} \, dA\]

Integrating by parts twice:

\[\delta W_i = -\int_A \delta W_o \, M_{\alpha\beta,\alpha\beta} \, dA + \int_{\Gamma_F} \delta W_o \, M_{\alpha\beta,\beta} \, n_\alpha \, d\Gamma_F - \int_{\Gamma_F} \delta W_{o,\alpha} \, M_{\alpha\beta} \, n_\beta \, d\Gamma_F\]

Preliminary Equilibrium Conditions

Applying \(\delta W_i = \delta W_e\), the preliminary (temporary) result is:

Equilibrium equation in \(\Omega\):

\[M_{\alpha\beta,\alpha\beta} + p = 0\]

Natural boundary conditions (in \(\Gamma_F\)):

\[Q = \hat{Q}\] \[M_{\alpha\beta} \, n_\beta = \hat{M}_\alpha\]

Essential boundary conditions (in \(\Gamma_U\)):

\[\delta W_o = 0\] \[\delta W_{o,\alpha} = 0\]

Problem: This formulation is ill-posed! The equilibrium equation is fourth-order, requiring 4 boundary conditions, but we have 6 (3 natural + 3 essential). The Kirchhoff condition addresses this issue.

14. The Kirchhoff Condition

The differential problem defined by the equilibrium condition and the relevant boundary conditions is ill-posed, i.e., it cannot be solved in the form that has been obtained.

Why the Problem is Ill-Posed

  1. The equilibrium equation is a fourth-order PDE. Indeed \(M_{\alpha\beta}\), when expressed in terms of displacement components, depends upon \(K_{\alpha\beta} = -W_{o,\alpha\beta}\) which is associated with the second derivative of \(W_o\). \(M_{\alpha\beta,\alpha\beta}\) leads to the fourth derivative of \(W_o\).
  2. The number of boundary conditions is 6 (3 natural and 3 essential), whereas a number of 4 is needed for a fourth-order differential problem.

Resolution via PVW

It is necessary to reduce the number of unknowns. This operation is based on the application of the PVW.

The components can be expanded in a reference system \(S_n\), where the axes \(s\) and \(n\) are tangent and normal, respectively, to a generic point \(P \in \Gamma_F\).

The first term involving \(\delta W_{o,s}\) can still be integrated by parts. The boundary contribution vanishes for a closed boundary (\(\Gamma_{end} \equiv \Gamma_{start}\)).

Final Equilibrium Conditions

Equilibrium Equation
\[M_{\alpha\beta,\alpha\beta} + p = 0\]
Natural Boundary Conditions (in \(\Gamma_F\))
\[Q + M_{sn,s} = \hat{Q} + \hat{M}_{sn,s}\]

(Kirchhoff free-edge condition)

\[M_{nn} = \hat{M}_n\]
Essential Boundary Conditions (in \(\Gamma_U\))
\[\delta W_o = 0\] \[\delta W_{o,n} = 0\]

The balance between the order of the equation and the number of boundary conditions is now satisfied!

Interpretation of the Kirchhoff Condition

The boundary conditions obtained are:

  • \(Q + M_{sn,s} = \hat{Q} + \hat{M}_{sn,s}\) — shear equilibrium
  • \(M_{nn} = \hat{M}_n\) — bending equilibrium

In contrast to the case of Mindlin, the equilibrium to the twisting moment is no longer available.

The effect of an applied twisting moment \(\hat{M}_{sn}\) is reported into the shear condition. The applied twisting moment is then transformed into a statically equivalent shear.

15. Rectangular Plate Boundary Conditions

Consider a rectangular plate. For simplicity, assume that none of the edges is free to undergo out-of-plane displacements (so \(W_o = 0\) for all the edges).

Classification of Boundary Conditions

Type Description Conditions
Free edge No kinematic conditions specified (not considered here) 2 natural conditions
Simply-supported Transverse displacement is zero; rotation around tangent is free 1 essential + 1 natural
Clamped Transverse displacement and rotation around tangent prevented 2 essential conditions

Example: Two Sides Clamped, Two Sides Simply-Supported

Top edge (simply-supported): \(W_o = 0\), \(M_{xx} = 0\)

Bottom edge (clamped): \(W_o = 0\), \(W_{o,x} = 0\)

Left edge (clamped): \(W_o = 0\), \(W_{o,y} = 0\)

Right edge (simply-supported): \(W_o = 0\), \(M_{yy} = 0\)

16. Plate Constitutive Law

The relation between generalized stresses and deformations is found in analogy to the procedure illustrated in the case of the Mindlin model. It is assumed that the plate is subjected to a plane state of stress.

For the Kirchhoff plate model the transverse shear forces do not enter the equilibrium equations. The constitutive law is then restricted to:

\[\begin{Bmatrix} N_{xx} \\ N_{yy} \\ N_{xy} \\ M_{xx} \\ M_{yy} \\ M_{xy} \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & & & & \\ A_{12} & A_{22} & & & & \\ & & A_{66} & & & \\ & & & D_{11} & D_{12} & \\ & & & D_{12} & D_{22} & \\ & & & & & D_{66} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} U_{o,x} \\ V_{o,y} \\ U_{o,y} + V_{o,x} \\ -W_{o,xx} \\ -W_{o,yy} \\ -2W_{o,xy} \end{Bmatrix}\]

This holds for an orthotropic plate and for an isotropic plate. For the isotropic case:

Membrane stiffnesses:

\[A_{11} = A_{22} = \frac{Et}{1-\nu^2}\] \[A_{12} = \nu \frac{Et}{1-\nu^2}\] \[A_{66} = \frac{Et}{2(1+\nu)}\]

Bending stiffnesses:

\[D_{11} = D_{22} = D\] \[D_{12} = \nu D\] \[D_{66} = \frac{1-\nu}{2} D\]

Equilibrium Equation in Terms of Displacement

The only equation relative to the bending equilibrium is:

\[M_{\alpha\beta,\alpha\beta} + p = 0\]

In expanded form:

\[M_{xx,xx} + 2M_{xy,xy} + M_{yy,yy} + p = 0\]

Substituting the constitutive equation:

For orthotropic plate:

\[D_{11} W_{o,xxxx} + 2(D_{12} + 2D_{66}) W_{o,xxyy} + D_{22} W_{o,yyyy} = p\]

Under the assumption of isotropic material:

For isotropic plate:

\[\Delta\Delta W_o = \frac{p}{D}\]

Where the Laplacian operator \(\Delta\) is defined as:

\[\Delta(\cdot) = (\cdot)_{,xx} + (\cdot)_{,yy}\]

And so:

\[\Delta\Delta(\cdot) = (\cdot)_{,xxxx} + 2(\cdot)_{,xxyy} + (\cdot)_{,yyyy}\]

17. Exact Solution for Simple Case

The strong form formulation for the bending of a thin plate allows an exact solution for the case of sinusoidal pressure distribution. This initial simple case serves also as introduction to the Navier-type solution.

Problem Setup

Consider a plate of dimensions \(a\) and \(b\), subjected to a simply-supported boundary condition along the four edges and loaded with a sinusoidal pressure:

\[p = p_o \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b}\]

Strong Form Formulation

\[\begin{cases} \Delta\Delta W_o = p/D & \text{in } \Omega \\ W_o = 0 & \text{in } \partial\Omega \text{ (all four edges)} \\ M_{xx}(0,y) = M_{xx}(a,y) = 0 \\ M_{yy}(x,0) = M_{yy}(x,b) = 0 \end{cases}\]

In terms of displacement components:

\[\begin{cases} \Delta\Delta W_o = p/D \\ W_o(0,y) = W_o(a,y) = W_o(x,0) = W_o(x,b) = 0 & \text{(essential)} \\ W_{o,xx}(0,y) = W_{o,xx}(a,y) = 0 \\ W_{o,yy}(x,0) = W_{o,yy}(x,b) = 0 \end{cases} \text{(natural)}\]

Solution

It is straightforward to verify that the solution (guess):

\[W_o = \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b} A_o\]

(with \(A_o\) = scalar to be determined) identically satisfies the boundary conditions (both essential and natural).

After substituting the guess solution into the equilibrium equation:

\[\left[ \left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)^4 + 2\left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\pi}{b}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\pi}{b}\right)^4 \right] \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b} A_o = \frac{p_o}{D} \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b}\]

Solution for \(A_o\)

\[A_o = \frac{p_o}{\pi^4 D \left[ \left(\frac{1}{a}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{b}\right)^2 \right]^2}\]

Displacement Field

\[W_o = A_o \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b}\]

with \(A_o\) as given above.

Stress Field

\[\begin{Bmatrix} \sigma_{xx}(x,y,z) \\ \sigma_{yy}(x,y,z) \\ \sigma_{xy}(x,y,z) \end{Bmatrix} = \frac{E}{1-\nu^2} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \nu & 0 \\ \nu & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{1-\nu}{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} -z W_{o,xx} \\ -z W_{o,yy} \\ -2z W_{o,xy} \end{Bmatrix}\]

Where:

\[W_{o,xx} = -A_o \left(\frac{\pi}{a}\right)^2 \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b}\] \[W_{o,yy} = -A_o \left(\frac{\pi}{b}\right)^2 \sin\frac{\pi x}{a} \sin\frac{\pi y}{b}\] \[W_{o,xy} = A_o \frac{\pi^2}{ab} \cos\frac{\pi x}{a} \cos\frac{\pi y}{b}\]
