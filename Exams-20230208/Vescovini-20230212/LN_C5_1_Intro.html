Introduction and Basic Concepts

Some basic ideas can be introduced by considering simple 1 DOF examples.

Two Preliminary Considerations

1. Sources of Instability

Instability phenomena can arise in the presence of compressive loads. However, it is worth remarking that this is not the only case of practical interest: instability phenomena can be triggered also by shear loads, bending moments, ... and, in some cases, also by traction loads.

2. Nature of Nonlinearity

The phenomena here considered imply the elastic behavior of the material. The source of nonlinearity, necessary to analyze the stability, is of geometrical nature.

Bifurcations

For small strains/infinitesimal displacements the uniqueness of the solution is guaranteed a priori. By removing this assumption the uniqueness is no more guaranteed (instability = loss of uniqueness).

Consider a geometrically non-linear system. Its response can be analyzed by "monitoring" two characteristic parameters:

  1. \(P\): load parameter
  2. \(\theta\): displacement parameter
Note:
  • 1 DOF problem → \(P, \theta\) are the load and the DOF
  • N DOF problem → \(P, \theta\) are two scalars representative of the behaviour of the structure

The Equilibrium Path

The equilibrium path in the \(P - \theta\) plane can be either linear or nonlinear:

Due to the non-uniqueness of the solution, multiple equilibrium paths can exist.

Bifurcation Point

The bifurcation point is the intersection between equilibrium paths. The equilibrium condition can change from stable to unstable. After reaching the bifurcation, four scenarios are possible (symmetric stable, symmetric unstable, asymmetric stable, asymmetric unstable).

Limit Points

Equilibrium paths can be characterized by the presence of limit points.

After reaching a limit point, an increase in the load parameter can determine a drastic change of configuration (snap-through behavior).

1 DOF Examples - Energy Principles

The stability is here analyzed using energy principles.

\[\Delta\Pi = \Pi(\theta + \delta\theta, P) - \Pi(\theta, P)\]

Taylor expansion gives:

\[\Delta\Pi = \Pi'(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} \delta\theta + \frac{1}{2!}\Pi''(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} \delta\theta^2 + \frac{1}{3!}\Pi'''(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} \delta\theta^3 + \cdots\]

Equilibrium Condition

\[\boxed{\Pi'(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} = 0 \quad \forall \delta\theta}\]

For an equilibrium configuration, the sign of \(\Delta\Pi\) depends on the second order term:

Stability Condition

\[\boxed{\Pi''(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} \geq 0 \quad \forall \delta\theta}\]

The transition from stability \(\left(\Pi''(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} \geq 0\right)\) to instability is represented by:

\[\boxed{\Pi''(\theta, P)\Big|_{\theta_{eq}} = 0}\]

← NEUTRAL EQUILIBRIUM METHOD

Note: This is not the only possible method; see Trefftz method (later).

Example 1: Cantilever with Rotational Spring

Problem Setup

Consider a rigid bar of length \(\ell\) with a rotational spring of stiffness \(K\) at the pin support. A horizontal force \(P\) is applied at the free end.

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\theta^2 - Pu\]

where the horizontal displacement is:

\[u = \ell(1 - \cos\theta)\]

Step 2: First Derivative (Equilibrium)

\[\Pi' = K\theta - P\ell\sin\theta\]

Setting \(\Pi' = 0\) for equilibrium:

\[\boxed{K\theta = P\ell\sin\theta}\]

Step 3: Equilibrium Paths

Two equilibrium paths can be identified as solutions:

  1. Path 1: \(\theta = 0\)
  2. Path 2: \(P = \frac{K}{\ell}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\)

Step 4: Second Derivative (Stability)

\[\Pi'' = K - P\ell\cos\theta\]
a. Equilibrium Path 1 (\(\theta = 0\))
\[\Pi''\Big|_{\theta=0} = K - P\ell \begin{cases} > 0 & \text{if} \quad P < K/\ell \quad \text{(stable)} \\ < 0 & \text{if} \quad P > K/\ell \quad \text{(unstable)} \end{cases}\]
b. Equilibrium Path 2 \(\left(P = \frac{K}{\ell}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)\)
\[\Pi''\Big|_{P = \frac{K}{\ell}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta}} = K - \frac{K}{\ell}\frac{\theta}{\sin\theta} \cdot \ell\cos\theta = K\left(1 - \frac{\theta}{\tan\theta}\right) > 0 \quad \forall\theta\]

This path is always stable!

Remarks

  1. The bifurcation determines the passage from stable to unstable equilibrium (one of the three paths is unstable)
  2. The bifurcation is, in this case, symmetric and stable

Example 1: Including Imperfections

Problem Setup

Let's analyze the effect of a small deviation from the nominal geometry on the response of a structure characterized by a symmetric and stable bifurcation.

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K(\theta - \theta_0)^2 - Pu\]

where:

\[u = \ell\cos\theta_0 - \ell\cos\theta\]

Expanding:

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\left(\theta^2 + \theta_0^2 - 2\theta\theta_0\right) - P\ell\cos\theta_0 + P\ell\cos\theta\]

Step 2: Derivatives

\[\Pi' = K(\theta - \theta_0) - P\ell\sin\theta\] \[\Pi'' = K - P\ell\cos\theta\]

Step 3: Equilibrium Condition

\[K(\theta - \theta_0) - P\ell\sin\theta = 0\]

\(\theta = 0\) is not a solution!

The only solution is:

\[\boxed{P = \frac{K(\theta - \theta_0)}{\ell\sin\theta}}\]

Step 4: Stability Analysis

\[\Pi''\Big|_{P = \frac{K(\theta - \theta_0)}{\ell\sin\theta}} = K - \frac{K(\theta - \theta_0)}{\ell\sin\theta} \cdot \ell\cos\theta = K\left(1 - \frac{\theta - \theta_0}{\tan\theta}\right)\]

Define \(\bar{\theta}\) such that: \(1 - \frac{\theta - \theta_0}{\tan\theta} = 0\)

  • \(\theta_{cr} < \theta < 0\): unstable equilibrium
  • \(\theta = \theta_{cr}\) or \(\theta > 0\): stable equilibrium

Remarks

  1. No more bifurcation! As \(P\) is increased, the structure follows the equilibrium path
  2. In the neighborhood of the bifurcation of the "perfect structure" a sudden increase of \(\theta\) is observed for small increments of \(P\) → the critical load of the perfect structure is a useful information
  3. A transition to the secondary path is made possible if energy is introduced in the system

Example 1: Linearized Analysis

Motivation

Let's consider the analysis of the elastic stability in the context of a linearized analysis. This is the kind of analysis performed by the majority of finite element codes.

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\theta^2 - P\ell + P\ell\cos\theta\]

Step 2: Taylor Expansion

\[\Pi = \delta\Pi + \frac{1}{2!}\delta^2\Pi + \cdots\]

First variation:

\[\delta\Pi = K\theta \cdot \delta\theta - P\ell\sin\theta\Big|_{\theta=0} \delta\theta = 0\]

Second variation:

\[\delta^2\Pi = \left(K - P\ell\cos\theta\right)\Big|_{\theta=0} \delta\theta^2 = (K - P\ell)\delta\theta^2\]

Step 3: Quadratic Approximation

\[\Pi \simeq \frac{1}{2}(K - P\ell)\theta^2 \quad \text{(quadratic } \Pi \text{ = linear equations)}\]

Step 4: Equilibrium

\[\Pi' = (K - P\ell)\theta = 0\]

This is a 1 × 1 eigenvalue problem.

The equilibrium solutions are:

  1. \(\theta = 0\) (trivial solution)
  2. \(P = K/\ell\) (non-trivial solution)

Step 5: Stability Analysis

a. Equilibrium Path 1 (\(\theta = 0\))
\[\Pi''\Big|_{\theta=0} = K - P\ell > 0 \quad \text{if} \quad P < K/\ell\]
b. Equilibrium Path 2 (\(P = K/\ell\))
\[\Pi''\Big|_{P=K/\ell} = K - K = 0\]

Important Limitation

No information regarding the stability of the equilibrium beyond the bifurcation!

Example 1: Initial Post-Buckling Analysis

Motivation

By considering a second order Taylor expansion for \(\Pi\), nothing can be said on the stability of the equilibrium paths departing from the bifurcation. One possibility is to raise the order of the expansion.

Solution

Step 1: Higher Order Expansion

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\theta^2 - P\ell + P\ell\cos\theta\]

Computing derivatives:

\[\delta\Pi = 0 \cdot \delta\theta\] \[\delta^2\Pi = (K - P\ell)\delta\theta^2\] \[\delta^3\Pi = P\ell\sin\theta\Big|_{\theta=0} \delta\theta^3 = 0 \cdot \delta\theta^3\] \[\delta^4\Pi = P\ell\cos\theta\Big|_{\theta=0} \delta\theta^4 = P\ell \cdot \delta\theta^4\]

Step 2: Fourth-Order Approximation

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}(K - P\ell)\theta^2 + \frac{1}{4!}P\ell\theta^4\]

Step 3: Equilibrium

For small deviations with respect to \(\theta = 0\), the quartic term is negligible (if compared to the quadratic one):

  1. \(\theta = 0\)
  2. \(P = K/\ell\)

Step 4: Stability with Higher Order Terms

\[\Pi'' = K - P\ell + \frac{1}{2}P\ell\theta^2\]
a. Equilibrium Path 1 (\(\theta = 0\))
\[\Pi''\Big|_{\theta=0} = K - P\ell > 0 \quad \text{if} \quad P < K/\ell\]
b. Equilibrium Path 2 (\(P = K/\ell\))
\[\Pi''\Big|_{P=K/\ell} = K - K + \frac{1}{2}\frac{K}{\ell}\ell\theta^2 = \frac{1}{2}K\theta^2 > 0 \quad \forall\theta\]

The secondary path is stable!

Remarks

  1. Higher order terms make it possible the investigation of the stability of the equilibrium paths beyond the bifurcation
  2. Far away from \(\theta = 0\), the results here derived could be wrong

Example 2: Spring at Tip

Problem Setup

In this example, the spring measures the vertical displacement of the tip rather than the rotation at the support.

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K(\ell\sin\theta)^2 - Pu \quad \text{where} \quad u = \ell(1 - \cos\theta)\] \[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\ell^2\sin^2\theta - P\ell + P\ell\cos\theta\]

Step 2: Equilibrium Condition

\[\Pi' = K\ell^2\sin\theta\cos\theta - P\ell\sin\theta = 0\]

Solutions:

  1. \(\theta = 0\)
  2. \(P = K\ell\cos\theta\)

Step 3: Stability Analysis

\[\Pi'' = K\ell^2\left(\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta\right) - P\ell\cos\theta\]
a. Equilibrium Path 1 (\(\theta = 0\))
\[\Pi'' = K\ell^2 - P\ell > 0 \quad \text{if} \quad P < K\ell\]
b. Equilibrium Path 2 (\(P = K\ell\cos\theta\))
\[\Pi'' = K\ell^2(\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta) - K\ell^2\cos^2\theta = -K\ell^2\sin^2\theta < 0 \quad \forall\theta\]

This path is always unstable!

Remarks

  1. The bifurcation is now symmetric and unstable
  2. After the bifurcation the structure undergoes a catastrophic failure (according to this model)
  3. All the equilibrium paths beyond the bifurcation are unstable

Example 2: With Imperfections

Problem Setup

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\left(\ell\sin\theta - \ell\sin\theta_0\right)^2 - P\left(\ell\cos\theta_0 - \ell\cos\theta\right)\] \[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\ell^2\left(\sin^2\theta - 2\sin\theta\sin\theta_0\right) + P\ell\cos\theta\]

Step 2: Equilibrium

\[\Pi' = K\ell^2\cos\theta\left(\sin\theta - \sin\theta_0\right) - P\ell\sin\theta = 0\]

\(\theta = 0\) is not a solution!

\[P = K\ell\cos\theta\left(1 - \frac{\sin\theta_0}{\sin\theta}\right)\]

Step 3: Stability Analysis

\[\Pi'' = K\ell^2\left(-\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta + \sin\theta\sin\theta_0\right) - P\ell\cos\theta\]

Evaluating at equilibrium:

\[\Pi''\Big|_{eq} = K\ell^2\left(-\sin^2\theta + \frac{\sin\theta_0}{\sin\theta}\right)\]

Define \(\bar{\theta}\) such that:

\[-\sin^2\bar{\theta} + \frac{\sin\theta_0}{\sin\bar{\theta}} = 0\]

Remarks

  1. The perfect structure is characterized by an unstable post-buckling response. As a consequence, the introduction of geometric imperfections determines a drastic reduction of the max load carried by the structure.
  2. Note the presence of a limit load, followed by a snap

Example 3: Two-Member Truss

Problem Setup

A symmetric two-member truss with pin supports at the base and vertical load \(P\) at the apex. Height is \(\ell\cos\theta\).

Bifurcation Analysis Results

The following bifurcation diagram can be obtained:

  • The bifurcation is asymmetric
  • The perfect structure is characterized by the presence of one stable path and an unstable one (after the bifurcation)
  • The presence of an unstable path determines a drastic reduction of the max load carried by the imperfect structure
Key Insight: Asymmetric bifurcations are particularly dangerous because even small imperfections can lead to significant load-carrying capacity reductions.

2 DOF Example

Problem Setup

Two rigid bars of length \(\ell\) connected in series. Rotational springs of stiffness \(K\) at the left support and at the middle joint. Horizontal force \(P\) at the right end.

Solution

Step 1: Total Potential Energy

\[\Pi = \frac{1}{2}K\theta_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}K(\theta_2 - \theta_1)^2 - Pu\]

where:

\[u = 2\ell - \ell\cos\theta_1 - \ell\cos\theta_2\]

Expanding:

\[\Pi = K\theta_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}K\theta_2^2 - K\theta_1\theta_2 + P\ell\cos\theta_2 + P\ell\cos\theta_1\]

Step 2: Equilibrium Equations

\[\delta\Pi = \frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_1}\delta\theta_1 + \frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_2}\delta\theta_2 = 0 \quad \forall \delta\theta_1, \delta\theta_2\] \[\frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_1} = 2K\theta_1 - K\theta_2 - P\ell\sin\theta_1\] \[\frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_2} = K\theta_2 - K\theta_1 - P\ell\sin\theta_2\]

The nonlinear equilibrium equations:

\[2K\theta_1 - K\theta_2 - P\ell\sin\theta_1 = 0\] \[-K\theta_1 + K\theta_2 - P\ell\sin\theta_2 = 0\]

Step 3: Equilibrium Solutions

  1. \(\theta_1 = \theta_2 = 0\) (trivial solution)
  2. \(\theta_1 \neq 0\), \(\theta_2 \neq 0\) (can be computed using Newton-Raphson)

Step 4: Stability Analysis

\[\Delta\Pi = \delta\Pi + \frac{1}{2!}\delta^2\Pi + \cdots\] \[\delta^2\Pi = \frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_1^2}\delta\theta_1^2 + \frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_2^2}\delta\theta_2^2 + 2\frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_1\partial\theta_2}\delta\theta_1\delta\theta_2\]

Step 5: Hessian Matrix at \(\theta_1 = \theta_2 = 0\)

Second derivatives evaluated at the trivial solution:

\[\frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_1^2}\Big|_{\theta_1=\theta_2=0} = 2K - P\ell\] \[\frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_2^2}\Big|_{\theta_1=\theta_2=0} = K - P\ell\] \[\frac{\partial^2\Pi}{\partial\theta_1\partial\theta_2} = -K\]
\[\delta^2\Pi = \underline{\delta\theta}^T \begin{bmatrix} 2K - P\ell & -K \\ -K & K - P\ell \end{bmatrix} \underline{\delta\theta} = \underline{\delta\theta}^T \underline{\underline{H}} \underline{\delta\theta}\]

where \(\underline{\underline{H}}\) is the Hessian matrix

Step 6: Stability Criterion

The solution is stable if \(\delta^2\Pi > 0\) \(\forall \delta\theta_1, \delta\theta_2\)

⟹ the eigenvalues of \(\underline{\underline{H}}\) are \(> 0\).

The solution loses its stability when \(\delta^2\Pi = 0\) for a set \(\underline{\delta\theta} \neq 0\).

Step 7: Eigenvalue Problem

\[\det\left(\underline{\underline{H}} - \lambda\underline{\underline{I}}\right) = \lambda^2 + 2(P\ell - 3K)\lambda + K^2 + P^2\ell^2 - 3P\ell K = 0\]

Eigenvalues:

\[\lambda_1 = \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}K - P\ell\] \[\lambda_2 = \frac{3+\sqrt{5}}{2}K + P\ell\]

Step 8: Critical Load Analysis

Condition \(\lambda_1\) \(\lambda_2\) Stability
\(P < \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}\frac{K}{\ell}\) \(> 0\) \(> 0\) Stable
\(P > \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}\frac{K}{\ell}\) \(< 0\) \(> 0\) Unstable

Post-Bifurcation Analysis

The analysis of the stability of the post-bifurcation paths can be performed numerically, by solving the nonlinear equilibrium equations and evaluating the eigenvalues for each equilibrium solution.

2 DOF Example - Linearized

Linearized Analysis

Step 1: Potential Energy with Linearization

\[\Pi = K\theta_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}K\theta_2^2 - K\theta_1\theta_2 + \underbrace{P\ell\cos\theta_2 + P\ell\cos\theta_1}_{\text{contributions to linearize}}\]

Step 2: Variations

\[\Delta\Pi = \delta\Pi + \frac{1}{2!}\delta^2\Pi + \cdots\]

First variation:

\[\delta\Pi = \frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_1}\delta\theta_1 + \frac{\partial\Pi}{\partial\theta_2}\delta\theta_2\] \[= (2K\theta_1 - K\theta_2 - P\ell\sin\theta_1)\delta\theta_1 + (K\theta_2 - K\theta_1 - P\ell\sin\theta_2)\delta\theta_2\]

Second variation:

\[\delta^2\Pi = (2K - P\ell\cos\theta_1)\delta\theta_1^2 + (K - P\ell\cos\theta_2)\delta\theta_2^2 - 2K\delta\theta_1\delta\theta_2\]

Step 3: Linearization Around \(\theta_1 = 0\), \(\theta_2 = 0\)

First variation at origin:

\[\delta\Pi\Big|_{\theta_1=\theta_2=0} = 0 \cdot \delta\theta_1 + 0 \cdot \delta\theta_2\]

⟹ \(\theta_1 = 0\), \(\theta_2 = 0\) is an equilibrium solution

Second variation at origin:

\[\delta^2\Pi\Big|_{\theta_1=\theta_2=0} = (2K - P\ell)\delta\theta_1^2 + (K - P\ell)\delta\theta_2^2 - 2K\delta\theta_1\delta\theta_2\]

Step 4: Matrix Form

\[\delta^2\Pi = \begin{Bmatrix} \delta\theta_1 & \delta\theta_2 \end{Bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix} 2K - P\ell & -K \\ -K & K - P\ell \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \delta\theta_1 \\ \delta\theta_2 \end{Bmatrix} = \underline{\delta\theta}^T \underline{\underline{H}} \underline{\delta\theta}\]

Step 5: Eigenvalues

\[\begin{vmatrix} 2K - P\ell - \lambda & -K \\ -K & K - P\ell - \lambda \end{vmatrix} = 0\]
\[\lambda_1 = \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{2}K - P\ell\] \[\lambda_2 = \frac{3+\sqrt{5}}{2}K + P\ell\]

(as obtained previously from nonlinear analysis)

Key Conclusion

As usual, the linearized approach provides information regarding the bifurcation, but not on the stability of the post-bifurcation paths.

Main Takeaways

  1. Linearized analysis identifies critical loads but cannot determine post-buckling stability
  2. Higher-order analysis is required for post-buckling behavior
  3. Imperfection sensitivity is high for unstable bifurcations
  4. Limit points can lead to snap-through behavior
  5. For N-DOF systems, the Hessian matrix eigenvalues determine stability
