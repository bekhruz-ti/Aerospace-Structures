Semi-Monocoque Beam Approximation

Shear Flow Analysis of Thin-Walled Aerospace Structures

1. Introduction to Semi-Monocoque Approximation

1.1 Background

The solution obtained for the direct stress component \(\sigma_{zz}\) is an exact and closed-form result that can be applied independently on the beam section shape.

Contrarily, the evaluation of the shear stress components \(\sigma_{xz}\) and \(\sigma_{yz}\) (which are not null whenever the shear and/or torsion internal action is different from zero) is generally a more complex task to be accomplished. In this sense, the analogy with fluid motion allowed to gather insight into the qualitative description of the distribution of the shear flows.

The qualitative description of the stress distribution was then used as a basis for developing approximate solutions for:

  1. Torsion of thin open profiles
  2. Torsion of closed, single cell thin profiles

1.2 Extension to Aerospace Applications

It is now necessary to extend the range of applications, with regard to typical aerospace thin-walled constructions, and derive approximate solutions for:

  1. Torsion of thin walled single/multi cell section
  2. Shear of thin walled single/multi cell section

The typical application can be imagined as a multi-cell stringer-stiffened beam section:

The stress \(\sigma_{zz}\) is obtained as:

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x\]

where \(M_x\) and \(M_y\) depend linearly on the applied shear forces.

Key Point: The shear stress components will be evaluated in the context of the semi-monocoque approximation. With the term "semi-monocoque" it is here intended the approximating technique for evaluating the shear stresses of thin-walled beams.

2. The Shear Flow Equation

2.1 Derivation from Equilibrium

The evaluation of the shear flow was introduced in the context of the torsion of single-cell thin-walled beams. However, the effect of shear forces has not been assessed yet.

Consider a generic thin-walled beam section, subjected to generic internal forces \(T_x\) and \(T_y\) (shearing) and torsional moment \(M_z\).

A strategy for obtaining a solution for the shear stress distribution consists in considering the equilibrium equations:

\[\text{div } \underline{\underline{\sigma}} = \underline{0}\] \[\begin{cases} \sigma_{xz,z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{yz,z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{xz,x} + \sigma_{yz,y} + \sigma_{zz,z} = 0 \end{cases}\]

together with the boundary conditions \(\underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} = 0\) and the equivalence with internal actions:

\[T_y = \int_A \sigma_{yz} \, dA \quad ; \quad T_x = \int_A \sigma_{xz} \, dA \quad ; \quad M_z = \int_A \left( \sigma_{yz} x - \sigma_{xz} y \right) dA\]

Note: Compatibility is not accounted for, this constituting a source of approximation in the solution. The solution will be respectful of the equilibrium requirements, but will not guarantee compatibility in the thickness-wise direction.

2.2 Development of the Shear Flow Equation

The first two equations simply state that:

\[\sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{xz}(x, y) \quad ; \quad \sigma_{yz} = \sigma_{yz}(x, y)\]

The third equation is then the one to be solved:

\[\sigma_{xz,x} + \sigma_{yz,y} = -\sigma_{zz,z}\]

Recall the DSV solution for \(\sigma_{zz}\):

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x\]

with \(M_x = \overset{\circ}{M}_x + T_y z\) and \(M_y = \overset{\circ}{M}_y - T_x z\)

It follows that:

\[\sigma_{zz,z} = \frac{T_y}{J_{xx}} y + \frac{T_x}{J_{yy}} x\]

More conveniently, the expression can be written as:

\[\text{div } \underline{t} = -T_y \frac{y}{J_{xx}} - T_x \frac{x}{J_{yy}}\]

where \(\underline{t} = \sigma_{xz} \underline{e}_x + \sigma_{yz} \underline{e}_y\)

2.3 Integration Over Arbitrary Region

Consider an arbitrary region \(A'\) over the beam section \(A\):

The equilibrium equation can be integrated over \(A'\):

\[\int_{A'} \text{div } \underline{t} \, dA' = -\int_{A'} T_y \frac{y}{J_{xx}} \, dA' - \int_{A'} T_x \frac{x}{J_{yy}} \, dA'\]

Noting that \(T_x\), \(T_y\), \(J_{xx}\) and \(J_{yy}\) do not depend on \(A'\), they can be taken out of the integral. Applying the divergence theorem:

\[\int_{\partial A'} \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} \, d\Gamma = -\frac{T_y}{J_{xx}} \int_{A'} y \, dA' - \frac{T_x}{J_{yy}} \int_{A'} x \, dA' = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} - T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}}\]

where \(S_x' = \int_{A'} y \, dA'\) and \(S_y' = \int_{A'} x \, dA'\) are the first order moments of inertia of the area \(A'\).

Recalling that \(\phi = \int_{\partial A'} \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} \, d\Gamma\) denotes the net flow of shear stresses outflowing from \(A'\):

Shear Flow Equation: \[\phi = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} - T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}}\]

An alternative representation is:

\[\phi = -T_{z,z}'\]

where \(T_z' = \int_{A'} \sigma_{zz} \, dA'\) is the axial force associated with the portion of area \(A'\).

2.4 Remarks on the Shear Flow Equation

Analogy with Fluid Motion

The shear flow equation provides a meaningful analogy with fluid motion. Recalling that:

\[\phi = \oint_{\partial A'} \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} \, d\Gamma \quad \begin{cases} > 0 & \text{positive outflow (Source)} \\ < 0 & \text{negative outflow (Sink)} \end{cases}\]

Special Case: No Shear Forces

Whenever no shear forces are present, the shear flow equation reduces to:

\[\phi = 0\]

which is the result already available for torsion. Note that \(\phi = 0\) means constant shear flow (not null!).

Equivalence with Internal Forces

It can be shown that if the shear flow equation is satisfied, the resultant of the shear stresses over the beam section \(A\) is equal to the internal shear forces:

\[\int_A \sigma_{xz} \, dA = T_x \quad ; \quad \int_A \sigma_{yz} \, dA = T_y\]

3. The Assumption of Constant Shear Flow for Thin Panels

3.1 Analysis of I-Section

Consider a beam characterized by an I section with internal forces in the form of a shear force \(T_y\). Assume that the web is thin (in relation to the typical dimensions of the section).

The distribution of the internal shear flow can be traced by recalling the analogy with fluid motion. It is then clear that:

  1. The shear stresses are mainly carried by the web
  2. The two flanges do not contribute to the shear load carrying capabilities of the section, whilst they do contribute to the bending stiffness of the section

3.2 Shear Flow Distribution

From the shear flow equation:

\[\phi = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}}\]

The static moment is:

\[S_x' = \int_{A'} y \, dA' = S_{x_{flange}} + \int_{-h/2}^{y} \xi \, t \, d\xi = S_{x_{flange}} + \frac{t}{2} \left( y^2 - \frac{h^2}{4} \right)\]

This has a constant term (from the flange) and a quadratic term (from the web).

Whenever the web is thin, the contribution \(S_x'\) is mainly affected by the flange-related part:

\[S_x' = S_{x_{flange}} + \frac{t}{2} \left( y^2 - \frac{h^2}{4} \right) \approx S_{x_{flange}}\]

This means that the static moment \(S_x'\) does not vary as the position along the web is varied, so \(S_{x_1}' \approx S_{x_2}'\), and the shear stress distribution can be approximated as constant: \(\phi_1 = \phi_2\).

3.3 Lumped Area Model

According to these observations, a natural way of representing the section is:

  • Concentrated area (flange + web contribution)
  • Shear web (no area) - capable of transferring shear forces only
  • Concentrated area

In particular:

  1. The web is modelled as a line with no associated area, capable of transferring shear forces only
  2. The flanges are modelled as lumped areas where the contribution of the shear web is introduced

Key Result: Since \(S_{x_1}' = S_{x_2}'\) (the area is only in the lumped areas), then \(\phi_1 = \phi_2\), which means the shear flow over the web is constant.

4. Remarks on the Semi-Monocoque Scheme

4.1 Application to Wing-Box Structure

This type of approximation is one of the key ideas of the semi-monocoque scheme. Using the same approach, the following scheme can be applied to a two-cell wing-box structure:

4.2 Key Assumptions

1. Thin Panels

The panels are assumed to be thin, such that the shear flow can be assumed to be constant. Whenever the panels get thicker, the assumption of constant shear flow becomes stronger. The model could still be used as a preliminary mean to perform calculations, but the evaluation of the appropriateness is the responsibility of the analyst.

2. Small Stringers

The stringers should be characterized by a section which is small in comparison to the dimensions of the section.

3. Ribs/Frames Properties

Ribs/frames are assumed to be highly (infinitely) stiff in the in-plane directions, while they are considered highly compliant in the out-of-plane direction.

The in-plane rigidity is due to the fact that a rib, when loaded in the in-plane direction, works as a membrane with much higher stiffness values compared to bending.

The out-of-plane flexibility means that the beam sections are not prevented from warping in correspondence of the ribs. This is consistent with the torsion problem where the beam section was assumed free to warp.

Note: Whenever the beam is prevented from warping, different modelling choices are needed (e.g., Vlasov beam model) to account for the stresses \(\sigma_{zz}\) arising from this kind of constraint.

4.3 Lumping Process

According to the semi-monocoque model, the areas are lumped, thus any stringer is replaced with a concentrated area inclusive of the panel area:

The model can be refined using "dummy" lumped areas:

  • ○ : dummy area (no actual stringer)
  • ● : lumped stringers

4.4 Panel Stress Reference

The panels are assumed to be thin, and the thickness is not sketched as the internal shear stress is referred to the panel midline:

Important: According to the semi-monocoque approximation, a thin panel cannot react a torsional load. The shear flow referred to the midline will be zero, as the integral of the linear distribution is null: \(q = \int_t \sigma_{sz}(n) \, dn = 0\)

4.5 Bending Stiffness Considerations

When the stringer area is lumped, attention should be paid not to artificially increase the bending stiffness of the section.

The contribution of the stringer to the second moment of inertia:

  • Actual shape: \(J_{xx} = J_{xx}^{local} + A d_1^2\)
  • Lumped scheme: \(J_{xx} = A d_2^2\)

Since \(d_2 > d_1\), the process of lumping tends to raise the value of \(J_{xx}\). For this reason:

\[A_{lumped} = A \frac{d_1^2}{d_2^2}\]

Load Carrying Roles

Having lumped the areas in the stringers:

  1. The normal stress is carried by the lumped stringers
  2. The shear stress is carried by the panels

Variations of shear stresses are possible only when crossing lumped areas.

5.2 Resultant of Flow in a Panel

It is important to understand how to evaluate the resultants associated with the shear flow acting in a generic panel.

Resultant Along Direction \(\underline{r}\)

The contribution due to the infinitesimal portion \(ds\) is:

\[dR = q \cos \beta \, ds\]

It follows that \(dR = q \cos \beta \, ds = q \, dr\), and the resultant is:

\[R_{\underline{r}} = \int_A^B dR = \int_A^B q \, dr = q \, h_r\]

where \(h_r\) is the projection of the length \(h\) over the direction \(\underline{r}\)

As a consequence: \(R_{\underline{r}} = 0\) whenever \(A \equiv B\) (closed curve).

Moment with Respect to Point P

The infinitesimal moment contribution is:

\[dM = q \, ds \cdot d = 2q \, d\Omega\]

Integrating:

\[M = \int_A^B dM = 2q \int_A^B d\Omega = 2q \, \Omega_{AB}\]

where \(\Omega_{AB}\) is the area \(PAB\)

For a closed section (Bredt's formula): \(M = 2q \, \Omega_{cell}\)

6. Solution Strategy and Stress Evaluation

6.1 Principal Axes

The solution strategy relies upon the discretization of the section into lumped areas and the application of the shear flow equation. The solution was derived by considering principal axes, which will always be considered during the analysis.

Whenever the section does not display any kind of symmetry, the principal axes can be determined as follows:

  1. Consider a generic reference system \(\bar{x}\bar{y}\)
  2. Evaluate the position of the centroid: \[x_{CG} = \frac{\Sigma_i A_i \bar{x}_i}{\Sigma_i A_i} \quad ; \quad y_{CG} = \frac{\Sigma_i A_i \bar{y}_i}{\Sigma_i A_i}\]
  3. Determine the moments of inertia with respect to axes \(\tilde{x}, \tilde{y}\) with origin at the centroid: \[J_{\tilde{x}\tilde{x}} = \Sigma_i A_i \tilde{y}_i^2 \quad ; \quad J_{\tilde{y}\tilde{y}} = \Sigma_i A_i \tilde{x}_i^2 \quad ; \quad J_{\tilde{x}\tilde{y}} = \Sigma_i A_i \tilde{x}_i \tilde{y}_i\] \[\alpha = \frac{1}{2} \arctan \frac{2 J_{\tilde{x}\tilde{y}}}{J_{\tilde{y}\tilde{y}} - J_{\tilde{x}\tilde{x}}}\]
  4. Rotate \(\tilde{x}\tilde{y}\) by angle \(\alpha\): \[x = \cos \alpha \, \tilde{x} + \sin \alpha \, \tilde{y} \quad ; \quad y = -\sin \alpha \, \tilde{x} + \cos \alpha \, \tilde{y}\]

6.2 Evaluation of Stresses

Normal Stress \(\sigma_{zz}\)

Once the principal axes are available:

\[\sigma_{zz_i} = \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y_i - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x_i \quad (i = i\text{-th stringer})\]

where \(A = \Sigma_i A_i\) is the total section area.

Shear Stresses

Obtained by making use of the shear flow equation:

\[\phi = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} - T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}} \quad \text{(a)}\] \[= -T_{z,z}' \quad \text{(b)}\]

Equation (a) provides the "operative" formula. Equation (b) is a useful alternative providing insight into the actual meaning of the shear flow equation.

6.3 Shear Flow Equation Application

Applying the shear flow equation with respect to region \(A'\):

\[\phi = \int_{\partial A'} \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} \, d\Gamma = q_2 + q_3 - q_1\]

Recalling that \(\phi = -T_{z,z}'\):

\[q_2 + q_3 - q_1 = -T_{z,z}'\]

The equilibrium along \(z\) is:

\[T_z' + dT_z' - T_z' + q_2 \, dz - q_1 \, dz + q_3 \, dz = 0\] \[-dT_z' = (q_2 - q_1 + q_3) \, dz\]

The shear flow equation, obtained after integrating over \(A'\) the third equilibrium equation, is then an equation expressing the equilibrium condition along the \(z\)-axis.

6.4 Number of Equations

When solving a section, it is possible to write:

  • \(n - 1\) shear flow equations, linearly independent of each other
  • The number of unknown shear flows to be determined is \(m\)

The number of additional equations needed is:

\[\underbrace{m}_{\text{unknowns}} - \underbrace{(n-1)}_{\text{shear flow equations}} = m - n + 1 = N \quad \text{(number of cells)}\]

7. Solution Procedure for Open Sections

7.1 Open Section Example

For an open section with \(n = 4\):

  • 3 linearly independent shear flow equations available
  • 3 unknown shear flows (\(q_1, q_2, q_3\))
  • \(N = 0\) cells → Problem can be solved!
\[q_1 = -T_{z_1,z}' \quad ; \quad q_2 = -T_{z_2,z}' \quad ; \quad q_3 = -T_{z_3,z}'\]

where \(T_{z_i}' = \int_{A_i} \sigma_{zz} \, dA_i\)

7.2 C-Section Example

Consider a C-section subjected to internal shear forces \(T_x\) and \(T_y\):

The number of unknown shear flows is 3, and 3 independent shear flow equations are available. Recalling \(\phi = -T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}}\):

\[q_1 = -T_x \frac{S_{y_1}}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_{x_1}}{J_{xx}}\] \[q_2 = -T_x \frac{S_{y_1} + S_{y_2}}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_{x_1} + S_{x_2}}{J_{xx}}\] \[q_3 = -T_x \frac{S_{y_1} + S_{y_2} + S_{y_3}}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_{x_1} + S_{x_2} + S_{x_3}}{J_{xx}}\]

7.3 Shear Center Position

An important property of the section is the shear center position.

Write the equivalence with the internal moment:

\[2q_1 \Omega_1 + 2q_2 \Omega_2 + 2q_3 \Omega_3 = T_x b - T_y a\]

Substituting the expressions for \(q_i\) and simplifying:

\[T_y (a - x_{sc}) = T_x (b - y_{sc})\]

or:

\[b - y_{sc} = \frac{T_y}{T_x} (a - x_{sc})\]

where the shear center coordinates are:

\[x_{sc} = \frac{2}{J_{xx}} \Sigma_i S_{x_i}' \Omega_i \quad ; \quad y_{sc} = -\frac{2}{J_{yy}} \Sigma_i S_{y_i}' \Omega_i\]

Key Points:

  • An open section, when modelled according to the semi-monocoque scheme, cannot react torsion loads
  • Shear forces can be applied only at the shear center, so the load is purely of shearing type
  • The shear center position is fully determined by the geometric properties of the section - it is a section property

8. Solution Procedure for Single-Cell Closed Sections

8.1 Characteristics of Single-Cell Sections

A closed section with one single cell is characterized by:

  • \(m\) unknown shear flows
  • \(n-1\) shear flow equations
  • 1 equivalence condition with internal moment

Single-cell sections are a statically determined scheme. The solution can be found by considering equilibrium conditions only. Compatibility does not need to be enforced.

The Missing Equation

The shear flow equation states that \(q_2 - q_1 = \phi = -T_{z_2,z}'\). If an additional circulating flow \(q^*\) is added across all panels:

\[q_2 - q_1 + \cancel{q^*} - \cancel{q^*} = \phi = -T_{z_2,z}'\]

The result is unchanged! This means the solution is indeterminate, and one equation is missing.

The missing equation is the equivalence condition:

\[M_z = 2 \Sigma_i \Omega_i q_i\]

Note: The equation \(M_z = 2 \Sigma_i \Omega_i q_i\) is an equivalence condition, not an equilibrium one.

8.2 Solution Procedure

For a section with \(n=6\), \(m=6\), \(N=1\):

  1. Apply \(n-1\) shear flow equations after "cutting" one panel (choice is arbitrary). The flow along the cut is zero: \(q_6' = 0\). The other flows are denoted as \(q_i'\): \[q_i' = -T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} \quad (i = 1, \ldots 5)\]
  2. Introduce a circulating flow \(q^*\) in the closed cell. This flow circulates through all panels belonging to the cell, so the shear flow equations are still satisfied: \[q_i = q_i' + q^* \quad \text{if panel } i \in \text{cell}\] \[q_i = q_i' \quad \text{if panel } i \notin \text{cell}\]
  3. Impose equivalence to internal moment with respect to an arbitrary point \(P\): \[2 \Sigma_i q_i' \Omega_i + 2 q^* \Omega_{cell} = M_P\] where \(M_P = M_z - T_x b - T_y a\) is the moment due to internal actions with respect to \(P\).

The value of \(q^*\) is obtained as:

\[q^* = \frac{M_P - 2 \Sigma_i q_i' \Omega_i}{2 \Omega_{cell}}\]

9. Evaluation of Torsion

9.1 Purpose

The evaluation of the torsion \(\theta'\) is necessary for:

  1. Solving sections with more than one cell
  2. Evaluating the torsional stiffness of generic sections

9.2 Application of PCVW

Consider a section with two cells and extract an infinitesimal portion \(dz\). The evaluation of \(\theta'\) is conducted by applying the PCVW with known shear flows.

External Work

\[\delta W_e^* = \delta M_z (\theta + d\theta) - \delta M_z \theta = \delta M_z \, d\theta = d\theta\]

Internal Work

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_V \delta \sigma_{ik} \varepsilon_{ik} \, dV = \int_V \delta \tau \, \gamma \, dV\]

where \(\delta \sigma_{ik}\) identifies the variation subjected to equilibrium requirements due to \(\delta M_z\), and \(\varepsilon_{ik}\) are the real deformations.

For the real system, the only non-null strain components are \(\gamma_{xz}\) and \(\gamma_{yz}\), or in the \(snz\) system, \(\gamma_{sz} \neq 0\).

9.3 Derivation of Torsion Formula

The choice of \(\delta q_m\) is arbitrary as long as equilibrium is satisfied. A useful choice is to have flows only in one cell:

\[\delta q = \frac{\delta M_z}{2\Omega_{cell_1}} = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_1}}\]

Introducing the constitutive equation \(\gamma_m = \frac{\tau_m}{G} = \frac{q_m}{G t_m}\):

\[\delta W_i^* = \sum_{\textcircled{1}} \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_1} t_m^2 G} q_m \, dV_m = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_1} G} \sum_{\textcircled{1}} \frac{q_m \ell_m}{t_m} dz\]

where \(\ell_m\) is the length of the \(m\)-th panel.

Imposing \(\delta W_i^* = \delta W_e^*\):

Torsion Rate: \[\frac{d\theta}{dz} = \theta' = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_k} G} \sum_{\textcircled{k}} \frac{q_m \ell_m}{t_m}\]

Remark: The choice of the flows \(\delta q_m\) was arbitrary. All steps could be repeated by considering any cell \(k\), leading to the same \(\theta'\) as it refers to the overall section.

10. Solution Procedure for Multi-Cell Closed Sections

10.1 Statically Indeterminate Problem

Consider a section with \(N > 1\) cells:

This is a statically indeterminate problem: the equilibrium conditions do not suffice for satisfying the balance between equations and unknowns.

Overview of Equations

  • \(n-1\) shear flow equations
  • 1 equivalence with internal moment
  • \(N-1\) compatibility conditions (remaining equations)
\[\underbrace{m}_{\text{shear flows}} - \underbrace{(n - 1 + 1)}_{\text{equations}} = m - n = N - 1\]

10.2 Solution Procedure

  1. Cut a number of panels equal to \(N\)
  2. Evaluate the flows \(q_i'\) as: \[q_i' = -T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}} - T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} \quad \text{(non-cut panels)}\] \[q_i' = 0 \quad \text{(cut panels)}\]
  3. Impose the equivalence with internal moment: \[\Sigma_i 2\Omega_i q_i' + \sum_{k=1}^{N} 2\Omega_{cell_k} q_k^* = M_z\]
  4. Impose \(N-1\) compatibility conditions: \[\theta_1' = \theta_2' = \cdots = \theta_N'\] or: \[\frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_1} G} \sum_{\textcircled{1}} \frac{q_m \ell_m}{t_m} = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell_2} G} \sum_{\textcircled{2}} \frac{q_m \ell_m}{t_m} = \cdots\]
  5. Repeat steps 2-4 considering \(T_x\) to obtain \(y_{sc}\)

Justification of Compatibility: The condition \(\theta_1' = \theta_2' = \cdots \theta_N'\) is a requirement of internal compatibility which holds irrespectively on the presence of the ribs. It was obtained by application of the PCVW without any reference to the presence of ribs.

10.3 Shear Center for Closed Sections

The shear center is defined as the point where the application of the shear forces \(T_x\) and \(T_y\) determines a torsion \(\theta' = 0\).

Single-Cell Section Procedure

  1. Determine principal axes
  2. Evaluate shear flows: \[q_i' = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} \quad \text{(non-cut panel)}\] \[q_i' = 0 \quad \text{(cut panel)}\] \[\Sigma 2\Omega_i q_i' + 2\Omega_{cell} q^* = T_y x_{sc}\]
  3. Impose \(\theta' = 0\): \[\theta' = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell} G} \sum_{\textcircled{1}} \frac{q_m \ell_m}{t_m} = 0\]
  4. Solve to obtain \(q_i\) (\(i = 1, \ldots m\)) and \(x_{sc}\)

Remarks

  • The shear center is a property of the section (values of \(T_x\) and \(T_y\) can be arbitrarily chosen for calculations)
  • The shear center position identifies the center of relative rotation between two infinitely close sections when the internal action is purely torsional
  • Whenever the shear centers are aligned along the same line, this line is referred to as the elastic axis

11. Evaluation of Section Properties

11.1 Internal Complementary Work

The internal complementary work for a beam, previously restricted to bending and axial behaviour, is now generalized to account for torsion and shear:

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_V \underline{\underline{\delta \sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \, dV = \int_V \left( \delta \sigma_{zz} \varepsilon_{zz} + \delta \sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz} + \delta \sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz} \right) dV\]

After introducing the DSV solution:

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_\ell \left( \delta T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + \delta M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz + \int_V \left( \delta \sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz} + \delta \sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz} \right) dV\]

The volume integral is the internal work due to shear stresses and corresponding deformations.

11.2 Separation of Shear and Torsion Contributions

The shear stresses \(\sigma_{xz}\) and \(\sigma_{yz}\) are associated with:

  • Internal shear forces \(T_x\), \(T_y\)
  • Internal torsional moment \(M_z\)

To separate these contributions, consider a reference system with axis \(z\) passing through the shear center:

\[\int_V \left( \delta \sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz} + \delta \sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz} \right) dV = \int_\ell \delta M_z \frac{M_z}{GJ} dz + \int_V \left( \delta \sigma_{xz}^{shear} \gamma_{xz}^{shear} + \delta \sigma_{yz}^{shear} \gamma_{yz}^{shear} \right) dV\]

The shear contribution can be expressed as:

\[\int_V \delta \sigma_{xz}^{shear} \gamma_{xz}^{shear} \, dV = \int_\ell \delta T_x \frac{T_x}{GA_x^*} dz\] \[\int_V \delta \sigma_{yz}^{shear} \gamma_{yz}^{shear} \, dV = \int_\ell \delta T_y \frac{T_y}{GA_y^*} dz\]

where \(GA_x^*\) and \(GA_y^*\) are the shear stiffnesses.

11.3 Complete Internal Virtual Work

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_0^\ell \left( \delta T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + \delta M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} + \delta T_x \frac{T_x}{GA_x^*} + \delta T_y \frac{T_y}{GA_y^*} + \delta M_z \frac{M_z}{GJ} \right) dz\]

where:

  • The first three contributions are referred to the principal axes
  • The last three contributions are referred to the system passing through the shear center

Matrix Notation

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_0^\ell \underline{\delta T}^T \underline{\underline{C}} \, \underline{T} \, dz\]

with \(\underline{T} = \{ T_x \quad T_y \quad T_z \quad M_x \quad M_y \quad M_z \}^T\) and the compliance matrix:

\[\underline{\underline{C}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{GA_x^*} & & & & & \\ & \frac{1}{GA_y^*} & & & & \\ & & \frac{1}{EA} & & & \\ & & & \frac{1}{EJ_{xx}} & & \\ & & & & \frac{1}{EJ_{yy}} & \\ & & & & & \frac{1}{GJ} \end{bmatrix}\]

The choice of principal axes and axes passing through the shear center gives a diagonal compliance matrix. A different set of axes would result in a fully populated matrix.

11.4 Section Stiffness Formulas

Axial Stiffness

\[EA = E \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i\]

Bending Stiffnesses

\[EJ_{xx} = E \Sigma_i A_i y_i^2 \quad ; \quad EJ_{yy} = E \Sigma_i A_i x_i^2\]

where \(x_i\), \(y_i\) are distances from the principal axes.

Torsional Stiffness

Open sections: \(GJ = 0\) (using semi-monocoque scheme)

Single-cell closed sections:

\[J = \frac{4\Omega^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{m} \ell_i / t_i}\]

Multi-cell sections:

\[GJ = \frac{M_z}{\theta'}\]

where \(M_z\) is an arbitrary torsional moment and \(\theta' = \frac{1}{2\Omega_{cell} G} \sum_{\textcircled{k}} \frac{q_i \ell_i}{t_i}\)

Shear Stiffnesses

The evaluation requires solving the section with an internal shear force applied on the shear center. Using energy equivalence:

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_\ell \sum_{i=1}^{m} \delta q_m \frac{\ell_m}{G t_m} q_m \, dz\]

Equating with the expression in terms of internal actions:

\[GA_x^* = \frac{\delta T_x T_x}{\sum_{i=1}^{m} \delta q_m \frac{\ell_m}{G t_m} q_m} \quad ; \quad GA_y^* = \frac{\delta T_y T_y}{\sum_{i=1}^{m} \delta q_m \frac{\ell_m}{G t_m} q_m}\]

The shear factor \(\chi_x\) is defined as:

\[\chi_x = \frac{A_x^*}{A_{TOT}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad GA_x^* = \chi_x \, G A_{TOT}\]

which provides a measure of how much area is collaborating to resist the shear force.

12. Comparison Examples: Compact vs. Thin-Walled Sections

12.1 Problem Setup

For quantifying the effect of shear deformability in thin-walled beams, compare the strain energies in two sections: a compact square section and a thin-walled one.

Internal forces: \(M_x = -Fz\), \(T_y = -F\)

Example 1: Compact Square Section

Section properties:

\[J = \frac{1}{12} a^4 \quad ; \quad A_y^* = \frac{5}{6} a^2\]

Strain energies:

\[U_{bending} = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \frac{M_x^2}{EJ} dz = \frac{1}{2} \frac{F^2 \ell^3}{3EJ}\] \[U_{shear} = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \frac{T_y^2}{GA_y^*} dz = \frac{1}{2} \frac{F^2 \ell}{GA_y^*}\]

Ratio:

\[\frac{U_{shear}}{U_{bending}} = \frac{\ell}{GA_y^*} \cdot \frac{3EJ}{\ell^3} \approx \frac{3}{5} \frac{a^2}{\ell^2}\]

For \(a = 100\) and \(\ell = 1000\):

\[\frac{U_{shear}}{U_{bending}} \approx \frac{3}{5} \cdot 10^{-2} = \frac{3}{500} < 1\%\]

Conclusion: The energy associated with shear deformability is less than 1% of the bending energy.

Example 2: Thin-Walled Square Section

Section characterized by the same bending stiffness as the compact section:

\[J = 4A \left( \frac{a}{2} \right)^2 = A a^2 = \frac{1}{12} a^4\]

However, the shear area is now: \(A_y^* = 2at\)

Ratio:

\[\frac{U_{shear}}{U_{bending}} = \frac{1}{4} \frac{a^3}{\ell^2 t} = \frac{1}{4} \frac{a^3}{\ell^2} \cdot \frac{120}{a} = 30 \frac{a^2}{\ell^2}\]

For \(a = 100\) and \(\ell = 1000\):

\[\frac{U_{shear}}{U_{bending}} \approx 30 \cdot 10^{-2} = 30\%\]

Conclusion: The energy associated with shear deformability is 30% of the bending energy - not negligible!

Example 3: Rectangular Closed Section

Torsional Stiffness

\[J = \frac{4\Omega^2}{\oint \frac{1}{t(s)} ds} = \frac{4a^2 b^2 t}{2(a+b)} = \frac{2a^2 b^2 t}{(a+b)}\] \[GJ = G \frac{2a^2 b^2 t}{(a+b)}\]

Shear Stiffness

From symmetry, the shear center coincides with the centroid. The shear flows are:

\[A_x^* = \frac{\delta T_y T_y}{\sum \delta q_m \frac{\ell_m}{t_m} q_m} = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{2a^2 t} a} = 2at\]

Thus: \(GA_x^* = G \cdot 2at\)

Note: The area \(A_x^*\) equals the area of the shear webs - the fraction of total area contributing to carry the shear load. This coincidence occurs whenever the shear flow is constant along the webs. If the shear flow is not constant: \(A_y^* < A_{web}\)

Shear Flow Equation

\[\phi = -T_y \frac{S_x'}{J_{xx}} - T_x \frac{S_y'}{J_{yy}} = -T_{z,z}'\]
