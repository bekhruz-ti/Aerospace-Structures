Variational Principles in Structural Mechanics

Lecture Notes on Energy Methods and Virtual Work Principles

1. Introduction to Variational Principles

1.1 Overview

The solution of the elastic problem requires that both equilibrium and compatibility are fulfilled.

It was shown that the set of governing equations is given by the PDE representing the equilibrium and the compatibility in the domain as well as at the boundaries (via boundary conditions).

Strong Form: When the solution is obtained by solving exactly or approximately the governing equations, it is said that the problem is formulated in a strong form manner.
Weak Form: Another approach consists in solving the problem in weak form. The equations are multiplied with test functions and integrated over the domain. Generally this is the approach which is used in structural mechanics. The implementation into numerical technique is straightforward and the requirements on the regularity of the solution are relaxed.

Among the class of weak form formulations, a special set is given by the variational principles, such as the Principle of Virtual Displacements (PVD), the minimum potential energy principle and their dual counterparts (the Complementary-energy related ones).

1.2 Classification of Approaches

Impose Equilibrium (Compatibility a priori)
  • Principle of Virtual Works
  • Minimum Potential Energy Principles
Impose Compatibility (Equilibrium a priori)
  • Principle of Complementary Virtual Works
  • Menabrea's Theorem

This means that either the equilibrium or the compatibility needs to be satisfied a priori.

Note: More advanced variational principles exist where neither compatibility nor equilibrium need to be satisfied a priori â†’ mixed approaches.

Force-Based Approaches

Force-based approaches rely upon the use of forces as problem unknowns. The equilibrium can be easily established a priori and, whenever it is necessary, the compatibility can be imposed using the PCVW or Menabrea.

Displacement-Based Approaches

Displacement-based approaches are formulated by assuming a displacement field which is intrinsically compatible. The solution, in this case, is obtained by enforcing the equilibrium condition using the PVW or the Minimum Potential Energy principle.

2. Principle of Virtual Works (PVW)

2.1 Governing Equations

The set of governing equations for a 3D Continuum, under the assumptions of infinitesimal strain/displacements is given by:

\[ \begin{cases} \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f} = \underline{0} & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) = \underline{\underline{0}} & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{t} = \hat{\underline{t}} & \text{in } S_F \\[8pt] \underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}} & \text{in } S_u \end{cases} \]

Assume that compatibility conditions in \(\Omega\) and \(S_u\) are satisfied.

2.2 Weak Form Derivation

Consider now a set of arbitrary infinitesimal displacements \(\delta \underline{u}\). It follows that the problem can be formulated as:

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot (\text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f}) \, dV - \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot (\underline{t} - \hat{\underline{t}}) \, dS_F = 0 \quad \forall \delta \underline{u} \]

Whenever this condition is satisfied for any \(\delta \underline{u}\), it means that the equilibrium conditions are identically satisfied, viz:

\[ \begin{cases} \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f} = 0 & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{t} - \hat{\underline{t}} = 0 & \text{in } S_F \end{cases} \]
Note: The "weak form" reported in the box is the same mathematical approach adopted in the courses of Mathematics where \(\delta \underline{u}\) are denoted as test functions (or weight functions).

2.3 Integration by Parts and Divergence Theorem

Recall that the virtual displacements are compatible, meaning that \(\delta \underline{u} = 0\) on \(S_u\).

The equation in the box is not yet the expression of the PVW. To achieve the final form of the PVW it is necessary to perform integration by parts and apply the divergence theorem.

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V \delta u_i \, \sigma_{ik,k} \, dV \]
\[ = \int_V (\delta u_i \sigma_{ik})_{,k} \, dV - \int_V \delta u_{i,k} \, \sigma_{ik} \, dV \]
\[ = \int_V \text{div}(\delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{\underline{\sigma}}) \, dV - \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV \]
Note on strain: Having noted that: \[\varepsilon_{ik} = \frac{1}{2}(u_{i,k} + u_{k,i})\] \[\delta \varepsilon_{ik} = \frac{1}{2}(\delta u_{i,k} + \delta u_{k,i})\] In addition \(\sigma_{ik} = \sigma_{ki}\), so: \[\delta \varepsilon_{ik} \, \sigma_{ik} = \frac{1}{2}(\delta u_{i,k} + \delta u_{k,i}) \sigma_{ik} = \delta u_{i,k} \, \sigma_{ik}\]

Applying divergence theorem:

\[ = \int_S \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} \, dS - \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV \]

Observe now that \(\delta \underline{u} = 0\) in \(S_u\) and \(\delta \underline{u} \neq 0\) in \(S_F\):

\[ = \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} \, dS_F - \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{t} \, dS_F - \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV \]

2.4 Final Form of PVW

The expression in the box can then be re-organized as:

\[ \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{t} \, dS_F - \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV + \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV - \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot (\underline{t} - \hat{\underline{t}}) \, dS_F = 0 \]

and simplifying:

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F \]

or equivalently:

Principle of Virtual Works

\[\boxed{\delta W_i = \delta W_e}\]

where:

Internal Virtual Work: \[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\]
External Virtual Work: \[\delta W_e = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F\]

A body is in equilibrium if and only if the virtual internal work equals the external virtual work.

Note that the equilibrium equation + BCs are identically satisfied whenever the PVW is verified for any set of infinitesimal virtual displacements.

2.5 Properties of PVW

  • The PVW is a variational principle: Both the equilibrium equation and the equilibrium condition at the boundaries \(S_F\) are accounted for.
  • The PVW is a scalar (one single!) equation.
  • No reference is made to the material model (the constitutive law did not enter the previous steps). It follows that the PVW holds irrespective of the material behaviour.
  • It is useful to remark, once more, that compatibility requirements need to be verified a priori.

2.6 Equilibrium Equations from PVW

Clearly the reverse process can be performed, and the equilibrium conditions obtained from the PVW. It is a useful exercise:

Starting from \(\delta W_i = \delta W_e\):

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F \]

Consider the internal virtual work:

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \varepsilon_{ik} \, \sigma_{ik} \, dV\]

Recalling that \(\delta \varepsilon_{ik} = \frac{1}{2}(\delta u_{i,k} + \delta u_{k,i})\) and that the virtual variations should be compatible:

\[\Rightarrow \quad \delta W_i = \int_V \delta u_{i,k} \, \sigma_{ik} \, dV\]
\[= \int_V (\delta u_i \, \sigma_{ik})_{,k} \, dV - \int_V \delta u_i \, \sigma_{ik,k} \, dV\]
\[= \int_V \text{div}(\delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{\underline{\sigma}}) \, dV - \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\]
\[= \int_S \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} \, dS - \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\]
\[= \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{t} \, dS_F - \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\]

So, the variational statement reads:

\[ \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{t} \, dS_F - \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F \]

or:

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot (\text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f}) \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot (\hat{\underline{t}} - \underline{t}) \, dS_F = 0 \]

If this equation is verified for \(\forall \delta \underline{u}\), then:

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f} &= \underline{0} & \text{in } \Omega \\ \underline{t} &= \hat{\underline{t}} & \text{in } S_F \end{aligned} \]

2.7 Virtual Variations and Boundary Conditions

Note that, in spite of the fact that compatibility conditions are satisfied a priori, the virtual variations satisfy homogeneous boundary conditions.

\(\underline{u}\) satisfies:

\[\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) \quad \text{in } \Omega\] \[\underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}} \quad \text{in } S_u\]

The perturbed configuration \(\underline{u} + \delta \underline{u}\) will lead to:

\[\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} + \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T + \text{grad} \, \delta \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \delta \underline{u}^T \right)\] \[\underline{u} + \delta \underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}}\]

and so:

\[\delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \delta \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \delta \underline{u}^T \right) \quad \text{in } \Omega\] \[\delta \underline{u} = \underline{0} \quad \text{in } S_u\]

3. Principle of Complementary Virtual Works (PCVW)

3.1 Starting Point

Consider again the set of governing equations in terms of equilibrium and compatibility:

\[ \begin{cases} \text{div} \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{f} = \underline{0} & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) = \underline{\underline{0}} & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{t} = \hat{\underline{t}} & \text{in } S_F \\[8pt] \underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}} & \text{in } S_u \end{cases} \]

Assume now that the equilibrium conditions are identically satisfied in \(\Omega\) and \(S_F\).

3.2 Weak Form of Compatibility

The weak form of the compatibility conditions is then obtained as:

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \left( \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} - \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) \right) dV + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \left( \underline{u} - \hat{\underline{u}} \right) dS_u = 0 \]

The variations \(\delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}\) and \(\delta \underline{t}\) are infinitesimal and in equilibrium.

\[\int_V \left[ \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) \right] dV =\]
\[= \int_V \delta \sigma_{ik} \frac{1}{2} (u_{i,k} + u_{k,i}) \, dV = \int_V \delta \sigma_{ik} \, u_{i,k} \, dV\]

(due to the symmetry of \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}}\))

So:

\[\int_V \left( \delta \sigma_{ik} \, \varepsilon_{ik} - \delta \sigma_{ik} \, u_{i,k} \right) dV + \int_{S_u} \delta t_i \left( u_i - \hat{u}_i \right) dS_u = 0\] \[\int_V \left[ \delta \sigma_{ik} \, \varepsilon_{ik} - (\delta \sigma_{ik} \, u_i)_{,k} + \delta \sigma_{ik,k} \, u_i \right] dV + \int_{S_u} \delta t_i (u_i - \hat{u}_i) \, dS_u = 0\]

3.3 Applying Divergence Theorem

\[\int_V \left[ \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} - \text{div}(\underline{u} \cdot \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}) + \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \right] dV + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot (\underline{u} - \hat{\underline{u}}) \, dS_u = 0\]

Applying the divergence theorem:

\[\int_V \left( \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} + \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \right) dV - \int_S \underline{u} \cdot \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} \, dS + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot (\underline{u} - \hat{\underline{u}}) \, dS_u = 0\]

Recall now that the variations \(\delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}\) and \(\delta \underline{t}\) should respect the equilibrium conditions:

\[ \begin{cases} \text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \delta \underline{f} = \underline{0} & \text{in } \Omega \\ \delta \underline{t} = \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \delta \hat{\underline{t}} & \text{in } S_F \end{cases} \]

So:

\[\int_V \underline{u} \cdot \text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV = \int_V -\delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \, dV\] \[\int_S (\delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n}) \cdot \underline{u} \, dS = \int_S \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS = \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_u\]

where \(S_F \cup S_u = S\), so:

\[= \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_u\]

The variational statement is then written as:

\[\int_V \left( \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} - \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \right) dV - \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F - \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_u + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot (\underline{u} - \hat{\underline{u}}) \, dS_u = 0\]

3.4 Final Form of PCVW

and so:

\[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \hat{\underline{u}} \, dS_u \]

or:

Principle of Complementary Virtual Works

\[\boxed{\delta W_i^* = \delta W_{ext}^*}\]
  • The internal virtual work obtained from an infinitesimal and equilibrated variation of the stress components is equal to the external complementary virtual work.
  • The PCVW, if verified for \(\forall \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}\), is identical to the Compatibility conditions on \(\Omega\) and \(S_u\). This means that if \(\delta W_i^* = \delta W_{ext}^*\) then the compatibility conditions are fulfilled.

3.5 Compatibility from PCVW using Lagrange Multipliers

As done for the PVW, it is instructive to illustrate that the compatibility conditions can be derived from the PCVW.

In other words, what happens if the PCVW is required to hold for any arbitrary set of admissible virtual variations?

\[\int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \hat{\underline{u}} \, dS_u\]

To account for the fact that the variations are in equilibrium, the variational statement can be modified by introducing those conditions via Lagrange multipliers:

\[\delta W_i^* = \delta W_{ext}^* - \int_V \underline{\lambda} \cdot (\text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \delta \underline{f}) \, dV + \int_{S_F} \underline{\mu} \cdot (\delta \underline{t} - \delta \hat{\underline{t}}) \, dS_F\]

where \(\underline{\lambda}\), \(\underline{\mu}\) are vectors of Lagrange multipliers.

Note: It is clear that the expression of the PCVW is unchanged whenever \(\text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \delta \underline{f} = 0\) and \(\delta \underline{t} = \delta \hat{\underline{t}}\).

Rearranging the First Contribution

\[\int_V \underline{\lambda} \cdot (\text{div} \, \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \delta \underline{f}) \, dV = \int_V \left( \lambda_i \, \delta \sigma_{ik,k} + \lambda_i \, \delta f_i \right) dV\]
\[= \int_V \left[ (\lambda_i \delta \sigma_{ik})_{,k} - \lambda_{i,k} \, \delta \sigma_{ik} + \lambda_i \, \delta f_i \right] dV\]
\[= \int_V \left[ (\lambda_i \delta \sigma_{ik})_{,k} - \delta \sigma_{ik} \frac{1}{2} (\lambda_{i,k} + \lambda_{k,i}) + \lambda_i \, \delta f_i \right] dV\]
\[= \int_V \left[ \text{div}(\underline{\lambda} \cdot \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}) - \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda} + \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda}^T \right) + \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \right] dV\]
\[= \int_S \underline{\lambda} \cdot \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} \, dS - \int_V \left[ \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda} + \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda}^T \right) - \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \right] dV\]
\[= \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \, dS_u - \int_V \left[ \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda} + \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda}^T \right) - \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \right] dV\]

The expression of the PCVW (augmented using the Lagrange multipliers) reads:

\[\int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \, dV = \int_V \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \hat{\underline{u}} \, dS_u\] \[- \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \, dS_F - \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \, dS_u + \int_V \left[ \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda} + \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda}^T \right) - \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{\lambda} \right] dV\] \[+ \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \underline{\mu} \, dS_F - \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{\mu} \, dS_F\]

If this scalar equation is required to hold for any variation \(\delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}}\), then it is equivalent to the following set of equations:

\[ \begin{cases} \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda} + \text{grad} \, \underline{\lambda}^T \right) & \text{in } \Omega & (1) \\[8pt] \underline{u} = \underline{\lambda} & \text{in } \Omega & (2) \\[8pt] \underline{u} = \underline{\mu} & \text{in } S_F & (3) \\[8pt] \hat{\underline{u}} = \underline{\lambda} & \text{in } S_u & (4) \\[8pt] \underline{\lambda} = \underline{\mu} & \text{in } S_F & (5) \end{cases} \]

From conditions (2) and (4) it is clear that the Lagrange multiplier \(\underline{\lambda}\) is the displacement field \(\underline{u}\).

Thus, the equations can be expressed by replacing \(\underline{\lambda}\) with \(\underline{u}\):

Compatibility Conditions

\[ \begin{cases} \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) & \text{in } \Omega \\[8pt] \underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}} & \text{in } S_u \end{cases} \]

It can be observed, in addition, that \(\underline{\mu}\) is the displacement in \(S_F\).

4. Minimum Potential Energy Principle

4.1 Derivation

It is the energetic counterpart of the PVW. While, as seen, the PVW holds irrespective of the constitutive law, it is now introduced the assumption of hyperelastic material:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} = U_{/\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}} \qquad \left( \text{or} \quad \sigma_{ik} = U_{/\varepsilon_{ik}} \right)\]

Consider the PVW: \(\delta W_i = \delta W_e\)

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\] \[\delta W_e = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F\]

According to the assumption of hyperelasticity, the internal virtual work reads:

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : U_{/\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}} \, dV = \int_V \delta U \, dV\]

since \(U = U(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}) \Rightarrow \delta U = U_{/\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}} : \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\)

It follows that:

Minimum Potential Energy Principle

\[\delta W_i = \delta W_e \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\delta(U + V) = \delta \Pi = 0}\]

where: \(\Pi = U + V\)

  • \(U\) = strain energy
  • \(V\) = potential of external forces
  • \(\Pi\) = total potential energy

4.2 Properties

  • The Minimum Potential Energy Principle is restricted to the case of hyperelastic materials (not necessarily linear).
  • Similarly to the PVW it is a variational tool for imposing the equilibrium conditions. The equilibrium conditions are obtained in terms of displacements as far as the constitutive equation is introduced.
  • Contrarily, the equations obtained via PVW, still representing the equilibrium, are obtained in terms of forces (as no use is made of the constitutive equation).

4.3 Example: Axially Loaded Bar

Simple Example: Cantilever with Distributed Axial Load

PVW Approach

Internal virtual work:

\[\delta W_i = \int_0^l \delta u_{,x} N_x \, dx\]

External virtual work:

\[\delta W_e = \int_0^l \delta u \, \hat{n} \, dx\]

\(\delta W_i = \delta W_e\) leads to:

\[\boxed{N_{x,x} + \hat{n} = 0}\]

(equilibrium in terms of forces)

Energy Approach

Strain energy:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^l EA \, u_{,x}^2 \, dx\]

Potential of external forces:

\[V = \int_0^l \hat{n} \, u \, dx\]

\(\delta(U + V) = 0\) leads to:

\[\boxed{EA \, u_{,xx} + \hat{n} = 0}\]

(equilibrium in terms of displacements)

5. Minimum Complementary Potential Energy Principle (Menabrea's Theorem)

5.1 Derivation

It is the energy counterpart of the PCVW. Thus, by assuming that the strains can be derived as:

\[\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = U^*_{/\underline{\underline{\sigma}}} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \varepsilon_{ik} = U^*_{/\sigma_{ik}}\]

with \(U^*\) complementary energy density.

And recalling the PCVW:

\[\delta W_i^* = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \, dV = \int_V \delta U^* \, dV = \delta U^*\] \[\delta W_e^* = \int_V \delta \underline{f} \cdot \underline{u} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \hat{\underline{t}} \cdot \underline{u} \, dS_F + \int_{S_u} \delta \underline{t} \cdot \hat{\underline{u}} \, dS_u = \delta V^*\]

Menabrea's Theorem

\[\delta W_i^* = \delta W_e^* \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\delta(U^* + V^*) = \delta \Pi^* = 0}\]
  • \(U^*\) = complementary strain energy
  • \(V^*\) = complementary potential of external effects
  • \(\Pi^*\) = total complementary potential energy

6. Hamilton's Principle

6.1 Setup

Consider the displacement field \(\underline{u} = \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t)\)

and consider a virtual variation \(\delta \underline{u}\) such that:

\[ \begin{aligned} \delta \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t) &= \underline{0} & \text{in } S_u, \, \forall t \\ \delta \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t_1) &= \delta \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t_2) = 0 & \text{in } \Omega, \, \forall \underline{x} \end{aligned} \]
  • The first condition is identical to the compatibility requirement used in the context of the PVW.
  • The second condition states that the perturbed configuration at \(t = t_1\), and \(t = t_2\) is equal to the actual configuration \(\underline{u}\): \[\underline{u} + \delta \underline{u} \rightarrow = \underline{u} \qquad \text{in} \quad t = t_1, t_2\]

6.2 PVW with Inertial Forces

Consider now the PVW:

\[\delta W_i = \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV\] \[\delta W_e = \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F - \underbrace{\int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \rho \ddot{\underline{u}} \, dV}_{\text{Contribution of the inertial forces}}\]
Note: The inertial forces are naturally expressed as function of the displacements. This is why the variational approach is formulated starting from the PVW.

6.3 Time Integration

Integrate now between \(t_1\) and \(t_2\):

\[\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV \right] dt = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left[ \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV + \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F - \int_V \rho \, \delta \underline{u} \cdot \ddot{\underline{u}} \, dV \right] dt\]

The last contribution can be integrated by parts:

\[\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \int_V \rho \, \delta \underline{u} \cdot \ddot{\underline{u}} \, dV = - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \int_V \rho \, \delta \dot{\underline{u}} \cdot \dot{\underline{u}} \, dV \, dt + \left. \int_V \rho \, \delta \underline{u} \cdot \dot{\underline{u}} \, dV \right|_{t_1}^{t_2}\]
\[= - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \int_V \rho \, \delta \dot{\underline{u}} \cdot \dot{\underline{u}} \, dV \, dt\]

(since \(\delta \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t_1) = 0\) and \(\delta \underline{u}(\underline{x}, t_2) = 0\))

Then:

\[\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left[ \int_V \delta \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} : \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \, dV - \int_V \delta \underline{u} \cdot \underline{f} \, dV - \int_{S_F} \delta \underline{u} \cdot \hat{\underline{t}} \, dS_F - \int_V \rho \, \delta \dot{\underline{u}} \cdot \dot{\underline{u}} \, dV \right] dt = 0\] \[\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \delta(U + V - K) \, dt = 0\]

6.4 Hamilton's Principle - Final Form

Hamilton's Principle

\[\boxed{\delta \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L \, dt = 0 \qquad \text{with} \quad L = K - (U + V)}\]
  • \(L\) = Lagrangian = Kinetic energy minus Total potential energy
  • \(K\) = kinetic energy = \(\frac{1}{2} \int_V \rho \, \dot{\underline{u}} \cdot \dot{\underline{u}} \, dV\)
Physical Interpretation: The action integral \(\int_{t_1}^{t_2} L \, dt\) is stationary for the true motion. This is the fundamental principle for dynamics and serves as the basis for Lagrangian mechanics.
