Strain Energy and Complementary Strain Energy in Beams

PCVW/Menabrea's Theorem for Evaluating Displacements and Solving Statically Undetermined Beam Systems

1. Strain Energy and Complementary Strain Energy in Beams

The DSV solution allows to express the strain energy and the complementary one as function of the internal actions.

Fundamental Definitions

Recall that:

\(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} = U_{/\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}}\) and

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \underline{\underline{\sigma}}(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}) : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\]

Strain energy density

\(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = U^*_{/\underline{\underline{\sigma}}}\) and

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}(\underline{\underline{\sigma}})\]

Complementary strain energy density

where \(U^* = \underline{\underline{\sigma}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} - U\)

Important Notes:
  • Note that \(U\) has to be intended as function of \(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\) while \(U^*\) as function of \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}}\).
  • In the linear case \(U = U^*\)
  • Recall that \(U\) is used in the context of displacement based approaches (to impose equilibrium via Minimum potential energy principle); \(U^*\) is used in force-based approaches to impose compatibility (via Menabrea's theorem)

2. Strain Energy (Axial Force and Bending)

The strain energy is now specialized to case of axial force and bending, so:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma_{zz} \varepsilon_{zz} + \sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz} + \sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz} \right)\]

Note: neglect for now the contributions \(\sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz}\) and \(\sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz}\)

The two contributions \(\sigma_{xz} \gamma_{xz}\) and \(\sigma_{yz} \gamma_{yz}\) are now neglected by assuming that the beam is slender (axial force and/or bending moment, constant or linear, are the only internal actions; no twisting moment.)

DSV Solution

The DSV solution was found to be:

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x\]

and

\[\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E} = \frac{T_z}{EA} + \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} x\]
Note: For the case of homogeneous and isotropic beam the contributions \(EA\) and \(EJ_{\alpha\alpha}\) are the product between the elastic modulus and the area or the second moment of inertia, respectively. However, it is more appropriate to intend \(EA\) as a single contribution expressing the axial stiffness of the beam. Similarly \(EJ_{\alpha\alpha}\) should be intended as the bending stiffness, not necessarily the product between \(E\) and \(J_{\alpha\alpha}\).

3. Generalization for Non-Homogeneous Beams

This generalization of the idea of axial and bending stiffness is useful when dealing with non-homogeneous beams. Consider, for instance, a beam made of two materials.

Given:

  • Upper region: Material A with elastic modulus \(E_A\)
  • Lower region: Material B with elastic modulus \(E_B\)
  • \(A_A\) and \(A_B\): corresponding areas
  • Clearly \(A_A + A_B = A\), where \(A\) is the section area

Derivation of Axial Stiffness

Question: Which is the axial stiffness of the area?

\[EA = \int_A E \, dA \quad \text{where } E = E(x, y)\]

In this example:

  • \(E = E_A\) if \(y > 0\)
  • \(E = E_B\) if \(y < 0\)
\[EA = E_A A_A + E_B A_B\]

Physical Interpretation

It is clear that the axial stiffness cannot be felt as the product between an elastic modulus (which one? \(E_A\) or \(E_B\)?) and an area (\(A_A\) or \(A_B\)?)

The symbol \(EA\) stands for the axial stiffness and highlights the dependence on the elastic properties and the area of the section.

In this example it could be possible to write:

\[EA = E_A \left( A_A + \frac{E_B}{E_A} A_B \right) = E_A \cdot \tilde{A}\]

with

\[\tilde{A} = A_A + \frac{E_B}{E_A} A_B\]
Note: In any case, \(\tilde{A}\) depends on the elastic properties thus a description as the product between \(E\) and \(A\) is no more possible. The same considerations hold for the bending stiffness (and, even more, for the torsional one).

4. Strain Energy Expression from DSV Solution

From the DSV solution the strain energy reads:

Derivation

Step 1: Volume integral formulation

\[U = \int_V U \, dV = \frac{1}{2} \int_V \sigma_{zz} \varepsilon_{zz} \, dV\] \[= \frac{1}{2} \int_V \left( \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x \right) \left( \frac{T_z}{EA} + \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} y - \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} x \right) dV\]

Step 2: Expansion

\[= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \int_A \left( \frac{T_z}{A} \frac{T_z}{EA} + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} y^2 + \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} x^2 + \right.\] \[\left. + \frac{T_z}{A} \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} y - \frac{T_z}{A} \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} x + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} \frac{T_z}{EA} y - \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} x + \right.\] \[\left. - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} \frac{T_z}{EA} x - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} xy \right) dA \, dz\]

Step 3: Apply centroidal axis properties

Recalling that for principal centroidal axes:

\[\int_A x \, dA = \int_A y \, dA = \int_A xy \, dA = 0\]

it follows that:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( \frac{T_z \, T_z}{EA} + M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz\]

Constitutive Relations

From the DSV solution for axial and bending moments it is known that:

\[\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{EA} \quad \text{and} \quad \kappa_x = \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} \quad ; \quad \kappa_y = \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}}\]

where \(\kappa_x, \kappa_y\) are curvatures.

5. Alternative Forms of Strain Energy

The strain energy \(U\) is then written as:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( EA \, \varepsilon_{zz}^2 + EJ_{xx} \, \kappa_x^2 + EJ_{yy} \, \kappa_y^2 \right) dz\]

having highlighted the displacement-related components \(\varepsilon_{zz}\) and \(\kappa_{\alpha\alpha}\) as far as the strain energy is intended as \(U = U(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}})\)

Remarks

Alternative Expression

Alternatively \(U\) can be written as:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( T_z \, \varepsilon_{zz} + M_x \, \kappa_x + M_y \, \kappa_y \right) dz\]

The quantities multiplied in the expression are energetically-conjugated. The axial force is energy conjugated with the deformation \(\varepsilon_{zz}\), while the bending moments \(M_\alpha\) are energy conjugated with the curvature \(\kappa_\alpha\).

Generalized Displacements

For this reason the quantities \(\varepsilon_{zz}\) and \(\kappa_\alpha\) can be referred to as generalized displacements.

Note: The expression in the box does not account for the shear contribution. For slender beams this contribution is, in fact, negligible. Whenever a torsional rigidity is accounted for, the torsion must be included.

Alternative Form with Derivatives

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( EA \, W_{/z}^2 + EJ_{xx} \, V_{/zz}^2 + EJ_{yy} \, U_{/zz}^2 \right) dz\]

6. Complementary Strain Energy (Axial Force and Bending)

The expression of the complementary strain energy is already available from the strain energy \(U\).

Indeed, for a linear hyperelastic material, \(U = U^*\)

(however, it is important to remark, one more time, that \(U\) and \(U^*\) are associated with different physical meanings. \(U^*\) is the Legendre transform of \(U\) and has to be intended as a scalar function which depends on the stresses).

Result

It was found that:

\[U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz\]

This expression is already written as function of the stress-related quantities \(T_z\), \(M_x\) and \(M_y\). Thus:

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz\]

where the internal actions \(T_z\), \(M_x\) and \(M_y\) are denoted as generalized forces.

7. PCVW / Menabrea's Theorem for Evaluating Displacements and Solving Statically Undetermined Beam Systems

The variational tools previously developed are now applied to analyze beams and systems of beams. With regard to:

  1. Evaluation of displacements/rotations in parts of the structure
  2. Solution of statically undetermined problems

These two set of problems are solved in the context of force-based approaches (⇒ PCVW and Menabrea's th are used).

Remark: Force-based approaches are particularly useful for solving relatively simple problems, where hand-like calculations can be performed. While it is possible to extend the use of force-based approaches even for problems with several dofs, in these cases it is generally simpler to deal with displacement-based approaches; their implementation in computer problems is, in fact, substantially easier.

Within the context of linear hyperelastic materials the use of PCVW and Menabrea's th. is interchangeable.

\[\delta \Pi^* = \delta (U^* + V^*) = 0\] \[= \delta \left( \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\ell \left( T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz + F_i \hat{U}_i + M_j \hat{\theta}_j + \hat{F}_r U_r + \hat{M}_s \theta_s \right) = 0\]

or, similarly:

\[\delta W_i^* = \delta W_e^*\] \[\int_0^\ell \left( \delta T_z \frac{T_z}{EA} + \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}} + \delta M_y \frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}} \right) dz = \delta F_i \hat{U}_i + \delta M_j \hat{\theta}_j + \delta \hat{F}_r U_r + \delta M_s \hat{\theta}_s\]

where the left side represents imposed displ./rotations and the right side represents variation of forces/moments.

8. Solution of Statically Undetermined Structures

Procedure Summary:

  1. Remove the overconstraints and replace them with the reacting force
  2. Impose equilibrium conditions
  3. Apply PCVW/Menabrea's th. to obtain, among the class of equilibrium solutions, the one which is also compatible.

Equilibrium conditions are imposed in system (B) where everything is expressed as function of the unknown reaction \(X\).

The generalized deformations can be obtained as \(\frac{T_z}{EA}\), \(\frac{M_x}{EJ_{xx}}\) and \(\frac{M_y}{EJ_{yy}}\)

Application of Variational Principle

The variational principle is then imposed after evaluating the variations \(\delta T_z\), \(\delta M_x\) and \(\delta M_y\) in a properly chosen "dummy system".

Important Notes:
  1. The dummy system should be taken such that the external virtual work can be easily expressed.
  2. The variations \(\delta T_z\), \(\delta M_x\) and \(\delta M_y\) are required to be in equilibrium; compatibility is not a constraint! Any choice of the variations is fine, provided they are in equilibrium.
  3. For a statically determined problem (say a cantilever beam) the internal actions are readily available from equilibrium considerations. Compatibility is intrinsically satisfied.
  4. The evaluation of the unknown reactions (in this example \(X\)) requires the application of a variational principle, whose expression is function of the stiffnesses of the beam. In turn, the stiffnesses reaction forces are function of the internal load paths, or, in other words, they depend upon the internal load paths.

9. Example: Simply Supported Beam with Point Load

Given:

  • Beam length: \(2a\)
  • Point load \(P\) at distance \(a\) from left support
  • Bending stiffness: \(EJ\)
  • Unknown reaction at right support: \(X\)

Solution

Step 1: Internal Moment Expressions

For \(0 \leq z \leq a\):

\[M_x = -Xz\]

For \(a \leq z \leq 2a\):

\[M_x = -Xz + P(z-a)\]

Step 2: Real Deformations (Curvatures)

For \(0 \leq z \leq a\):

\[\frac{M_x}{EJ} = -\frac{Xz}{EJ}\]

For \(a \leq z \leq 2a\):

\[\frac{M_x}{EJ} = \frac{P(z-a) - Xz}{EJ}\]

Step 3: Apply PCVW

\[\int_0^{2a} \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ} dz = \delta X \hat{V}\]

where \(\hat{V} = 0\) (imposed displacement at \(z=0\))

The dummy system gives: \(\delta M_x = -z\) (equilibrated variation)

Step 4: Evaluate the Integral

\[\int_0^{2a} \delta M_x \frac{M_x}{EJ} dz = \int_0^a \frac{Xz^2}{EJ} dz + \int_a^{2a} \frac{-(P-X)z^2 + Paz}{EJ} dz\] \[= \frac{1}{EJ} \left( \frac{1}{3} X a^3 - \frac{1}{3} (P-X) 7a^3 + P \frac{3}{2} a^3 \right) = 0\]
Final Answer: \[X = \frac{5}{16} P\]

10. Example: Beam with Variable Stiffness

Given:

  • Left portion: stiffness \(EJ_2\), length \(a\)
  • Right portion: stiffness \(EJ_1\), length \(a\)
  • Point load \(P\) at junction
  • Unknown reaction: \(X\)

The internal actions, as long as the dummy system, are readily available from the previous example.

Solution

Step 1: Apply PCVW with Variable Stiffness

\[\int_0^a \frac{Xz^2}{EJ_1} dz + \int_a^{2a} \frac{(P-X)z^2 - Paz}{EJ_2} dz = 0\]

Step 2: Evaluate the Integrals

\[\frac{1}{EJ_1} \frac{a^3}{3} X + \frac{1}{EJ_2} \frac{7a^3}{3} X - \frac{1}{EJ_2} \frac{5}{6} Pa^3 = 0\] \[\left( \frac{\alpha}{3} + \frac{7}{3} \right) X = \frac{5}{6} P \quad \text{with } \alpha = \frac{EJ_2}{EJ_1}\]
Final Answer: \[X = \frac{5}{2} \frac{P}{\alpha + 7}\]
Physical Interpretation:

Note that \(X\) depends on the stiffnesses \(EJ_1\) and \(EJ_2\).

For increasing values of \(\alpha\), the value of \(X\) is progressively reduced. The internal load path is in fact modified and higher values of \(EJ_2\) tend to unload the beam of stiffness \(EJ_1\).

The load flows where the structure is stiffer.

11. Extensions of Beam Model

For several cases of practical interest, it is necessary to relax the hypotheses involved in the derivation of the DSV solution. In particular the DSV solutions will be assumed to be a good approximation when:

  1. The beam is slightly curved
  2. The beam is slightly twisted
  3. The beam is slightly tapered
  4. The load are applied not only at the ends of the beam, but they are introduced also at the outer surface \(\delta F_{body}\) (concentrated forces can be seen as Dirac's functions)

Observations

  1. For those cases the DSV solution is an approximation
  2. The higher the violation of the hypothesis, the higher the level of approximation.
  3. The application of the DSV results is reasonable provided the region of interest is not drastically affected by local effects.
Note on Local Effects: Local effects could be relevant at load application points. If an accurate description of the internal stress is sought, a 3D analysis is needed (accordingly the idealization of the load as a concentrated force could be questionable).

12. Sign Conventions

Having extended the analysis range to the case of distributed loads, it is useful to define sign conventions and corresponding differential relations.

  • Forces > 0 if directed along coordinate axis
  • Moments > 0 according to right hand rule

Differential Relations

zy Plane

Differential equations:

\[dT_y = -q \, dz\] \[dM_x = T_y \, dz\]

Integrated forms:

\[T_y(z) = -\int_0^z q(\xi) \, d\xi + T_y(0)\] \[M_x(z) = \int_0^z T_y(\xi) \, d\xi + M_x(0)\]

zx Plane

Differential equations:

\[dT_x = -q \, dz\] \[dM_y = -T_x \, dz\]

Integrated forms:

\[T_x(z) = -\int_0^z q(\xi) \, d\xi + T_x(0)\] \[M_y(z) = -\int_0^z T_x(\xi) \, d\xi + M_y(0)\]

Distributed Twisting Moment

Whenever a distributed twisting moment is considered:

\[dM_z = -m_z \, dz\]
\[M_z(z) = -\int_0^z m_z(\xi) \, d\xi + M_z(0)\]

13. Example: Cantilever Beam with Linearly Distributed Load

Consider a cantilever beam with a linearly distributed load. Evaluate the internal actions at \(z = \ell/2\). To verify the independence on the choice of the reference system.

Given:

  • Cantilever beam of length \(\ell\)
  • Fixed support at left end
  • Case A: \(q(z) = q_0 \frac{z}{\ell}\) (load increases from fixed end)
  • Case B: \(q(z) = q_0 (1 - z/\ell)\) (load decreases from fixed end)

Case A: \(q(z) = q_0 \frac{z}{\ell}\)

Step 1: Shear Force

\[T_y(z) = -\int_0^z q(\xi) \, d\xi\] \[= -\int_0^z q_0 \frac{\xi}{\ell} \, d\xi = -\frac{q_0 z^2}{2\ell}\]

Step 2: Bending Moment

\[M_x(z) = \int_0^z T_y(\xi) \, d\xi + M_x(0)\] \[= -\int_0^z \frac{q_0 \xi^2}{2\ell} \, d\xi = -\frac{q_0 z^3}{6\ell}\]
Results at \(z = \ell/2\): \[T_y(\ell/2) = -\frac{q_0 \ell}{8}\] \[M_x(\ell/2) = -\frac{q_0 \ell^2}{48}\]

14. Case B: \(q(z) = q_0 (1 - z/\ell)\)

Reaction Forces:

  • \(V = -q_0 \ell/2\)
  • \(M = q_0 \ell^2/6\)

Boundary Conditions:

  • \(T_y(0) = q_0 \ell/2\)
  • \(M_x(0) = -q_0 \ell^2/6\)

Solution

Step 1: Shear Force

\[T_y(z) = -\int_0^z q_0 (1 - \xi/\ell) \, d\xi + T_y(0)\] \[= -q_0 z + q_0 \frac{z^2}{2\ell} + q_0 \ell/2\]

Step 2: Bending Moment

\[M_x(z) = \int_0^z T_y(\xi) \, d\xi + M_x(0)\] \[= -q_0 \frac{z^2}{2} + q_0 \frac{z^3}{6\ell} + q_0 \frac{\ell}{2} z - q_0 \frac{\ell^2}{6}\]
Results at \(z = \ell/2\): \[T_y(\ell/2) = \frac{q_0 \ell}{8}\] \[M_x(\ell/2) = -\frac{q_0 \ell^2}{48}\]
Key Observations:
  • If the differential relations between the internal forces are applied, the evaluation of the internal forces is straightforward.
  • It is not a surprise to verify that the internal actions do not depend on the reference system.
