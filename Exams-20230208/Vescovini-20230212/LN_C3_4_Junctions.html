Junctions in Aerospace Structures

Analysis of Discontinuous and Continuous Junction Techniques

1. Introduction to Junctions

The various components of a typical light-weight structure – panels, stringers, longerons, ribs and frames – are connected each other by means of junctions.

A common classification divides the junctions into:

  1. Continuous junctions (welding, bonding)
  2. Discontinuous junctions (bolts, rivets)

2. Discontinuous Junctions

Discontinuous junctions are widely used both in metal and composite structures (although their uses are particularly affected by the interruption of the fibers).

In the aerospace field, the rivet is a widely used kind of discontinuous connection.

The rivet is inserted and then, by compression, it is created the second "head" of the rivet.

Fatigue Considerations

Fatigue problems (potential issues associated with repeated cycles of loads) may affect the region close to the hole, where the concentration of stresses can determine the onset and propagation of cracks.

With this regard, a useful strategy consists in adopting a rivet with a diameter slightly larger in comparison to the hole's diameter. The result is a state of internal compression which mitigates the stress concentration around the hole and improve the fatigue life of the part.

Bolted Connections

Another common solution is constituted by bolts.

Bolts are generally used:

  1. When it is necessary to guarantee the possibility of disassembling the part
  2. In those areas characterized by high traction loads, where fatigue is a crucial issue (e.g. the bottom of a wing structure)

At the same time, it should be highlighted that they are a relatively heavy junction and, in general, do not offer good performance when loaded in shear.

3. Comparison of Rivets and Bolts

Overall, the underlying load transfer mechanisms in rivets and bolts is inherently different.

Indeed rivets work mainly in shear, whereas bolts are associated with an axial load transfer mechanism.

The two solutions can be compared by illustrating, in a simplified manner, the loads transmitted by the junction itself:

Rivet

\[\tau_y = \frac{\sigma_y}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{T_{max}^{rivet}}{A}\] \[\Rightarrow T_{max}^{rivet} = \frac{\sigma_y A}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.58 \sigma_y A\]

The rivet transfers the shear load working in shear. The maximum stress \(\tau\) can be taken as the yielding stress \(\tau_y\) which, according to von Mises is \(\sigma_y/\sqrt{3}\).

Bolt

\[T_{max}^{bolt} = \mu F_{max} \text{ with } F_{max} = \sigma_y A\] \[T_{max}^{bolt} = \mu \sigma_y A \quad \mu \approx 0.1\]

The bolt transfers load via friction, leading to a shear force obtained by application of Coulomb's law for friction, where \(\mu\) is the coefficient for friction.

Conclusion

It is then clear that, for a given amount of area:

\[T_{max}^{rivet} = 0.58 \sigma_y A > T_{max}^{bolt} = 0.10 \sigma_y A\]

Thus the rivets can transfer a higher shear force per unit of area (and of weight).

4. Types of Junctions and Their Modeling

The junctions may regard the connection of:

Longitudinal Junctions

  • Panel – panel
  • Panel – stringer

Transversal Junctions

  • Panel – rib
  • Panel – frame

Junction Idealization

Within the modeling assumptions here introduced, the junctions are represented as continuous lines responsible for the load transfer between the two connected parts.

The conceptual representation of the junction can refer to the semi-monocoque description based on lumped areas, thus facilitating the understanding of how the shear flows run inside the structure and the junction.

Example: Single Junction

For a T-junction with shear flows \(q_1\), \(q_2\), and junction flow \(q_G\):

\[q_2 = q_1 + q_G\]

Example: Multiple Junctions

For a cruciform junction:

  • \(q_{G1} = q_1\)
  • \(q_{G2} = q_2\)
  • \(q_{G3} = q_3\)

5. Evaluation of Forces Over Junction Elements

Consider the case of a stringer connected to a panel by means of one single row of rivets.

By separating the two portions of panel and isolating the junction line, it is obtained:

The flow along the junction \(q_G\) can then be seen as:

\[q_G = q_1 - q_2\]

(Note: this is an equilibrium condition)

Force Transmitted by Each Rivet

To evaluate the amount of shear force transmitted by each single rivet, define:

  • \(N =\) number of rivets
  • \(p =\) pitch of rivets
  • \(F =\) force transmitted by single rivet

Derivation

\[q_G \ell = NF\]

but \(N = \ell/p\)

So:

\[q_G \ell = \frac{\ell}{p} F\]
\[F = p \cdot q_G\]

This result illustrates that each rivet is responsible for the transfer of the shear flow associated with the region of width \(p\) (half-pitch on each side):

\[F = (p/2 + p/2) q_G\]

6. Analysis of Panel-Panel Connection with 1 Row of Rivets

In this simple case:

\[q_1 = q_2 = q_G = q\]

Known the shear flow \(q\) (which is equal to \(q_1\) and \(q_2\)), the value of \(q_G\) is readily available as \(q_G = q\).

The force transmitted by the rivet will be determined as:

\[F = qp\]

7. Analysis of Panel-Panel Connection with 2 Rows of Rivets

Consider two panels with thicknesses \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) connected by two rows of rivets with spacing \(b\).

Trivial Solution (Equal Thickness)

The solution of the problem is trivial if the two panels have equal thickness \(t_1 = t_2\). Indeed by symmetry: \(q_1 = q_2\) and, as for \(q_1 + q_2 = q\)

it follows that:

\[q_1 = q_2 = \frac{q}{2}\]

Non-Trivial Solution (Unequal Thickness)

The solution is not trivial if \(t_1 \neq t_2\) and has to be calculated.

The problem is formulated within the framework of the force-based approach.

Equilibrium Condition

\[q_1 + q_2 = q\]

It follows that the problem is statically indeterminate as the evaluation of the two unknowns \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) requires an additional equation, which cannot be obtained from the equilibrium requirements.

As usual, the additional condition should represent the compatibility and, as such, can be obtained using the PCVW or Menabrea's theorem.

Solution Using Menabrea's Theorem

Step 1: Total Complementary Energy

\[U^* = \sum_{i=1}^{2} \left( U^*_{panel_i} + U^*_{rivet_i} \right)\]

Step 2: Strain Energy Density

The density of strain energy reads:

\[u^*_{panel} = \frac{1}{2} \tau_i \gamma_i = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_i}{t} \frac{q_i}{Gt} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_i^2}{Gt^2}\]

Step 3: Panel Contribution (Repeating Unit)

The strain energy of the generic panel of the repeating unit is:

\[U^*_{panel, unit} = \int_V u^* \, dV = \int_V \frac{1}{2} \frac{q^2}{Gt^2} \, dV = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q^2}{Gt^2} \cdot b \cdot t \cdot p\]
\[U^*_{panel, unit} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q^2 bp}{Gt}\]

Step 4: Rivet Contribution

The contribution associated with the rivets deformability can be determined as:

\[U^*_{rivet} = \frac{1}{2} k u^2 \quad \text{but} \quad u = \frac{F}{k}\] \[= \frac{1}{2} \frac{F^2}{k}\]

where \(F =\) shear force transmitted by the rivet, \(k =\) rivet stiffness

Recalling that the force transmitted by a rivet is \(F = q_G \cdot p\), it follows that:

\[U^*_{rivet} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_G^2 p^2}{k}\]

Step 5: Total Energy Expression

\[U^* = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} \frac{q_1^2 bp}{Gt} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_2^2 bp}{Gt}}_{\text{panels' contrib.}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2} \frac{q_1^2 p^2}{k} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_2^2 p^2}{k}}_{\text{rivets' contrib.}} + U^*(q)\]
Note: The contribution \(U^*(q)\) is due to the outer portions of the structure. This contribution does not depend on \(q_1\) and \(q_2\). Thus it is not contributing to the compatibility conditions.

Step 6: Express Energy as Function of \(q_1\) Only

Recalling the equilibrium condition \(q = q_2 + q_1\), or \(q_2 = q - q_1\):

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_1^2 bp}{Gt_1} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{(q_1^2 - 2qq_1 + q^2)}{Gt_2} bp + \frac{1}{2} \frac{q_1^2 p^2}{k} + \frac{1}{2} \frac{(q_1^2 - 2qq_1 + q^2)p^2}{k} + U^*(q)\]

Step 7: Define Nondimensional Parameters

\[\alpha = \frac{Gt_1 p}{kb} \qquad \beta = \frac{t_1}{t_2}\]

\(\alpha\) is a ratio between the stiffness of the panel and the stiffness of the rivet

The energy becomes:

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \frac{bp}{Gt_1} \left( q_1^2 + \beta(q_1^2 - 2qq_1) \right) + \frac{1}{2} \frac{bp}{Gt_1} \left( \alpha(2q_1^2 - 2qq_1) \right) + U^*(q)\]

Step 8: Apply Stationarity Condition

\[\frac{\partial U^*}{\partial q_1} = 0\] \[\frac{\partial U^*}{\partial q_1}: \quad q_1 + \beta(q_1 - q) + 2q_1\alpha - q\alpha = 0\] \[q_1(1 + \beta + 2\alpha) = q(\alpha + \beta)\]
Solution: \[q_1 = \frac{\alpha + \beta}{1 + \beta + 2\alpha} q\]

Verification: Equal Thickness Case

Clearly if \(t_1 = t_2\), i.e. \(\beta = 1\), the shear flow is evenly distributed between the two rivets:

\[q_1 = \frac{\alpha + 1}{2\alpha + 2} q = \frac{1}{2} q\]

Whenever \(t_1 \neq t_2\) the shear flow is unevenly distributed between the two rivets. In particular, higher values of \(\beta\) \((t_2 > t_1)\) will lead to \(q_2 > q_1\).

8. Analysis of Panel-Panel Connection with 3 Rows of Rivets

The analysis can be easily extended to consider the case of a junction with 3 lines of rivets. For simplicity the analysis is here restricted to the case of panels of equal thickness.

From symmetry and recalling that \(q_3 = q - q_1\), the schematic can be simplified.

Solution

Equilibrium Conditions

\[\begin{cases} q_1 + q_3 = q \\ q_2 + q_1 = q_3 \end{cases}\]

3 unknowns \((q_i: i = 1, 2, 3)\), 2 equations

The third equation is available by invoking the compatibility of the solution.

Complementary Energy Expression

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \underbrace{\frac{(2q_1^2 + 2q_3^2)bp}{Gt}}_{\text{Contribution of four panels}} + \frac{1}{2} \underbrace{\frac{2q_1^2 p^2}{k}}_{\text{Contrib. of two outer rivets}} + \frac{1}{2} \underbrace{\frac{q_2^2 p^2}{k}}_{\text{Contrib. of middle rivet}} + U^*(q)\]

Express as Function of \(q_1\)

Using the equilibrium conditions:

\[q_3 = q - q_1\] \[q_2 = q_3 - q_1 = q - 2q_1\]

Apply Stationarity

The expression of \(U^*\), after a few algebraic manipulations, is:

\[U^* = \frac{bp}{Gt} \left[ (2 + 3\alpha) q_1^2 - (2q + 2\alpha q) q_1 \right] + U^*(q)\]

And imposing \(\frac{\partial U^*}{\partial q_1} = 0\):

\[\frac{\partial U^*}{\partial q_1} = 2(2 + 3\alpha)q_1 - 2q(1 + \alpha) = 0\]
Solution: \[q_1 = \frac{1 + \alpha}{2 + 3\alpha} q\]

Comments on the Result

\(\alpha\) is defined as \(\alpha = \frac{Gt_1 p}{kb}\)

  • \(\alpha \rightarrow +\infty\) means that the panel is much stiffer than the rivet
  • \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\) means that the rivet is much stiffer than the panel (situation closer to typical values)

Case: \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\)

\[q_1 = \frac{1}{2} q, \quad q_2 = 0, \quad q_3 = \frac{1}{2} q\]

Whenever the rivet is much stiffer than the panel, the central row of rivets is unloaded and so unuseful.

Important: This situation is typically close to common aerospace values and explains why using a triple row of rivets is not a good practice.

Case: \(\alpha \rightarrow +\infty\)

\[q_1 = \frac{1}{3} q, \quad q_2 = \frac{1}{3} q, \quad q_3 = \frac{1}{3} q\]

All rivets are equally loaded.

Intermediate Cases

\[\frac{1}{3}q \leq q_1 \leq \frac{1}{2}q\] \[0 \leq q_2 \leq \frac{1}{3}q\] \[\frac{1}{3}q \leq q_3 \leq \frac{1}{2}q\]

9. Bonding (Continuous Junctions)

A continuous junction technique, which is commonly used in the aerospace field, is the bonding.

Often bonding makes use of thin films of pre-polymerized material, reinforced with glass fiber.

It is a cheap technology, offering the additional advantage of guaranteeing isolation with outside.

For metallic materials, the typical thickness of the bonding layers is of the order of \(1/10\) mm.

In the presence of a bonding line the load transfer is continuous, as opposed to the case of discrete junctions, where the load transfer happens in discrete locations.

10. Differential Problem for Bonded Junctions

It follows that the problem of evaluating the shear flow transfer is a differential problem and not an algebraic one as in the case of discrete junctions.

The shear flows \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are now functions of the position \(x\), meaning that the unknowns are no more scalars but functions.

At any section of the structure the equilibrium is satisfied, meaning that:

\[q_1(x) + q_2(x) = q \quad \text{(equilibrium)}\]

This can be easily seen by considering an arbitrary path \(\Pi\). The shear flow equation states that: \(q_2 + q_1 = q\)

Differential Element Analysis

Equilibrium of the Upper Panel

\[(q_2 + dq_2) dz - q_2 dz - \tau_g dx \, dz = 0\] \[dq_2 = \tau_g dx\]
\[q_{2,x} = \tau_g\]

Equilibrium of the Lower Panel

\[(q_1 + dq_1) dz - q_1 dz + \tau_g dx \, dz = 0\]
\[q_{1,x} = -\tau_g\]

11. Energy Formulation for Bonded Junctions

The problem is formulated, as done for the rivets, by referring to Menabrea's theorem for imposing the compatibility.

Derivation of Governing Equation

Step 1: Total Complementary Energy

\[U^* = U^*_{panels} + U^*_{glue}\]

Step 2: Panel Contribution

\[U^*_{panels} = \frac{1}{2} \int_V \frac{q^2}{Gt^2} dV = \frac{1}{2} \int_\ell \int_{-b}^{b} \frac{q^2}{Gt} dx \, dz\]

Step 3: Glue Contribution

\[U^*_{glue} = \frac{1}{2} \int_V \tau_g \gamma_g dV = \frac{1}{2} \int_V \frac{\tau_g^2}{G_g} dV = \frac{1}{2} \int_\ell \int_{-b}^{b} \frac{\tau_g^2}{G_g} t_g \, dx \, dz\]

where \(G_g\) is the glue's shear modulus

Step 4: Combined Expression

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \int_\ell \int_{-b}^{b} \left( \frac{q_1^2 + q_2^2}{Gt} + \frac{\tau_g^2 \cdot t_g}{G_g} \right) dx \, dz + U^*(q)\]

The problem does not depend on \(z\), so it can be taken \(dz = 1\):

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-b}^{b} \left( \frac{q_1^2 + q_2^2}{Gt} + \frac{\tau_g^2 t_g}{G_g} \right) dx + U^*(q)\]

Step 5: Introduce Equilibrium Conditions

Express \(U^*\) as function of \(q_2\) only:

\[q_1 + q_2 = q \Rightarrow q_1 = q - q_2\] \[q_{2,x} = \tau_g\]

The strain energy is then:

\[U^* = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-b}^{b} \left( \frac{q^2 + q_2^2 - 2qq_2 + q_2^2}{Gt} + \frac{q_{2,x}^2 \cdot t_g}{G_g} \right) dx + U^*(q)\]
\[U^* = \frac{1}{Gt} \int_{-b}^{b} (q_2^2 - qq_2) dx + \frac{1}{2} \int_{-b}^{b} \frac{q_{2,x}^2 \cdot t_g}{G_g} dx + U^*(q)\]

Step 6: Apply Euler-Lagrange Equations

The functional \(U^*\) depends on \(q_2\) and \(q_{2,x}\), or \(U^* = U^*(q_2, q_{2,x})\)

The stationarity condition \(\delta U^* = 0\) can be imposed by applying the Euler-Lagrange equations:

\[\frac{\partial u^*}{\partial q_2} - \frac{d}{dx} \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial q_{2,x}} = 0\]

where \(U^* = \int_{-b}^{b} u^* dx\)

Step 7: Compute Derivatives

\[\frac{\partial u^*}{\partial q_2} = \frac{1}{Gt} (2q_2 - q)\] \[\frac{d}{dx} \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial q_{2,x}} = \frac{t_g}{G_g} q_{2,xx}\]

Step 8: Euler-Lagrange Equation

\[\frac{1}{Gt} (2q_2 - q) - \frac{t_g}{G_g} q_{2,xx} = 0\]

or:

\[-\frac{G t \cdot t_g}{G_g} q_{2,xx} + 2q_2 = q\] \[-\frac{G t_g}{2G_g} q_{2,xx} + q_2 = q/2\]

Step 9: Define Parameter

Define the parameter (dimensional):

\[d^2 = \frac{2G_g}{G t_g}\]
Governing Differential Equation: \[-\frac{1}{d^2} q_{2,xx} + q_2 = \frac{q}{2}\]

This is a non-homogeneous equation describing the compatibility requirements.

12. Solution of the Bonded Junction Problem

The solution of the problem involves:

  1. The definition of the boundary conditions
  2. The solution of the homogeneous part and the particular one

Complete Solution

Differential Problem Statement

\[\begin{cases} -\frac{1}{\alpha^2} q_{2,xx} + q_2 = q/2 \\ q_2(b) = q \\ q_2(-b) = 0 \end{cases}\]

Homogeneous Solution

\[-\frac{1}{\alpha^2} q_{2,xx} + q_2 = 0\] \[-\frac{\lambda^2}{\alpha^2} + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda^2 = \alpha^2 \Rightarrow \lambda = \pm \alpha\]
\[q_2^H = Ae^{\alpha x} + Be^{-\alpha x}\]

\(A, B\) are integration constants to evaluate by imposing BCs

Particular Solution

\[q_2^P = q/2\]

General Solution

\[q_2 = Ae^{\alpha x} + Be^{-\alpha x} + q/2\]

Apply Boundary Conditions

\[\begin{cases} q_2(b) = Ae^{\alpha b} + Be^{-\alpha b} + q/2 = q & (1) \\ q_2(-b) = Ae^{-\alpha b} + Be^{\alpha b} + q/2 = 0 & (2) \end{cases}\]

From \((1) - (2)\):

\[A(e^{\alpha b} + e^{-\alpha b}) + B(e^{\alpha b} + e^{-\alpha b}) = 0\] \[\Rightarrow B = -A\]

From (1): \(A(e^{\alpha b} - e^{-\alpha b}) = q/2\)

Recalling that:

\[\sinh \alpha = \frac{e^{\alpha} - e^{-\alpha}}{2}, \quad \cosh \alpha = \frac{e^{\alpha} + e^{-\alpha}}{2}\] \[A = \frac{q}{2(e^{\alpha b} - e^{-\alpha b})} = \frac{q}{4 \sinh(\alpha b)}\]

Final Solutions

Summarizing, the solution is obtained as:

\(q_2 = A(e^{-\alpha x} - e^{\alpha x}) + q/2 = 2A \sinh(\alpha x) + q/2\)

\[q_2 = \frac{q}{2} \frac{\sinh(\alpha x)}{\sinh(\alpha b)} + \frac{q}{2}\]

From \(q_1 = q - q_2\):

\[q_1 = -\frac{q}{2} \frac{\sinh(\alpha x)}{\sinh(\alpha b)} + \frac{q}{2}\]

From \(\tau_g = q_{2,x}\):

\[\tau_g = \frac{q}{2} \alpha \frac{\cosh(\alpha x)}{\sinh(\alpha b)}\]

The solutions can be reported graphically:

Remarks

1. Dependence on Parameter \(\alpha\)

The behaviour of \(\tau_g\) depends on the parameter \(\alpha\)

(Recall \(\alpha^2 = \frac{2G_g}{Gt \cdot t_g}\))

  • High \(\alpha\): high \(G_c\), low \(t_g\)
  • Low \(\alpha\): low \(G_c\), high \(t_g\)

2. Optimal Operation

The bonding works fine for high values of \(\alpha\). In these cases (\(\alpha\) high) the central region is substantially unloaded, which is beneficial from a viscoelastic phenomena (creep) point of view.

3. Stress Peaks

The peaks of stresses on the outer regions will be, in any case, reduced by yielding phenomena.

14. Remark on Euler-Lagrange Equations

Euler-Lagrange equations are used in variational calculus to determine the stationarity conditions for a functional.

Given a functional:

\[I = \int_a^b F(x, u, u_x, u_{xx}) dx\]

where \(I: U \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) maps from a generic function space to real numbers.

The stationarity condition (the maximum or the minimum) is obtained by applying the so-called Euler-Lagrange equations:

\[\frac{\partial F}{\partial u} - \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial u_x}\right)_x + \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial u_{xx}}\right)_{xx} = 0\]

meaning that the function \(u\) which satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations will determine an extremal value (maximum or minimum) for \(I\).

Instead of applying the Euler-Lagrange equations, the same result can be obtained by setting:

\[\delta I = 0\]

and integrating by parts.

Verification for Bonded Junction Problem

Starting Expression

Consider this approach with reference to the problem of minimizing \(U^*\) for continuous junctions; it was found that:

\[U^* = \frac{1}{Gt} \int_{-b}^{b} (q_2^2 - qq_2) dx + \frac{1}{2} \int_{-b}^{b} \frac{q_{2,x}^2 \cdot t_g}{G_g} dx\]

Impose \(\delta U^* = 0\)

\[\delta U^* = \frac{1}{Gt} \int_{-b}^{b} (2q_2 \delta q_2 - q \delta q_2) dx + \int_{-b}^{b} q_{2,x} \delta q_{2,x} \frac{t_g}{G_g} dx\]

Integrate by Parts

Integrate the second contribution by parts:

\[= \frac{1}{Gt} \int_{-b}^{b} \delta q_2 (2q_2 - q) dx - \int_{-b}^{b} \delta q_2 \cdot q_{2,xx} \frac{t_g}{G_g} dx + \delta q_2 \cdot q_{2,x} \frac{t_g}{G_g} \Big|_{-b}^{b} = 0\]

Apply Boundary Conditions

Observe now that:

\[q_2(b) = q \Rightarrow \delta q_2(b) = 0\] \[q_2(-b) = 0 \Rightarrow \delta q_2(-b) = 0\]

So:

\[= \int_{-b}^{b} \delta q_2 \left[ \frac{1}{Gt}(2q_2 - q) - \frac{t_g}{G_g} q_{2,xx} \right] dx = 0 \quad \forall \delta q_2\]

Final Result

As the previous equation should be verified for \(\forall \delta q_2\), it follows that:

\[\frac{1}{Gt}(2q_2 - q) - \frac{t_g}{G_g} q_{2,xx} = 0\]

which is the same stationarity condition obtained using the Euler-Lagrange equations.
