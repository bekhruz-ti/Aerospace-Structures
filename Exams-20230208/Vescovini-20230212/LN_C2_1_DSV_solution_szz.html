Introduction to Beams and De Saint Venant Solution

1. Introduction

Consider a 3D body, characterized by the following features:

  1. The body is slender, with one dimension which is much greater in comparison to the two other directions.
  2. The body is obtained by the rigid translation of the section, which is equal to itself along the beam axis.
  3. The material is isotropic, elastic and homogeneous (beams with sections made of two different materials, i.e. non-homogeneous sections, are not considered).
  4. Loads and constraints are applied at the extremal sections.
Goal of the investigation: To determine the state of stress for a generic section, provided it is far enough from where the loads are applied (and the constraints as well, as they are responsible for the introduction of the reaction forces).

2. Beam Representation and Coordinate System

The 3D body, hereinafter denoted as beam, is represented with the following coordinate system:

where:

  • \(z\): beam axis
  • \(xy\): plane of the section

In addition, \(xyz\) is taken such that:

\[\int_A x \, dA = \int_A y \, dA = 0\] \[\int_A xy \, dA = 0\]

Principal centroidal axes

Important: The loads, as reported at point 4, are applied at the ends. Notice that the distribution of the loads over the surface is not relevant for the present investigation. The only information that is relevant is the resultant of the surface forces.

The stress distribution over a section can be represented by its resultant:

\[\sigma(x,y) \quad \rightarrow \quad \int_A \sigma(x,y) \, dA\]

3. Applied Loads and Internal Actions

Consider a generic system of applied loads, which are now considered in terms of resultants (forces and moments).

The applied loads are denoted with the \(\hat{}\) symbol. They are taken such that:

  1. The internal shear and axial forces are positive
  2. The internal bending and twisting moments are positive according to the right-hand rule convention

3.1 Equilibrium Relations

From equilibrium considerations, it is obtained that:

Forces (constant):

\[T_x = \hat{T}_x, \quad T_y = \hat{T}_y, \quad T_z = \hat{T}_z\]

⟹ The shear is constant, the axial force is constant

Moments (can be constant or linear):

\[M_x = \hat{M}_x + z T_y\] \[M_y = \hat{M}_y - z T_x\] \[M_z = \hat{M}_z\]

3.2 Stress Components and Internal Actions

It is also remarked that the resultants are the integrals over the beam section of the stress components \(\sigma_{zz}\), \(\sigma_{xz}\) and \(\sigma_{yz}\).

Recalling the convention for the positive internal actions, it follows that:

Forces:

\[T_x = \int_A \sigma_{xz} \, dA\] \[T_y = \int_A \sigma_{yz} \, dA\] \[T_z = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, dA\]

Moments:

\[M_x = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, y \, dA\] \[M_y = \int_A -\sigma_{zz} \, x \, dA\] \[M_z = \int_A \left( \sigma_{yz} \, x - \sigma_{xz} \, y \right) dA\]
Note: The internal actions are statically equivalent to the stress resultants, and do not express the equilibrium with respect to the internal state of stress.

4. Formulation of the Problem

The starting point are the set of equations:

\[\begin{cases} div \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} + \underline{F} = \underline{0} & \text{in } \Omega \\ \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) = \underline{\underline{O}} & \text{in } \Omega \\ \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{t} & \text{in } S_F \\ \underline{u} = \hat{\underline{u}} & \text{in } S_u \end{cases}\]

4.1 Solution Approach

How can this problem be solved for the structure under investigation (3D slender body)?

  1. Instead of dealing with the compatibility requirement \(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \text{grad} \, \underline{u} + \text{grad} \, \underline{u}^T \right) = \underline{\underline{O}}\), the compatibility equations are introduced.
  2. Instead of obtaining a solution from the equations, an initial guess solution is proposed. It is checked ex-post if the solution satisfies the relevant equations. If this is the case, it means that the guess solution is the exact one. This approach is called the semi-inverse approach.
  3. Goal of the investigation is obtaining a solution far from the end sections where loads (also in the form of reaction forces) are applied.

4.2 Implications for Boundary Conditions

This means that the solution will be required to provide equilibrium at any section to the external loads. No Cauchy equilibrium is involved (with regard to the sections) in the derivation of the solution. This is, in fact, consistent with the idea of neglecting the actual shape of the applied loads and considering their resultants.

The two sets of loads are statically equivalent, i.e., \(\tilde{\sigma} A = \sigma A\), however their distribution is different.

5. De Saint Venant Principle

Consider the exact 3D solution in a section close to the loaded area. It is clear that both solutions satisfy the equilibrium and the compatibility equations. However, they are different because the boundary conditions over the load section are different:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \tilde{\sigma} \underline{e}_3\]
\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \hat{\sigma} \underline{e}_3\]

At the same time, the resultant of the stresses in the two sections is the same (however the equilibrium would not be fulfilled).

De Saint Venant Principle: The two solutions, when calculated at a distance of the order of the maximum size of the section, are equal. This means that local effects have disappeared (they have been spatially damped out).

5.1 Implications of DSV Principle

The DSV principle is exactly the reason for having assumed at the beginning that:

  1. The load shape is not relevant
  2. The solution is sought far away from the extremal sections

It follows that the boundary conditions relative to the section area, which could be imagined as \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \hat{\underline{t}}\), are replaced by a sort of "integral condition" in the form:

\[T_z = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, dA \qquad M_x = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, y \, dA\] \[T_y = \int_A \sigma_{zy} \, dA \qquad M_y = \int_A -\sigma_{zz} \, x \, dA\] \[T_x = \int_A \sigma_{zx} \, dA \qquad M_z = \int_A \left( \sigma_{zy} \, x - \sigma_{zx} \, y \right) dA\]

These conditions are intrinsically independent of how the stresses are distributed, but depend only on their integrals over \(A\).

5.2 Boundary Surfaces

  • \(S_{F_{body}}\) is the external surface of the body. No loads are applied here (thus the prescribed force is zero, while the displacements are part of the solution).
  • \(S_{F_{face}}\) is the beam cross-section. Here the DSV hypothesis takes place, and the equivalence with the internal actions is imposed. These latter can be seen as "integral" conditions, as they regard the integrals of the stresses over the section.

6. Complete Problem Statement

The problem is formulated as:

\[div \, \underline{\underline{\sigma}} = 0 \quad (\underline{F} = 0) \qquad \text{in } \Omega\]

Compatibility equations in \(\Omega\)

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{0} \qquad \text{in } S_{F_{body}}\]

Equivalence with internal actions in \(S_{F_{face}}\)

7. Solution via Semi-Inverse Method

7.1 The Guess Solution

The guess solution is taken as:

\[\boxed{\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = \sigma_{xy} = 0}\]

Clearly it is not hard to understand that this is a reasonable guess!

The procedure will be the following: if the guess solution satisfies the differential problem in the box, then it is the exact solution (unicity of the solution is guaranteed in linear elasticity).

Important Remark: The approach here outlined relies upon the assumptions of isotropic material and homogeneous section. If this is not the case, as it happens in the case of composite materials, the stress state will be in general 3D (\(\Rightarrow \sigma_{xx} \neq 0\); \(\sigma_{yy} \neq 0\); \(\sigma_{xy} \neq 0\)). In this case the classical DSV solution cannot be achieved in the analytical form here presented.

7.2 Composite Sections

Imagine a cross section composed of two different materials:

Different Poisson's ratios would, in fact, determine a deformed cross-section. Proper internal stress must then arise to restore the compatibility.

This simple example illustrates the concept by highlighting the contribution \(\sigma_{xx}\). However, it should be clear that in general the state of stress will be 3D for a non-homogeneous and/or non-isotropic section.

Just to avoid misunderstandings: for a composite beam the DSV solution can still be obtained, but the approach to be used is different from the semi-inverse strategy here illustrated.

8. Equilibrium Equations

Begin with the equilibrium equations:

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{xx/x} + \sigma_{xy/y} + \sigma_{xz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{xy/x} + \sigma_{yy/y} + \sigma_{yz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y} + \sigma_{zz/z} = 0 \end{cases}\]

As the guess solution is \(\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = \sigma_{xy} = 0\), we obtain:

Result R1

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{xz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{yz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y} + \sigma_{zz/z} = 0 \end{cases}\]

8.1 Implications

The first two equations reveal that:

\[\sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{xz}(x,y) \quad \text{(they do not depend on } z \text{)}\] \[\sigma_{yz} = \sigma_{yz}(x,y)\]

The last equation reveals that:

\(\sigma_{zz}\) can be up to a linear function of \(z\):

\[\underbrace{\sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y}}_{\text{depend on } x,y} + \sigma_{zz/z} = 0\]

\(\sigma_{zz/z}\) depends on \(x,y\) ⟹ \(\sigma_{zz}\) can be linear in \(z\)

9. Constitutive Law

Recall that the solid under investigation is a 3D solid. As such, consider the 3D elastic law:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} = \underline{\underline{\underline{\underline{C}}}} : \underline{\underline{\varepsilon}} \quad \leadsto \quad \{\sigma\} = [C]\{\varepsilon\} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \{\varepsilon\} = [S]\{\sigma\}\]

For a 3D isotropic hyperelastic material, the strain-stress relation reads:

\[\varepsilon_{xx} = \frac{1}{E}\left(\sigma_{xx} - \nu \sigma_{yy} - \nu \sigma_{zz}\right)\] \[\varepsilon_{yy} = \frac{1}{E}\left(-\nu \sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy} - \nu \sigma_{zz}\right)\] \[\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{1}{E}\left(-\nu \sigma_{xx} - \nu \sigma_{yy} + \sigma_{zz}\right)\] \[\gamma_{xy} = \frac{1}{G}\sigma_{xy}, \quad \gamma_{yz} = \frac{1}{G}\sigma_{yz}, \quad \gamma_{xz} = \frac{1}{G}\sigma_{xz}\]

Since \(\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = \sigma_{xy} = 0\), it follows that:

Result R2

\[\begin{array}{ll} \varepsilon_{xx} = -\frac{\nu}{E}\sigma_{zz} & \gamma_{xy} = 0 \\ \varepsilon_{yy} = -\frac{\nu}{E}\sigma_{zz} & \gamma_{yz} = \frac{1}{G}\sigma_{yz} \\ \varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E} & \gamma_{xz} = \frac{1}{G}\sigma_{xz} \end{array}\]

9.1 Section Shape Change

Note that, according to the guess solution (which will be demonstrated to be the exact one), the not null components of \(\underline{\underline{\varepsilon}}\) are 5.

More specifically, the presence of an axial stress \(\sigma_{zz}\) implies that \(\varepsilon_{xx} \neq 0\) and \(\varepsilon_{yy} \neq 0\).

Thus the section changes its original shape (in contrast to the common wrong assumption that the section remains equal to itself after the deformation process).

10. Compatibility Equations

The compatibility equations are:

\[\begin{cases} \varepsilon_{xx/yy} + \varepsilon_{yy/xx} = \gamma_{xy/xy} \\ \varepsilon_{yy/zz} + \varepsilon_{zz/yy} = \gamma_{yz/yz} \\ \varepsilon_{zz/xx} + \varepsilon_{xx/zz} = \gamma_{zx/zx} \end{cases}\] \[\begin{cases} 2\varepsilon_{xx/yz} = \gamma_{xy/xz} + \gamma_{xz/xy} - \gamma_{yz/xx} \\ 2\varepsilon_{yy/zx} = \gamma_{yz/yx} + \gamma_{yx/yz} - \gamma_{zx/yy} \\ 2\varepsilon_{zz/xy} = \gamma_{zx/zy} + \gamma_{zy/zx} - \gamma_{xy/zz} \end{cases}\]

10.1 Simplifications

Using the results from R1 and R2:

  • \(\gamma_{xy} = 0\) (from R2)
  • \(\sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{xz}(x,y)\) and \(\sigma_{yz} = \sigma_{yz}(x,y)\) ⟹ \(\gamma_{xz/z} = 0\), \(\gamma_{yz/z} = 0\) (from R1, R2)
  • \(\sigma_{zz/zz} = 0\) (from R1) ⟹ \(\varepsilon_{xx/zz} = 0\), \(\varepsilon_{yy/zz} = 0\), \(\varepsilon_{zz/zz} = 0\) (from R2)

And so:

\[\begin{cases} \varepsilon_{xx/yy} + \varepsilon_{yy/xx} = 0 \\ \varepsilon_{zz/yy} = 0 \\ \varepsilon_{zz/xx} = 0 \\ 2\varepsilon_{xx/yz} = \gamma_{xz/xy} - \gamma_{yz/xx} \\ 2\varepsilon_{yy/zx} = \gamma_{yz/yx} - \gamma_{zx/yy} \\ 2\varepsilon_{zz/xy} = 0 \end{cases}\]

Expressing the compatibility condition in terms of stresses using the constitutive law (Result R2):

Result R3

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{zz/yy} = 0 \\ \sigma_{zz/xx} = 0 \\ -\nu \, \sigma_{zz/yz} = \sigma_{xz/xy} - \sigma_{yz/xx} \\ -\nu \, \sigma_{yy/zx} = \sigma_{yz/xy} - \sigma_{zx/yy} \\ \sigma_{zz/xy} = 0 \end{cases}\]

11. Summary of the Governing Equations

Complete Set of Equations

Equilibrium:

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{xz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{yz/z} = 0 \\ \sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y} + \sigma_{zz/z} = 0 \end{cases}\]

Compatibility:

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{zz/yy} = 0 \\ \sigma_{zz/xx} = 0 \\ \sigma_{zz/xy} = 0 \\ -\nu \, \sigma_{zz/yz} = \sigma_{xz/xy} - \sigma_{yz/xx} \\ -\nu \, \sigma_{yy/zx} = \sigma_{yz/xy} - \sigma_{zx/yy} \end{cases}\]

Boundary Conditions:

Equivalence with internal actions in \(S_{F_{face}}\)

\(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{0}\) in \(S_{F_{body}}\)

Note: The constitutive relation has been used for obtaining the current expression of the compatibility equation.

12. Solution for \(\sigma_{zz}\)

The solution is sought starting from the equilibrium:

\[\begin{cases} \sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y} + \sigma_{zz/z} = 0 & \text{in } \Omega \\ \underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{0} & \text{in } S_{F_{face}} \end{cases}\]

(For now only the equilibrium along the beam axis is considered.)

12.1 Cauchy Condition

It is worth observing that the Cauchy condition \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{0}\), expressing the equilibrium at the boundaries between the internal stresses and the applied ones (none), is here written as:

\[\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \cdot \underline{n} = \underline{0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} = 0 \quad \text{with} \quad \underline{t} = \sigma_{xz} \, \underline{e}_1 + \sigma_{yz} \, \underline{e}_2\]

The condition requires that \(\underline{t} \perp \underline{n}\).

12.2 Integration over Cross Section

Integrate the equilibrium equation over the cross section:

\[\int_A \left( \sigma_{xz/x} + \sigma_{yz/y} + \sigma_{zz/z} \right) dA =\] \[= \int_A \left( \text{div} \, \underline{t} + \sigma_{zz/z} \right) dA =\] \[= \oint_{\partial A} \underline{t} \cdot \underline{n} \, ds + \int_A \sigma_{zz/z} \, dA = 0\]
\[\Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\int_A \sigma_{zz/z} \, dA = 0}\]

12.3 Form of \(\sigma_{zz}\)

Considering the following results:

  • \(\int_A \sigma_{zz/z} \, dA = 0\) (equilibrium along beam axis)
  • \(\sigma_{zz/yy} = \sigma_{zz/xx} = \sigma_{zz/xy} = 0\) (Compatibility)

It is clear that \(\sigma_{zz}\) is expressed as:

\[\boxed{\sigma_{zz} = A_0 + A_1 x + A_2 y + \left( A_3 x + A_4 y \right) z}\]

Note that \(\sigma_{zz/z} = A_3 x + A_4 y\) and

\[\int_A \sigma_{zz/z} \, dA = \int_A \left( A_3 x + A_4 y \right) dA = A_3 \int_A x \, dA + A_4 \int_A y \, dA = 0\]

as the axes are principal centroidal.

13. Internal Actions and Final Solution

13.1 Calculation of Internal Actions

According to the definition of the internal actions:

Axial Force:

\[T_z = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, dA = A_0 \, A \quad \text{(as the axes are principal centroidal)}\]

Bending Moment \(M_x\):

\[M_x = \int_A \sigma_{zz} \, y \, dA = \int_A \left( A_0 y + A_1 xy + A_2 y^2 + A_3 xy z + A_4 y^2 z \right) dA\] \[= J_{xx} \left( A_2 + z \, A_4 \right)\]

Bending Moment \(M_y\):

\[M_y = -\int_A \sigma_{zz} \, x \, dA = -\int_A \left( A_0 x + A_1 x^2 + A_2 xy + A_3 x^2 z + A_4 xy z \right) dA\] \[= -J_{yy} \left( A_1 + A_3 z \right)\]

13.2 Determining the Constants

Recalling that:

\[\begin{cases} T_z = \text{const} = A_0 \, A \\ M_x = \hat{M}_x + z \, T_y = J_{xx} \left( A_2 + z \, A_4 \right) \\ M_y = \hat{M}_y - z \, T_x = -J_{yy} \left( A_1 + A_3 z \right) \end{cases}\]

And equating the constant and the linear parts:

\[\begin{array}{lcl} A_0 = T_z / A & \Rightarrow & A_0 = T_z / A \\ T_y = J_{xx} \, A_4 & \Rightarrow & A_4 = T_y / J_{xx} \\ \hat{M}_x = J_{xx} \, A_2 & \Rightarrow & A_2 = \hat{M}_x / J_{xx} \\ T_x = J_{yy} \, A_3 & \Rightarrow & A_3 = T_x / J_{yy} \\ \hat{M}_y = -J_{yy} \, A_1 & \Rightarrow & A_1 = -\hat{M}_y / J_{yy} \end{array}\]

13.3 Final Expression for \(\sigma_{zz}\)

It follows that:

\[\sigma_{zz} = A_0 + A_1 x + A_2 y + \left( A_3 x + A_4 y \right) z\] \[= \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{\hat{M}_y}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{\hat{M}_x}{J_{xx}} y + \left( \frac{T_x}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{T_y}{J_{xx}} y \right) z\] \[= \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{\hat{M}_y - T_x z}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{\hat{M}_x + T_y z}{J_{xx}} y\]

De Saint Venant Solution for Normal Stress

\[\boxed{\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y}\]

where \(M_y = M_y(z)\) and \(M_x = M_x(z)\)

14. Remarks on the Solution

Remark 1: Exact Solution

The problem has been solved for \(\sigma_{zz}\). Starting from the guess solution, it was verified that equilibrium and compatibility conditions can be identically fulfilled if \(\sigma_{zz}\) has the expression here obtained.

The solution derived is the exact one. No approximations are involved in this procedure.

A stress field characterized by:

\[\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = \sigma_{xy} = 0\] \[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y\]

identically satisfies all the governing equations.

Remark 2: Shear Stress Problem

If the shear stresses are to be evaluated, the problem to be solved is more complex, and some approximations are generally needed to achieve a solution.

Remark 3: Solution Properties

The solution \(\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{M_y}{J_y} x + \frac{M_x}{J_x} y\):

  1. is obtained assuming principal centroidal axes
  2. is an exact solution under the DSV hypothesis (constant section, material..., loads at the extreme sections)
  3. what is useful is the possibility of obtaining the stress state once the internal actions are available

Remark 4: Stress Components by Load Type

  • In the presence of \(M_x\) and/or \(M_y\) and/or \(T_z\) → \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \sim \sigma_{zz}\)
  • In the presence of \(T_x\) and/or \(T_y\) → \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \sim \sigma_{zz}\) and \(\sigma_{zx}\) / \(\sigma_{zy}\)
  • In the presence of \(M_z\) → \(\underline{\underline{\sigma}} \sim \sigma_{zx}\) / \(\sigma_{zy}\)

Remark 5: Linearity

This result is obtained in the context of linearity assumption. This means that by amplifying the loading conditions by a factor \(\lambda\), the stresses will be amplified by the same factor \(\lambda\).

\[\sigma_{zz} = \hat{\sigma}_{zz} \quad \rightarrow \quad \sigma_{zz} = \lambda \hat{\sigma}_{zz}\]

Remark 6: Equilibrium vs. Equivalence

  1. The internal actions equilibrate the external loads
  2. The internal actions are statically equivalent to the cross section state of stress

Remark 7: Load Distribution Independence

The beam model, consistently with the DSV assumptions, cannot account for the differences between applied loads characterized by the same resultants.

Remark 8: Material Independence

Note that \(\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{M_y}{J_y} x + \frac{M_x}{J_x} y\) does not depend on the section elastic properties. \(\sigma_{zz}\) is just a function of the internal actions.

This means that for a given set of internal actions \(T_z\), \(M_y\) and \(M_x\), the state of stress \(\sigma_{zz}\) will be identical for two beams made of different materials, say a beam of steel and another of rubber.

The internal stress arises to equilibrate the external loads. The difference between the steel and the rubber beam will regard the deformation!

15. DSV Solution for Axial Force

Consider the case where the only internal action is given by \(T_z\). The DSV solution is then:

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} + \frac{M_{xx}}{J_{xx}} y - \frac{M_{yy}}{J_{yy}} z \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A}}\]

15.1 Displacement Field

The displacement field can be obtained after:

  1. Introducing the constitutive law
  2. Integrating the strain components

Strains:

\[\varepsilon_{xx} = -\frac{\nu}{E} \sigma_{zz} = -\nu \frac{T_z}{EA}\] \[\varepsilon_{yy} = -\frac{\nu}{E} \sigma_{zz} = -\nu \frac{T_z}{EA}\] \[\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{\sigma_{zz}}{E} = \frac{T_z}{EA}\]

Displacement Field for Axial Force

\[u = -\nu \frac{T_z}{EA} x \quad \text{(change of shape due to Poisson's effect)}\] \[v = -\nu \frac{T_z}{EA} y \quad \text{(change of shape due to Poisson's effect)}\] \[w = \frac{T_z}{EA} z \quad \text{(linear displacement along beam axis)}\]

16. DSV Solution for Bending

Consider the case of pure bending (constant along the beam axis). No shear-induced bending contribution.

The DSV simplifies to:

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y \quad \text{where } M_x = \text{Const} = \hat{M}_x\]

16.1 Strains

\[\varepsilon_{xx} = -\frac{\nu}{E} \sigma_{zz} = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} y\] \[\varepsilon_{yy} = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} y\] \[\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} y\] \[\gamma_{xy} = 0, \quad \gamma_{xz} = 0, \quad \gamma_{yz} = 0\]

16.2 Displacement Field

The displacement field is obtained by integration:

\[\varepsilon_{xx} = u_{/x} \quad \Rightarrow \quad u = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} xy + f_u(y,z)\] \[\varepsilon_{yy} = v_{/y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} \frac{1}{2} y^2 + f_v(x,z)\] \[\varepsilon_{zz} = w_{/z} \quad \Rightarrow \quad w = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} zy + f_w(x,y)\]

It can be shown that a solution is found by taking \(f_w = f_u = 0\).

To determine \(f_v\):

\[\gamma_{xy} = u_{/y} + v_{/x} = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} x + f_{v/x} = 0\] \[\gamma_{yz} = v_{/z} + w_{/y} = f_{v/z} + \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} z = 0\] \[\Rightarrow \quad f_v = \frac{1}{2} \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} \left( \nu x^2 - z^2 \right)\]

Complete Displacement Field for Pure Bending

\[u = -\nu \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} xy\] \[v = -\frac{M_x}{2 E J_{xx}} \left( z^2 + \nu \left( y^2 - x^2 \right) \right)\] \[w = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} zy\]

16.3 Centroidal Fiber Displacement

The displacement field of the centroidal fiber (\(x = y = 0\)) is thus:

\[u = 0\] \[v = -\frac{M_x}{2 E J_{xx}} z^2\] \[w = 0\]

The rotation \(\theta_x\), at the generic point \(z\) is:

\[\boxed{\theta_x \simeq -v_{/z} = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} z}\]

17. Curvature and Bernoulli-Navier Law

It follows that:

\[\theta_{x/z} \simeq -v_{/zz} = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}} \quad \text{(constant second derivative)}\]

Denote the second derivative \(v_{/zz}\) with \(-k_x\) (curvature):

\[-k_x = v_{/zz}\]

Bernoulli-Navier Law

\[\boxed{k_x = \frac{M_x}{E J_{xx}}} \quad \text{or} \quad \boxed{M_x = EJ \, k_{xx}}\]

Remarks

1. Constant Curvature: For constant bending moment, the curvature is constant along the beam axis.

2. Section Remains Normal: For constant bending moment, \(\sigma_{zz} \neq 0\) while \(\sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{yz} = 0\), thus:

\[\boxed{\gamma_{xz} = \gamma_{yz} = 0}\]

No shearing deformation implies that the section remains orthogonal to the beam axis after deformation happens.

18. Conclusion: Euler-Bernoulli Model

These observations (the fact that sections remain normal to the beam axis, i.e., \(\gamma_{xz} = \gamma_{yz} = 0\)) will be the ingredients for formulating the beam problem from a kinematic point of view, within the context of the so-called Euler-Bernoulli model (see displacement-based approaches later).

Key Results Summary

De Saint Venant Solution

\[\sigma_{zz} = \frac{T_z}{A} - \frac{M_y}{J_{yy}} x + \frac{M_x}{J_{xx}} y\]

Valid under assumptions:

  • Slender beam with constant cross-section
  • Isotropic, elastic, homogeneous material
  • Loads applied at extremal sections
  • Solution evaluated far from end sections (DSV principle)
  • Principal centroidal axes used

Bernoulli-Navier Law

\[M_x = E J_{xx} \, k_x\]
