Exercise 1

The figure represents a semi-monocoque model of a fuselage trunk of length \(2l\). It has three circular frames, each with outer radius \(r_1\) and inner radius \(r_2\), and eight uniformly spaced stringers, each with concentrated area \(A\). The outer cylindrical thin panel thickness is equal to \(t\). The panel is connected to the central frame by means of sixteen uniformly spaced rivets, each with shank diameter \(d\), and repre- sented by the red dots in the figure. The outer cylindrical thin panel in continuous across the central rib (there is not a junction between a "forward" and "rear" panel), thus the rivets do only connect the rib to the continuous panel. The fuselage is transmitting a constant torsional moment \(M_z\). Compute the shear stress in the rivet shank highlighted by the red arrow.

(Unit for result: MPa)

Data
\(r_1 = 1.5\, \text{m}\)
\(r_2 = 1.45\, \text{m}\)
\(l = 1\, \text{m}\)
\(d = 3\, \text{mm}\)
\(A = 10\, \text{cm}^2\)
\(t = 1.5\, \text{mm}\)
\(M_z = 15000\, \text{N m}\)

Answer \(\tau_{zz} = 0\, \text{MPa}\)

Explanation: Since the outer cylindrical thin panel is continuous across the central rib, there is no shear flow discontinuity at the rivet location. The rivets connect the rib to the continuous panel, but the torsional shear flow in the panel passes continuously without being transferred through the rivets. Therefore, the shear stress in the rivet shank is zero.

Exercise 2

The semi-monocoque cross section sketched in the figure has three concentrated areas \(A\), two panels with thickness \(t\) and one panel with thickness \(2t\). Compute, with respect to the reference system sketched in the figure, the \(x\) coordinate of the shear center.

(Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(a = 1\, \text{m}\)
\(A = 4\, \text{cm}^2\)
\(E = 7 \times 10^4\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)

Answer \(x_{SC} = 369.4\, \text{mm}\)

Solution approach: Using the open cell shear flow distribution and equilibrium equations. With \(b = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}a\), \(c = 2b = \sqrt{2}a\), and \(J_{xx} = 2Ab^2\): \[x_{SC} = \frac{2a}{4 + \sqrt{2}} = 369.4\, \text{mm}\]

Exercise 3

Consider the thin beam model sketched in the figure, and loaded by the two forces \(F\). All the beams have the same cross-section. Compute the rotation \(\theta\) of the point where the beam of length \(b\) is joined to the two beams of length \(a\). Neglect shear deformability.

(Unit for result: rad)

Data
\(a = 1\, \text{m}\)
\(b = 2\, \text{m}\)
\(c = 3\, \text{m}\)
\(EI_{xx} = EI_{yy} = 12 \times 10^{14}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(EA = 6 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N}\)
\(GJ = 7 \times 10^{14}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(K = 1 \times 10^6\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(F = 10000\, \text{N}\)

Answer \(\theta = 7.4 \times 10^{-5}\, \text{rad}\)

Solution: Using the Principle of Complementary Virtual Work (PCVW). First compute the spring reaction \(X = 8817.5\, \text{N}\), then: \[\theta = \frac{2F_a \cdot b}{GJ} - \frac{X \cdot c^2}{2EI} + \frac{2F_a \cdot c}{EI} = 7.4 \times 10^{-5}\, \text{rad}\]

Exercise 4

Consider the simply supported thin beam model sketched in the figure, with overall length \(l\), and loaded by the linearly varying force per unit of length \(f(z)\), with maximum value equal to \(a\). Compute the exact vertical displacement \(v(l/2)\) in the middle of the beam. Neglect shear deformability.

(Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(a = 1000\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(l = 2000\, \text{mm}\)
\(EI = 12 \times 10^{12}\, \text{N mm}^2\)

Answer \(v(l/2) = -0.04\, \text{mm}\)

Solution: The load distribution is triangular with peak at mid-span: \[f(z) = \frac{2a}{l}z \quad \text{for } z \in \left[0, \frac{l}{2}\right]\] \[f(z) = a\left(1 - \frac{2z}{l}\right) \quad \text{for } z \in \left[\frac{l}{2}, l\right]\] Using PCVW with virtual unit load at \(z = l/2\): \[v = -\frac{7al^4}{233280 \cdot EI} = -0.04\, \text{mm}\] (Negative sign indicates downward displacement)

Exercise 5

The thin beam sketched in the figure, of length \(2l\), is loaded by the compressive force \(F\). A spring with stiffness \(K\) is connected in the middle of the beam, and helps increasing the beam buckling load. Compute the approximated value of critical buckling load by resorting to a suitable polynomial approximation of the transverse displacement truncated to the first non-null term.

(Unit for result: N)

Data
\(l = 2000\, \text{mm}\)
\(EA = 6 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N}\)
\(EI = 12 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(K = 1 \times 10^3\, \text{N/mm}\)

Answer \(F_{cr} = 84000\, \text{N}\)

Solution: Using polynomial approximation \(v = Az^2 + Bz + C\) with boundary conditions \(v(0) = 0\) and \(v(2l) = 0\), leading to \(v = A(z^2 - 2lz)\). Applying the Principle of Virtual Work with the Green-Lagrange strain tensor for buckling analysis: \[F_{cr} = \frac{8EI/l + Kl^4}{(8-4+32/3)l^3} = 84000\, \text{N}\]

Exercise 6

The clamped beam sketched in the figure has a varying axial stiffness \(EA(z) = a + bz\), and is loaded by the constant force per unit of length \(f\). By resorting to a polynomial approximation of the axial dis- placement, truncated to one term, estimate the axial displacement \(w(l)\) at the free extremity of the beam.

(Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(l = 4000\, \text{mm}\)
\(f = 1000\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(a = 6 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N}\)
\(b = 2.5 \times 10^7\, \text{N/mm}\)

Answer \(w(l) = 0.0727\, \text{mm}\)

Solution: Using 1-term polynomial approximation \(w = cz\) with \(w' = c\). From PVW: \[\int_0^l \delta c \cdot (a + bz) \cdot c \, dz = \int_0^l \delta c \cdot f \cdot z \, dz\] \[\left(al + \frac{1}{2}bl^2\right)c = \frac{1}{2}fl^2\] \[c = \frac{fl}{2\left(a + \frac{1}{2}bl\right)}\] \[w(l) = c \cdot l = \frac{fl^2}{2\left(a + \frac{1}{2}bl\right)} = 0.0727\, \text{mm}\]
