Exercise 1

The semi-monocoque beam sketched in the figure is loaded at the free end by the vertical force \(F\), applied along the lefmost panel. Compute the upper right stringer axial stress \(\sigma_{zz}\) at point \(B\). (Unit for result: MPa)

Data \(a = 400\, \text{mm}\)
\(l = 12000\, \text{mm}\)
\(E = 70000.0\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)
\(A_1 = 200\, \text{mm}^2\)

\(A_2 = 400\, \text{mm}^2\)

\(F = 5000\, \text{N}\)

Answer

Solution:

Point \(B\) is located at \(z = \frac{l}{2}\) from the clamp.

The force \(F\) is applied at an angle, with components \(\frac{F}{\sqrt{2}}\) in both \(x\) and \(y\) directions.

Step 1: Calculate the bending moment at \(z = \frac{l}{2}\): \[M_y\left(\frac{l}{2}\right) = -\frac{F}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{l}{2}\] Step 2: Calculate the second moment of area: \[I_{xx} = A_1 \cdot 2 \cdot \left(\frac{a}{2}\right)^2 = A_1 \cdot a^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = A_1 \cdot a^2\] (considering the contribution of stringers \(A_1\) at distance \(\frac{a}{2}\) from neutral axis)

Step 3: Calculate stress at point \(B\) (located at \(y = \frac{a}{2}\), \(x = \frac{a}{2}\)): \[\sigma_{zz}(B) = \frac{M_y\left(\frac{l}{2}\right)}{I_{xx}} \cdot \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}} = -\frac{F}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{l}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{A_1 a^2} \cdot \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}\] \[\sigma_{zz}(B) = -\frac{F \cdot l}{4 \cdot A_1 \cdot a}\] Step 4: Substitute numerical values: \[\sigma_{zz}(B) = -\frac{5000 \times 12000}{4 \times 200 \times 400} = -\frac{60000000}{320000} = -187.5\, \text{MPa}\]
Final Answer: \(\sigma_{zz} = -187\, \text{MPa}\)

Exercise 2

The beam sketched in the figure is loaded by a distributed force for unit of length with butterfly distri- bution and null resultant \(q(x) = -q + 2qx/l\). By resorting to the displacement method, and using a trigonometric approximation for the vertical dis- placement \(v\), with the first and second term (i.e. \(v \approx a \sin(\pi x/l) + b \sin(2\pi x/l)\) ), estimate the rotation \(\theta\) at point \(A\) (the point at \(x = l/2\) from the hinge). Account for the axial pre-stress. Note: \(\int \sin(\frac{c\pi x}{l})x dx = -\frac{lx}{c\pi} \cos(\frac{c\pi x}{l}) + \frac{l^2}{\pi^2 c^2} \sin(\frac{c\pi x}{l})\). (Unit for result: rad)

Data \(q = 100\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(F_x = 10000\, \text{N}\)
\(EA = 1 \times 10^8\, \text{N}\)
\(EI = 1 \times 10^{11}\, \text{Nmm}^2\)

\(l = 4000\, \text{mm}\)

Answer

Solution:

Using the Principle of Virtual Work (PVW) with the displacement approximation:
\[w = a \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{l}\right) + b \sin\left(\frac{2\pi x}{l}\right)\] Step 1: Compute derivatives: \[w' = \frac{\pi}{l} a \cos\left(\frac{\pi x}{l}\right) + \frac{2\pi}{l} b \cos\left(\frac{2\pi x}{l}\right)\] \[w'' = -\frac{\pi^2}{l^2} a \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{l}\right) - \frac{4\pi^2}{l^2} b \sin\left(\frac{2\pi x}{l}\right)\] Step 2: Apply PVW: \[\int_0^l \left(\delta w'' \cdot EI \cdot w'' + \delta w' \cdot F_x \cdot w'\right) dx = \int_0^l \delta w \cdot q(x)\, dx\] Step 3: Equation for coefficient \(a\) (\(\delta a\)): \[\left[\left(\frac{\pi}{l}\right)^4 EI \cdot \frac{l}{2} + \left(\frac{\pi}{l}\right)^2 F_x \cdot \frac{l}{2}\right] a = 0\] This gives: \(a = 0\) Step 4: Equation for coefficient \(b\) (\(\delta b\)): \[\left[\left(\frac{2\pi}{l}\right)^4 EI \cdot \frac{l}{2} + \left(\frac{2\pi}{l}\right)^2 F_x \cdot \frac{l}{2}\right] b = -\frac{l\bar{q}}{\pi}\] \[b = -\frac{\bar{q}}{\pi} \cdot \frac{2}{\left(\frac{2\pi}{l}\right)^4 EI + \left(\frac{2\pi}{l}\right)^2 F_x}\] Step 5: Calculate rotation at \(x = \frac{l}{2}\): \[\theta = w'\left(\frac{l}{2}\right) = \frac{2\pi}{l} b \cos(\pi) = -\frac{2\pi}{l} b\] Step 6: Substitute values and compute: \[\theta = 0.15\, \text{rad}\]
Final Answer: \(\theta = 0.15\, \text{rad}\)

Exercise 3

The beam in the figure, with the sketched triangular cross section, is loaded by a distributed traction force per unit of length \(f\). By resorting to a FE model of the beam with one linear element estimate the axial displacement of the free end. (Unit for result: mm)

Data \(a = 1\, \text{cm}\)
\(l = 1\, \text{m}\)
\(f = 100 \times 10^2\, \text{N/m}\)
\(E = 70000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)

Answer

Solution:

Using a single linear finite element to model the beam.

Step 1: Convert units: \[a = 1\, \text{cm} = 10\, \text{mm}\] \[l = 1\, \text{m} = 1000\, \text{mm}\] \[f = 100 \times 10^2\, \text{N/m} = 10\, \text{N/mm}\] Step 2: Calculate cross-sectional area (triangular): \[A = \frac{a^2}{2} = \frac{10^2}{2} = 50\, \text{mm}^2\] Step 3: Calculate axial stiffness: \[EA = 70000 \times 50 = 3.5 \times 10^6\, \text{N}\] Step 4: For linear element, shape function at node 2: \[N_2(x) = \frac{x}{l} = \frac{x}{1000}\] Step 5: Calculate equivalent nodal force from distributed load: \[\delta \mathcal{L}_e = \int_0^{1000} \delta u_2 \cdot \frac{x}{1000} \cdot f\, dx = \delta u_2 \cdot 500 f\] Step 6: Apply equilibrium equation: \[\frac{EA}{l} u_2 = 500 f\] Step 7: Solve for displacement: \[u_2 = \frac{500 \cdot l \cdot f}{EA} = \frac{500 \times 1000 \times 10}{3.5 \times 10^6} = \frac{5 \times 10^6}{3.5 \times 10^6} = 1.43\, \text{mm}\]
Final Answer: \(u_2 = 1.43\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 4

Consider the 3-D beam sketched in the figure, loaded by the force \(F\). The axial stiffness \(EA\), torsional stiffness \(GJ\) and bending stiffnesses \(EI_{xx}\) and \(EI_{yy}\) are the same for the three beams. The spring has stiffness \(K\). Compute the vertical displacement \(v\) at the extremity of beam 1. (Unit for result: mm)

Data \(E = 70000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)
\(l_1 = 1000\, \text{mm}\)
\(l_2 = 500\, \text{mm}\)
\(l_3 = 250\, \text{mm}\)
\(EA = 800 * E\, \text{N}\)
\(GJ = 1000 * G\, \text{Nmm}^2\)

\(EI_{xx} = EI_{yy} = 10000 * E\, \text{Nmm}^2\)

\(K = 10\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(F = 1000\, \text{N}\)

Answer

Solution:

Analyzing the 3D frame structure with spring support.

Step 1: Define internal forces. Let \(X\) be the reaction at the spring.
Moment equations in the beams: \[M_{y3} = F z_3, \quad M_{y2} = -F l_3, \quad M_{x2} = -F z_2\] \[M_{x1}' = -z_1, \quad M_{y1} = F l_2, \quad M_{x1} = -X z_1 + F l_3\] Step 2: Apply virtual work / compatibility at the spring: \[\int_0^{l_1} \frac{X z_1^2}{EI_{xx1}} dz_1 - \int_0^{l_1} \frac{F l_3 z_1}{EI_{xx1}} dz_1 + \frac{X}{K} = 0\] Step 3: Solve the compatibility equation: \[\left(\frac{1}{3} l_1^3 + \frac{EI_{xx1}}{K}\right) X = \frac{1}{2} F l_3 l_1^2\] \[X = \frac{\frac{1}{2} F l_3 l_1^2}{\frac{1}{3} l_1^3 + \frac{EI_{xx1}}{K}}\] Step 4: Calculate numerical values: \[EI_{xx1} = 10000 \times 70000 = 7 \times 10^8\, \text{Nmm}^2\] \[\frac{EI_{xx1}}{K} = \frac{7 \times 10^8}{10} = 7 \times 10^7\, \text{mm}^3\] \[\frac{1}{3} l_1^3 = \frac{1}{3} \times 10^9 = 3.33 \times 10^8\, \text{mm}^3\] Step 5: Calculate vertical displacement: \[v = -\frac{X}{K}\]
Final Answer: \(v = -71\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 5

Enterprise's antimatter containment chamber (the inner circle in the figure, to be considered as rigid) is held in place by a web of eight equal beams of lenght \(l\), each hinged at both extremities. The outer ring onto which the beams are hinged is rigid and fully constrained. The beams material have eleastic modulus \(E\) and Poisson coefficient \(\nu\). Their cross section area is \(A\), their bending stiffness are \(E_{xx}\) and \(E_{yy}\), and their torsional stiffness is \(GJ\) The beams are arranged in such a way that the angle between them is equal to 45 deg. The first vertical beam is aligned with a force \(F\) that is applied to the antimatter containment chamber. Compute the overall vertical displacement of the antimatter containment chamber in the direction of the force. (Unit for result: mm)

Data \(l = 1\, \text{m}\)
\(A = 150\, \text{mm}^2\)

\(E = 210000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)
\(EI_{xx} = EI_{yy} = 1000*E\, \text{Nmm}^2\)

\(GJ = 1000 * G\, \text{Nmm}^2\)

\(F = 1 \times 10^5\, \text{N}\)

Answer

Solution:

Since the beams are pinned at both ends, they can only carry axial forces (no bending).

Step 1: Define the axial stiffness of each beam: \[k = \frac{EA}{l} = \frac{210000 \times 150}{1000} = 31500\, \text{N/mm}\] Step 2: Analyze the geometry. With 8 beams at 45° intervals: - 1 beam aligned with force (vertical, 0°) - 2 beams at ±45° from vertical - 2 beams at ±90° (horizontal) - 2 beams at ±135° from vertical - 1 beam at 180° (opposite vertical) Step 3: For vertical displacement \(u\), the axial elongation of diagonal beams: \[\bar{w} = \frac{u}{\sqrt{2}}\] (for beams at 45°) Step 4: Force equilibrium in vertical direction: - Vertical beam contribution: \(2 \cdot k \cdot u\) (top and bottom beams) - Diagonal beams contribution: \(4 \cdot k \cdot \frac{u}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = 4 \cdot k \cdot \frac{u}{2} = 2ku\) - Horizontal beams: no contribution to vertical force \[\left(2 + \frac{4}{2}\right) k \cdot u = F\] \[4 \cdot k \cdot u = F\] Step 5: Solve for displacement: \[u = \frac{F}{4k} = \frac{1 \times 10^5}{4 \times 31500} = \frac{100000}{126000} = 0.79\, \text{mm}\]
Final Answer: \(u = 0.79\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 6

The semi-monocoque beam sketched in the figure, with the square cross section drawn below, is loaded by a vertical force \(F\) aligned with the vertical cross-section diagonal and applied at the free end, and an horizontal force \(2F\) aligned with the horizontal cross-section diagonal and applied in the middle of the beam. All panels have the same thickness \(t\). Compute the shear stress \(\tau\) in the panel 4 (the one with flux \(q_4\)) at \(z = 3/4l\) (i.e. at a distance of \(1/4l\) from the clamp). (Unit for result: MPa)

Data \(F = 1 \times 10^5\, \text{N}\)
\(l = 5\, \text{m}\)
\(a = 50\, \text{cm}\)
\(A_1 = 1500\, \text{mm}^2\)

\(A_2 = 1000\, \text{mm}^2\)

\(t = 2\, \text{mm}\)
\(E = 70000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)

Answer

Solution:

At \(z = \frac{3l}{4}\), we need to consider both loading contributions.
Convert: \(a = 50\, \text{cm} = 500\, \text{mm}\)

Step 1: Shear flow from vertical force \(F\) (applied at free end): For the diamond cross-section with vertical force along the diagonal: \[\bar{q} = \frac{F}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2a} = \frac{F}{2\sqrt{2} \cdot a}\] Step 2: Shear flow from horizontal force \(2F\) (applied at \(z = l/2\)): At \(z = \frac{3l}{4}\), the horizontal force \(2F\) does contribute (since \(\frac{3l}{4} > \frac{l}{2}\)): \[\tilde{q} = \frac{2F}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2a} = \frac{F}{\sqrt{2} \cdot a}\] Step 3: Calculate the shear flow in panel 4: The flows combine based on the cross-section geometry: \[q_4 = \tilde{q} - \bar{q} = \frac{F}{\sqrt{2} \cdot a} - \frac{F}{2\sqrt{2} \cdot a} = \frac{F}{2\sqrt{2} \cdot a}\] Step 4: Calculate shear stress: \[\tau_4 = \frac{q_4}{t} = \frac{F}{2\sqrt{2} \cdot a \cdot t}\] Step 5: Substitute numerical values: \[\tau_4 = \frac{1 \times 10^5}{2\sqrt{2} \times 500 \times 2} = \frac{100000}{2828.4} = 35.35\, \text{MPa}\]
Final Answer: \(\tau_4 = 35.35\, \text{MPa}\)
