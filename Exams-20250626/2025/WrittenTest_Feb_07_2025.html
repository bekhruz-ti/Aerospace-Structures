Exercise 1

The simply supported beam in the figure cross section is made with two different materials, as sketched in Sez A-A. The beam is loaded with a distributed force per unit of length \(q\). Compute the value of the vertical displacement \(v\) in the middle of the beam, i.e. at \(z = l/2\). Neglect shear deformability.

(Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(l = 1500\, \text{mm}\)
\(a = 30\, \text{mm}\)
\(E_1 = 70000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(E_2 = 210000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)
\(q = 1\, \text{N/mm}\)

Answer Solution:
From compatibility, both materials have the same strain, so the composite section behaves as a single beam.

Step 1: Compute equivalent bending stiffness:
\[EI_{xx} = \frac{a^4}{12}(E_1 + E_2) = \frac{30^4}{12}(70000 + 210000) = 1.89 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N·mm}^2\] Step 2: Using virtual work (unit load at midspan):
Real moment: \(M_x = \frac{ql}{2}z - \frac{1}{2}qz^2\)
Virtual moment: \(M'_x = \frac{1}{2}z\) for \(0 \leq z \leq \frac{l}{2}\)

Step 3: For simply supported beam with uniform load:
\[v = \frac{5ql^4}{384\,EI_{xx}} = \frac{5 \times 1 \times 1500^4}{384 \times 1.89 \times 10^{10}}\] \[v = \frac{5 \times 5.0625 \times 10^{12}}{7.26 \times 10^{12}} = 3.49\, \text{mm}\] Final Answer: \(v \approx 3.49\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 2

The beam structure sketched in the figure is loaded by the force \(F\), applied at the beam extremity. Compute the value of the displacement \(v\) in direction \(y\) at the point of application of the force. Do not neglect shear deformability; rather, do account for shear deformability. (Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(l = 1000\, \text{mm}\)
\(EI_{xx} = 1 \times 10^{12}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(GA = 1 \times 10^7\, \text{N}\)
\(k = 1000\, \text{N/mm}\)
\(F = 100\, \text{N}\)

Answer Solution:
Let \(v\) be the displacement at the free end. Spring reaction: \(R = kv\)

Step 1: Internal forces:
\(T_y = -F + kv\), \(\quad M_x = -Fz + kvz\)

Step 2: Virtual system (unit load at tip):
\(T'_y = -1\), \(\quad M'_x = -z\)

Step 3: Principle of Virtual Work including shear deformation:
\[\delta W'_i = \int_0^l \left(\frac{T_y T'_y}{GA^*} + \frac{M_x M'_x}{EI_{xx}}\right)dz\] \[v = \left(\frac{Fl}{GA^*} + \frac{Fl^3}{3EI_{xx}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{kl}{GA^*} + \frac{kl^3}{3EI_{xx}}\right)^{-1}\] Step 4: Numerical evaluation:
Numerator: \(\frac{100 \times 1000}{10^7} + \frac{100 \times 10^9}{3 \times 10^{12}} = 0.01 + 0.0333 = 0.0433\)
Denominator: \(1 + 0.1 + 0.333 = 1.433\)

\[v = \frac{0.0433}{1.433} = 0.0302\, \text{mm}\] Final Answer: \(v \approx 0.03\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 3

The continuous beam structure sketched in the figure has a prescribed rotation \(\theta_z\), around the \(z\) axis, at point C (a moment is applied there, leaving free the three displacement components and the other two rotation components). Compute the reaction force in the \(y\) direction transmitted by the constraint of point B. Neglect shear deformation. The coordinates of the points are given, with respect to the sketched reference system, in the data. The bending and torsional stiffness of the beams are given with respect to local reference systems, with the \(z\) axis aligned with the beam axis. Be careful with the measurement units. (Unit for result: N)

Data
\(A : (0; 1000; 0)\, \text{mm}\)
\(B : (1000; 1000; 0)\, \text{mm}\)
\(C : (1000; 1000; 1000)\, \text{mm}\)
\(\theta_z = 5^\circ\)
\(EI_{xx} = EI_{yy} = 1 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(GJ = 2 \times 10^{11}\, \text{N mm}^2\)

Answer Solution:
Step 1: Convert prescribed rotation to radians:
\[\theta_z = 5° = \frac{5\pi}{180} = 0.08727\, \text{rad}\] Step 2: Set up equilibrium with unknowns \(M\) (moment at C) and \(F\) (reaction at B):
Member A-B (length \(l = 1000\, \text{mm}\)): Torsion \(M_{z_1} = M_c\)
Member B-C (length \(l = 1000\, \text{mm}\)): Bending \(M_{x_2} = M_c - H_B \cdot z_2\)

Step 3: Apply PVW with two virtual systems:
\[\begin{bmatrix} \frac{l}{GJ} + \frac{l}{EI_{xx}} & \frac{l^2}{2EI_{xx}} \\ \frac{l^2}{2EI_{xx}} & \frac{l^3}{3EI_{xx}} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} M \\ F \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{Bmatrix} -0.08727 \\ 0 \end{Bmatrix}\] Step 4: Solve the system:
From the second equation: \(M = -\frac{3F \cdot l}{2}\)
Substituting into the first equation and solving:

\[M = -2.909 \times 10^6\, \text{N·mm}\] \[F = 4.364 \times 10^3\, \text{N}\] Final Answer: \(F = 4364\, \text{N}\)

Exercise 4

The semi-monocoque beam sketched in the figure, with overall length \(3l\), has four panels, each with thickness \(t\), and four concentrated areas, each with area \(A\). It is loaded on the upper panel, by force per unit of surface \(f\). The load is introduced into the structure by four different ribs, with pitch equal to \(l\) (the first rib is at the clamp, the last one at the beam free extremity). The four panels are continuous all over the structure in the \(z\) direction. The third rib from the free extremity (the one with all the dots near to it) is attached to the outer panels all around its boundary by means of rivets that are put in place with a pitch (a distance one from each other) equal to \(p\). The rivets shank has a radius equal to \(r\). Compute the shear stress in the rivets that connect the rib to the lateral panel. (Unit for result: MPa)

Data
\(l = 1000\, \text{mm}\)
\(a = 700\, \text{mm}\)
\(b = 100\, \text{mm}\)
\(t = 1\, \text{mm}\)
\(A = 500\, \text{mm}^2\)
\(E = 70000\, \text{MPa}\)
\(\nu = 0.3\)
\(f = 0.01\, \text{N/mm}^2\)
\(r = 1.5\, \text{mm}\)
\(p = 25\, \text{mm}\)

Answer Solution:
Key insight: Since the external panels are continuous in \(z\), the rivets transfer only the load introduced by the rib (not the shear flow in the panels).

Step 1: From symmetry, the shear flows in the lateral panels are equal: \(q_1 = q_2\), and \(q_3 = q_4 = 0\)

Step 2: Total force introduced by rib to each lateral panel:
\[T_y = f \cdot a \cdot l = 0.01 \times 700 \times 1000 = 7000\, \text{N}\] Step 3: Shear flux transferred to vertical panel:
\[\hat{q}_1 = \frac{T_y}{2b} = \frac{f \cdot a \cdot l}{2b} = \frac{1}{2} f \cdot l \cdot \frac{a}{b}\] Step 4: Force per rivet:
\[F_{rivet} = \hat{q}_1 \cdot p = \frac{1}{2} f \cdot l \cdot \frac{a}{b} \cdot p\] Step 5: Shear stress in rivet:
\[\tau = \frac{F_{rivet}}{\pi r^2} = \frac{1}{2} f \cdot l \cdot \frac{a}{b} \cdot p \cdot \frac{1}{\pi r^2}\] \[\tau = \frac{0.5 \times 0.01 \times 1000 \times 7 \times 25}{\pi \times 2.25} = \frac{875}{7.069} = 123.8\, \text{MPa}\] Final Answer: \(\tau \approx 124\, \text{MPa}\)

Exercise 5

Consider the slender beam structure sketched in the figure, loaded in the middle by the concentrated moment \(M_x\). Estimate the transverse displacement \(v\) at the right extremity by assuming a suitable trigonometric approximation of the transverse displacement truncated to the first non null term. Neglect shear deformability.

(Unit for result: mm)

Data
\(l = 3000\, \text{mm}\)
\(EI_{xx} = 1 \times 10^{13}\, \text{N mm}^2\)
\(M_x = 1 \times 10^5\, \text{N mm}\)

Answer Solution:
Step 1: Displacement approximation (satisfies BCs: \(v(0)=0\), \(v'(0)=0\), \(v'(l)=0\)):
\[v = A\left(1 - \cos\left(\frac{\pi z}{l}\right)\right)\] Step 2: Derivatives:
\[v' = A\frac{\pi}{l}\sin\left(\frac{\pi z}{l}\right), \quad v'' = A\frac{\pi^2}{l^2}\cos\left(\frac{\pi z}{l}\right)\] Step 3: Apply PVW (moment acts at \(z = l/2\)):
\[\delta W_e = -\delta v'\left(\frac{l}{2}\right) \cdot M_x, \quad \delta W_i = \int_0^l \delta v'' \cdot EI \cdot v''\, dz\] Step 4: Solve for coefficient A:
\[\phi'\left(\frac{l}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{l}\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{l}\] \[\int_0^l (\phi'')^2\, dz = \frac{\pi^4}{l^4} \cdot \frac{l}{2} = \frac{\pi^4}{2l^3}\] \[A = -\frac{M_x \cdot \frac{\pi}{l}}{EI_{xx} \cdot \frac{\pi^4}{2l^3}} = -\frac{2M_x l^2}{\pi^3 EI_{xx}}\] Step 5: Displacement at right end:
\[v(l) = A(1 - \cos(\pi)) = 2A = -\frac{4M_x l^2}{\pi^3 EI_{xx}}\] \[v(l) = -\frac{4 \times 10^5 \times (3000)^2}{\pi^3 \times 10^{13}} = -\frac{3.6 \times 10^{12}}{3.1 \times 10^{14}} = -0.0116\, \text{mm}\] Final Answer: \(v \approx -0.012\, \text{mm}\)

Exercise 6

The two node beam finite element in the figure interpolates linearly, and independently, the transverse displacements and the rotations. Compute the contribution stemming from the bending stiffness \(EI_{xx}\) to the diagonal term of the element stiffness matrix related to the rotation around axis \(y\) of node 1 (\(K_{\theta_y\theta_y}\) of node 1).

(Unit for result: N mm)

Data
\(l = 5\, \text{mm}\)
\(EI_{xx} = 1 \times 10^{10}\, \text{N mm}^2\)

Answer Solution:
Step 1: Linear interpolation for rotation:
Shape function: \(N_1 = \frac{\xi}{l}\)
Rotation field: \(\theta_y(\xi) = N_1 \cdot \theta_{y_1} = \frac{\xi}{l}\theta_{y_1}\)

Step 2: Curvature (derivative of rotation):
\[\frac{\partial \theta_y}{\partial \xi} = \frac{\theta_{y_1}}{l}\] Step 3: Internal virtual work contribution:
\[\delta W_i = \int_0^l \delta\theta_{y,\xi} \cdot EI_{xx} \cdot \theta_{y,\xi}\, d\xi\] Step 4: Extract stiffness coefficient:
\[K_{\theta_{y_1}\theta_{y_1}} = \int_0^l \frac{1}{l} \cdot EI_{xx} \cdot \frac{1}{l}\, d\xi = \frac{EI_{xx}}{l^2} \cdot l = \frac{EI_{xx}}{l}\] \[K_{\theta_y\theta_y} = \frac{1 \times 10^{10}}{5} = 2 \times 10^9\, \text{N·mm}\] Final Answer: \(K_{\theta_y\theta_y} = 2 \times 10^9\, \text{N·mm}\)

True/False Questions (Put a T (true) or F (false) at the end of the sentence)

  1. Shear locking affects beam finite elements based on Hermitian shape functions. F (False) — Hermitian shape functions do NOT suffer from shear locking; shear locking affects lower-order elements with reduced integration.
  2. The natural vibration frequency of a tensioned beam is lower than that of an unloaded beam. F (False) — Tensioned beams have HIGHER natural frequencies due to the geometric stiffness effect from the axial tension.
  3. The Neumann boundary condition reads \(\sigma \cdot n = f\), where \(f\) is the applied load per unit of volume. F (False) — In the Neumann boundary condition, \(f\) represents the applied load per unit of AREA (surface traction), not per unit of volume.

Multiple Choice questions (Circle the correct answer)

  1. Knowing the axial stiffness \(EA\), the two shear stiffness \(GA^*_x\) and \(GA^*_y\), the torsional stiffness \(GJ\) and the two bending stiffness \(EI_{xx}\) and \(EI_{yy}\)

    1. is enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section
    2. is not enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section, because one needs to know the position of the shear center wrt to the cross section
    3. is not enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section, because one needs to know the position of the beam principal axis wrt to the cross section
    4. is not enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section, because one needs to know the position and orientation of the beam principal axis wrt to the cross section
    5. is not enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section, because one needs to know the position of both the beam principal axis and shear center wrt to the cross section
    6. is not enough to fully characterize the behavior of a beam cross section, because one needs to know the position of both the beam principal axis and shear center wrt to the cross section, together with the orientation of the principal axes
    7. none of the above

    Answer: (f) — One needs the position of the centroid (for principal axes), the orientation of the principal axes, AND the position of the shear center to fully characterize beam behavior.

  2. The resultant over the cross section of a beam of the axial normal stress, \(N = \int_A \sigma_{zz} dA\) has the dimension of a force. It is:

    1. work conjugated with the axial displacement
    2. work conjugated with the derivative of the axial displacement
    3. work conjugate with the derivative of the axial rotation
    4. work conjugated with the shear deformation
    5. none of the above

    Answer: (b) — The axial force \(N\) is work conjugate with the axial strain \(\varepsilon_{zz} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\), which is the derivative of the axial displacement.

  3. A riveted connection between two panels loaded in-plane cannot fail due to:

    1. shear stress in the panels
    2. shear stress in the rivet
    3. axial stress in the rivet
    4. bearing
    5. axial stress in the panels
    6. none of the above

    Answer: (c) — In-plane loading transfers forces through shear in the rivets, not axial stress. Rivets experience shear, not tension/compression along their axis, for in-plane panel loading.
