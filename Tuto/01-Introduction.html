Section 1: Structures & Stress Tensor

This section introduces the fundamental concepts of structural analysis and the stress tensor, focusing on equilibrium conditions for statically determined structures.

Beam Configuration

  • Fixed support at left end (prevents translation and rotation)
  • Pin/hinge support at middle (prevents vertical translation, allows rotation)
  • Point load \(Q\) at left end (downward)
  • Point load \(Q\) at right end (downward)

Equilibrium Conditions

Force Equilibrium

The sum of all forces must equal zero:

\[\sum_{i=1}^{3} \underline{F}_i = \underline{0}\]

Moment Equilibrium

The sum of all moments about point O must equal zero:

\[\sum_{i=1}^{3} (\underline{P}_i - O) \times \underline{F}_i = \underline{0}\]
Note: Underlined symbols indicate vector quantities. These equilibrium equations form the basis for analyzing STATICALLY DETERMINED structures.

Reaction Force Analysis

For the beam with loads \(F\) at both ends and a pin support at the middle:

  • Downward force \(F\) at left
  • Downward force \(F\) at right
  • Reaction at middle support: \(X = 2F\) (upward)

Section 2: Structural Requirements

Three fundamental requirements govern structural design: Equilibrium, Stiffness, and Strength. This section examines each requirement through various structural configurations.

Cantilever Beam Analysis

For a cantilever beam with force \(F\) at the free end:

  • Applied force: \(F\) (downward at free end)
  • Reaction force: \(F\) (upward at fixed support)
  • Internal moment varies along the beam length

Analysis of Requirements

Moment Calculation

The internal moment at distance \(x\) from the free end:

\[M = F \cdot x\]

At the support, the reaction moment equals \(F \cdot x\) where \(x\) is the beam length.

1. Equilibrium Requirement

The equilibrium diagram shows a curved structural element subjected to:

  • Distributed upward forces along top surfaces
  • Distributed downward forces along bottom surfaces
  • Concentrated force \(Q\) at the center (downward)

2. Stiffness Requirement

The stiffness requirement ensures that structural deformations remain within acceptable limits. The deflected shape must not exceed serviceability criteria.

3. Strength Requirement

The strength requirement ensures that internal stresses do not exceed material capacity. This involves analyzing the structural member under applied loads to verify adequate strength.

Simply Supported Beam Example

For a beam with pin support at center and forces \(F\) at both ends:

  • Downward force \(F\) on left side
  • Downward force \(F\) on right side
  • Reaction force: \(2F\) upward at center support

The deflected shape shows sagging on both sides of the support.

Section 3: Beam Support Configurations

This section compares different beam support configurations and develops the equilibrium equations for free body diagram analysis.

Support Types

  • Fixed Support: Prevents translation and rotation (hatched wall symbol)
  • Pin/Hinge Support: Prevents translation, allows rotation (triangular symbol)
  • Roller/Cart Support: Prevents vertical translation only, allows horizontal movement and rotation

Configuration Details:

  • Left Configuration: Fixed support at left end, pin support at interior, load \(F\) between supports
  • Right Configuration: Pin support at left end, pin support at interior, load \(F\) at right free end

Free Body Diagram Analysis

Reaction Forces

Define the following reaction forces:

  • \(M\) = Moment reaction at fixed support
  • \(H\) = Vertical reaction at left support
  • \(R\) = Vertical reaction at interior support
  • \(F\) = Applied load
  • \(d_1\) = Distance from left support to interior support
  • \(\ell\) = Distance from left support to applied load

Vertical Force Equilibrium

Sum of vertical forces equals zero:

\[H + R - F = 0\]

Moment Equilibrium

Sum of moments about the left support equals zero:

\[M + R \cdot d_1 - F \cdot \ell = 0\]
Equilibrium Equations: \[\boxed{H + R - F = 0}\] \[\boxed{M + R \cdot d_1 - F \cdot \ell = 0}\]

Section 4: Truss and Beam Structural Systems

This section compares two fundamental structural systems for carrying vertical loads: truss structures and continuous beam structures.

Structural System Comparison

Triangular Truss Structure (Left)

  • Apex at top center with pin joint
  • Vertical downward point load \(F\) applied at apex
  • Two inclined members connecting apex to base
  • Pin supports at both base corners
  • Forms an isosceles triangle configuration
  • Load Transfer: Members carry axial loads only (tension or compression)

Three-Span Continuous Beam (Right)

  • Horizontal beam with three pin supports
  • Left support: pin connection
  • Middle support: pin connection
  • Right support: pin connection
  • Vertical downward point load \(F\) applied at middle support location
  • Load Transfer: Members carry bending moments and shear forces
Key Difference:
Trusses transfer loads through axial forces in members, while beams transfer loads through bending moments and shear forces.
